# Week 3: How Brains Decide

The Frontal Lobe and Prefrontal Cortex — where reasoning, planning, and executive function live. Students study how biological brains weigh options, inhibit impulses, and plan for the future. Then they watch Are-Self's frontal_lobe app run a reasoning session and its prefrontal_cortex app organize work into hierarchies. The gap this week: biological decision-making is soaked in emotion. Computational decision-making isn't. What gets lost?

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Reasoning, planning, and executive function — biological and computational

**Primary Variable:** Responsibility — some decisions carry weight. Know which ones.

<!-- Definition of Ready — perspective -->

**Why This Week Matters:**
Decision-making is where AI hype is loudest and understanding is thinnest. Students who can articulate how biological reasoning integrates emotion, social context, and embodied experience — and that software reasoning doesn't — are equipped to evaluate every AI claim they'll encounter for the rest of their lives.

<!-- Definition of Ready — assertions -->

**Learning Goals:**
- Describe the biological roles of the frontal lobe and prefrontal cortex in reasoning, planning, and impulse control
- Explain how Are-Self's frontal_lobe and prefrontal_cortex apps divide reasoning from planning
- Compare biological decision-making (emotion-integrated) to computational decision-making (logic-only)
- Analyze a real Are-Self reasoning session and identify where a human would have decided differently
- Articulate the role of emotion in human decision-making using evidence from neuroscience

<!-- Definition of Ready — outside -->

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This week does NOT cover the full neuroscience of emotion (amygdala, limbic system) — only emotion's role in decision-making
- Students are NOT expected to evaluate AI reasoning for correctness — only to compare the *process* to human reasoning

## Day 1: The Decision-Making Brain

### Objective

Students will describe the roles of the frontal lobe and prefrontal cortex in human decision-making, including the critical role of emotion.

### Materials Needed

- Phineas Gage case study (printed summary or video)
- [Week 3 Worksheet A: Decision Anatomy](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-a.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Weeks 1–2

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Present a moral dilemma appropriate for high schoolers. Give 60 seconds to decide. Then ask: **"What happened in your brain just now? Did you think about it logically, or did you feel something first?"**

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Frontal Lobe and PFC (15 minutes)

Teach the biological functions:
- **Frontal Lobe** — motor control, reasoning, speech production (Broca's area), voluntary action
- **Prefrontal Cortex** — the front of the frontal lobe. Planning, personality expression, moderating social behavior, impulse control, weighing consequences

Key insight: the PFC doesn't mature until the mid-20s. Ask students what that means for their own decision-making right now.

#### Part 2: Phineas Gage (10 minutes)

The railroad worker who survived a tamping iron through his prefrontal cortex — and became a different person. His intelligence was intact. His decision-making was destroyed. Why?

Because decision-making isn't just logic. It's logic integrated with social awareness, emotional regulation, and consequence-prediction. Damage the PFC, and the logic works but the integration breaks.

#### Part 3: Worksheet and Discussion (10 minutes)

Students complete [Worksheet A](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-a.md) and discuss: "If Phineas Gage's logic was intact, what was missing? Can software have that same problem?"

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Tomorrow we open the software version. Are-Self splits this into two apps — frontal_lobe for reasoning and prefrontal_cortex for planning. Phineas Gage had his PFC damaged but his frontal lobe was mostly fine. Is Are-Self accidentally doing the same split?"

### Exit Ticket

"The PFC's job isn't just to think — it's to ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Can students distinguish frontal lobe (execution) from PFC (planning/integration)?
- **Engagement:** Does Phineas Gage generate real discussion?
- **Habit practice:** Are students connecting this to their own decision-making? (Responsibility)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Research the Iowa Gambling Task (Damasio) — evidence that patients with PFC damage make logically sound but personally destructive decisions

**Struggling Learners:**
- Focus on the Gage story as the primary anchor — "logic without consequences"

## Day 2: The Software Reasoning Loop

### Objective

Students will observe Are-Self's frontal_lobe reasoning session and prefrontal_cortex planning hierarchy, then compare the computational process to biological decision-making.

### Materials Needed

- Computer running Are-Self, projected
- A complex prompt that requires multi-step reasoning
- [Week 3 Worksheet B: Reasoning Comparison](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-b.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Are-Self running with a well-configured Identity

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Yesterday we learned that human reasoning integrates emotion, social context, and consequence-prediction. Today we watch software reason. Your job: figure out what's missing."

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Live Reasoning Session (15 minutes)

Give Are-Self a multi-step problem. As it reasons, narrate:
- "The frontal_lobe app is running a reasoning loop — see the iterations?"
- "It's calling tools — that's like your frontal lobe coordinating motor actions"
- "Now the prefrontal_cortex has organized this into a hierarchy — epic, story, task"
- "Notice what's NOT happening: no emotional check, no social awareness, no 'is this the right thing to do?'"

#### Part 2: Side-by-Side Analysis (15 minutes)

Students complete [Worksheet B](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-b.md): for the same problem, describe how a human would reason through it (with emotions, social considerations, gut feelings) vs. how the software did it. Identify at least three points of divergence.

#### Part 3: Class Discussion (5 minutes)

"Is this software doing what Phineas Gage's brain did after the accident — logic without integration? Or is it something else entirely?"

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"The frontal lobe app reasons. The PFC app plans. Neither one feels anything. Is that a feature or a bug?"

### Exit Ticket

"The biggest thing missing from Are-Self's reasoning compared to mine is ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Can students identify specific points where human and software reasoning diverge?
- **Engagement:** Is the Phineas Gage parallel generating insight?
- **Habit practice:** Are they taking responsibility for understanding the difference? (Responsibility)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Write a one-paragraph argument: "Are-Self is a Phineas Gage brain — agree or disagree?"

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a pre-filled column for the software side; students fill in the human side

## Day 3: Emotion and Decision-Making

### Objective

Students will examine the neuroscience evidence that emotion is essential (not optional) for good decision-making, and evaluate what that means for AI systems that lack it.

### Materials Needed

- Somatic marker hypothesis summary (Damasio, simplified)
- [Week 3 Worksheet C: The Role of Emotion](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-c.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Days 1–2

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Raise your hand if you've ever made a decision because of a gut feeling — and it turned out to be right." Pause. "Now raise your hand if you've ever made a purely logical decision — and it turned out to be wrong."

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Somatic Marker Hypothesis (15 minutes)

Introduce Damasio's work (simplified): when you face a decision, your body sends signals (gut feelings, tension, excitement) that narrow your options before conscious reasoning even begins. Patients with specific brain damage can reason logically but make terrible real-world decisions because they've lost these body signals.

This isn't "emotions are irrational." It's "emotions are data."

#### Part 2: Apply to Are-Self (10 minutes)

Open question: "Are-Self has no body. It has no gut feelings, no tension, no excitement. According to what we just learned, what kind of decisions would it be worst at?"

Let students work through this. Guide them toward: decisions involving social consequences, moral weight, personal risk, ambiguity, and time pressure.

#### Part 3: Worksheet (10 minutes)

Students complete [Worksheet C](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-c.md): categorize a list of decisions as "software could handle this" vs. "software would struggle" and explain why for each.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Emotion isn't the enemy of good thinking. It's part of the machinery. Anyone building AI without understanding this is building Phineas Gage on purpose."

### Exit Ticket

"Emotion helps decision-making by ___. A system without emotion would struggle with ___ because ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Can students explain why emotion is evidence, not noise?
- **Engagement:** Are they rethinking their assumptions about "rational" AI?
- **Habit practice:** Responsibility — are they thinking about what it means to deploy emotion-free AI in the real world?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Read a simplified excerpt of Damasio's "Descartes' Error" and write a response

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide the decision list pre-categorized; students explain the reasoning for each

## Day 4: Designing a Decision Experiment

### Objective

Students will design an experiment that tests whether Are-Self's reasoning process misses something a human would catch — specifically something related to emotional or social context.

### Materials Needed

- [Week 3 Worksheet D: Decision Experiment Design](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-d.md)
- Access to Are-Self for planning

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Days 1–3

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"You've learned that human decision-making integrates emotion and social context. Are-Self's doesn't. Today you design an experiment to prove it — or prove yourselves wrong."

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Brainstorm (10 minutes)

Class brainstorm: what prompts would reveal the absence of emotional reasoning?
- Moral dilemmas
- Decisions with social consequences
- Situations where the "logical" answer feels wrong
- Ambiguous scenarios where gut feeling matters

#### Part 2: Experiment Design (20 minutes)

Pairs design their experiment using [Worksheet D](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-d.md). They must specify:
- The prompt they'll give Are-Self
- What a human would likely consider (emotional/social factors)
- What they predict Are-Self will miss
- How they'll evaluate the response

#### Part 3: Peer Review (5 minutes)

Swap and critique. Is the experiment actually testing emotional reasoning, or just testing knowledge?

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Tomorrow you run it. Be prepared to be surprised — either direction."

### Exit Ticket

"My experiment tests whether Are-Self can ___. I predict it [can / cannot] account for ___ because ___."

## Day 5: Run, Analyze, Present

### Objective

Students will execute their decision experiment, analyze results, and present findings.

### Materials Needed

- Computer access
- [Week 3 Worksheet E: Results and Analysis](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-e.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Experiment designs completed and reviewed

### Warm-Up (2 minutes)

"Run your experiment. Record everything. You present in 25 minutes."

### Main Activity (40 minutes)

#### Part 1: Run Experiments (15 minutes)

Students execute their experiments and record results on [Worksheet E](./worksheets/week-3-worksheet-e.md).

#### Part 2: Lightning Presentations (20 minutes)

2 minutes per pair: prompt, prediction, result, insight.

#### Part 3: Hypothesis Update (5 minutes)

"Update your Week 1 hypothesis. Has this week changed what you think?"

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Two weeks in. You've tested memory and reasoning. The pattern that's emerging is: software does the mechanics well. What it doesn't do is the integration — the part where memory, emotion, context, and social awareness all flow into a single decision. That integration is what your brain does every second of every day without you noticing."

### Common Questions & Troubleshooting

**Q: Students are saying "AI can't think" too confidently.**
A: Push them: "What would you need to see to change your mind?" The goal isn't to reach a conclusion — it's to hold the question open with evidence.

**Q: The Phineas Gage story is too graphic for some students.**
A: Focus on the behavioral changes, not the injury details. The point is what changed in his decision-making, not the mechanism of injury.

## Summary

Week 3 confronts the deepest gap in the brain-software metaphor: emotion. Students learn that biological decision-making isn't logic plus optional feelings — it's logic integrated with somatic markers, social context, and embodied experience. Software reasoning strips all of that out. Whether that's acceptable depends on what decisions you're asking it to make. That's a question about Responsibility.
