# Rubrics: The Human Brain and the Are-Self

## How to Use These Rubrics

Share the relevant rubric with students at the start of each week. Use them formatively — comment on specific criteria as students work, not just at the end. The goal is growth, not perfection. A student who starts at "Beginning" and ends at "Approaching" has learned more than one who stays at "Exceeds."

### Grading Philosophy

**The goal is not perfection — it's growth.** Track growth and celebrate progress in thinking, reasoning, and collaboration. These rubrics measure how students think, not whether they're "right" — because the central question of this course doesn't have a right answer.

## Week 6: Capstone — Where the Metaphor Breaks

| Criteria | 4 — Exceeds | 3 — Meets | 2 — Approaching | 1 — Beginning |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Claim clarity** | Claim is precise, specific, and stated in one sentence; audience immediately understands the position | Claim is clear and specific | Claim is present but vague or too broad | No clear claim |
| **Evidence quality** | Three or more pieces of evidence from different weeks; evidence directly supports the claim | At least three pieces of evidence; mostly relevant | Some evidence present but thin or loosely connected | Little or no evidence |

| **Counterargument** | Anticipates the strongest objection; responds to it substantively | Identifies a counterargument and responds | Counterargument is present but weak or response is missing | No counterargument |

| **Growth across the course** | Hypothesis evolved significantly with clear evidence trail; reflection shows deep learning | Hypothesis changed based on evidence; reflection is present | Some evolution in thinking; reflection is surface-level | No evidence of growth from Week 1 hypothesis |

## AI Collaboration (Cross-Cutting)

Use this rubric throughout the course to assess how students work with Are-Self as a research subject.

| Criteria | 4 — Exceeds | 3 — Meets | 2 — Approaching | 1 — Beginning |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Asks thoughtful questions** | Questions are specific, open-ended, and build on previous responses. Asks "why" and "how." | Questions are clear and prompt meaningful AI responses. | Questions are present but vague or fact-based. | Questions are missing, unclear, or closed-ended. |
| **Evaluates AI responses critically** | Consistently checks AI output against personal knowledge. Does not accept suggestions blindly. | Evaluates most AI responses before using them. | Evaluates inconsistently. May accept some suggestions without consideration. | Accepts AI responses without question. |
| **Iterates and refines** | Uses AI feedback to revise work multiple times. Builds on each interaction. | Engages in feedback loops. Revises based on suggestions. | Iterates occasionally or with prompting. | Does not iterate. Treats first response as final. |
| **Documents AI's contribution honestly** | Clearly identifies what AI helped with vs. what the student did independently. | Notes where AI was used. Acknowledges its role appropriately. | Mentions AI use but is vague about specifics. | Does not document AI use or misrepresents it. |
| **Uses AI as a partner, not a replacement** | AI extends thinking. Student does the core intellectual work; AI amplifies it. | Uses AI to support thinking and explore ideas. Does own work. | Sometimes over-relies on AI, sometimes under-uses it. | Treats AI as a replacement for thinking. |

## Student Self-Assessment

| Criteria | Still Learning | Getting There | Got It |
|----------|---------------|---------------|--------|
| **I can explain where the brain-software metaphor works** | I'm not sure which parallels are strong | I can name a few parallels but can't explain why they work | I can explain multiple parallels and why they hold |
| **I can explain where the metaphor breaks** | I know it breaks somewhere but can't say where | I can name a gap but struggle to explain why it matters | I can clearly state a gap and defend it with evidence |
| **I use evidence from experiments** | I have opinions but don't connect them to data | I sometimes use data but could do it more consistently | I build my arguments from evidence I've collected |
| **I ask good questions about AI** | I accept what AI says without questioning it | I sometimes question AI responses | I consistently evaluate AI output and push back when needed |
| **I'm growing as a thinker** | I'm not sure what's changed | I know I've learned things but can't articulate the growth | I can describe how my thinking has changed and point to evidence |

## Tips for Using These Rubrics

1. **Share them with students at the start of each week.** They should know what they're working toward.
2. **Use formatively.** Comment on specific criteria as students work — don't wait until the end.
3. **Conference together.** Ask: "What does a 3 look like in your work? What would it take to reach a 4?"
4. **Track growth, not just grades.** Moving from 1 to 2 is real progress. A student who grows from Beginning to Approaching has succeeded.
5. **The capstone rubric is the summative assessment.** Weeks 1–5 rubrics are formative checkpoints.
