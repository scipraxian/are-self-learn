# Week 1: The Architecture

Students meet the human brain and its software mirror side by side. By the end of the week, they'll be able to name the major brain regions, describe what each one does in biology, and identify its Are-Self counterpart. The week ends with students building their first hypothesis: where does the metaphor already feel wrong?

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Orientation — the human brain and its software mirror

**Primary Variable:** Humility — "I don't know yet" is the beginning of every good thing.

<!-- Definition of Ready — perspective -->

**Why This Week Matters:**
Students can't evaluate an analogy until they know both sides of it. This week builds the foundation — real neuroanatomy on one side, Are-Self architecture on the other — so every subsequent week can ask "how are these the same, and where do they diverge?"

<!-- Definition of Ready — assertions -->

**Learning Goals:**
- Identify and locate the nine brain regions covered in this course
- Describe the primary biological function of each region in their own words
- Map each biological region to its Are-Self Django app counterpart
- Formulate an initial hypothesis about where the brain-software metaphor might fail

<!-- Definition of Ready — outside -->

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This week does NOT cover the detailed internal mechanisms of any single region — that comes in Weeks 2–5
- Students are NOT expected to understand the software architecture in depth yet
- No experiments this week — observation and mapping only

## Day 1: What Does a Brain Do?

### Objective

Students will articulate what they already know (and don't know) about the human brain, and encounter the idea that someone built software to look like one.

### Materials Needed

- Whiteboard or shared doc for class brainstorm
- Printed or projected brain diagram (labeled, lateral and medial views)
- [Week 1 Worksheet A: What I Think I Know](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-a.md)

<!-- Definition of Ready — dependencies -->

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed at least one semester of introductory biology
- No Are-Self setup required for Day 1

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Write on the board: **"What does your brain do? List as many things as you can in 2 minutes."**

Students write individually, then share. Collect answers on the board. You'll get the obvious ones (think, remember, feel) and some surprising ones (breathe, balance, dream). Leave the list visible all week.

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Real Brain (15 minutes)

Walk through the brain diagram. For each of the nine regions in this course, give the name, point to the location, and offer a one-sentence description of what it does. Keep it conversational — this is a first pass, not a lecture. Students follow along on their worksheets.

The nine regions and their one-liners:

1. **Hippocampus** — Makes new memories and decides what's worth keeping
2. **Frontal Lobe** — Where you reason, decide, and control voluntary movement
3. **Prefrontal Cortex** — The planner — executive function, impulse control, long-term thinking
4. **Hypothalamus** — Tiny but critical — regulates temperature, hunger, hormones, survival
5. **Temporal Lobe** — Language comprehension, auditory processing, and some memory
6. **Central Nervous System** — Brain and spinal cord together — the command center
7. **Peripheral Nervous System** — Every nerve outside the CNS — sensation and motor control
8. **Thalamus** — The relay station — routes sensory input to the right place
9. **Synaptic Cleft** — The gap between neurons where chemical messages cross

Ask after each one: "What surprised you? What did you already know?"

#### Part 2: The Software Brain (15 minutes)

Now show the Are-Self brain region map (projected from the course index or the NanoBanana diagram). For each region, read the "What the Software Does" column alongside the biology.

Don't explain the software deeply. Just let the parallels sit:
- "The Hippocampus stores memories. The Are-Self hippocampus app stores memories too — as vectors instead of neurons."
- "The Hypothalamus keeps your body alive. The Are-Self hypothalamus app keeps the AI affordable."

Students note the parallels and differences on their worksheets.

#### Part 3: Worksheet (5 minutes)

Students complete [Worksheet A](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-a.md) — a two-column table where they write, in their own words, what each region does biologically and what its software counterpart does.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Which region surprised you the most when you saw its software version? Why?"**

Then: "This week we're going to go deeper into each of these. By Friday, I'm going to ask you to tell me where the metaphor is already wrong — where the software version does something the biology doesn't, or misses something the biology has. Start thinking about it."

<!-- Definition of Done -->

### Exit Ticket

On a sticky note: "The brain region that seems hardest to turn into software is ___ because ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Can students name at least 5 of 9 regions and give a basic function?
- **Engagement:** Are they asking questions about the parallels?
- **Habit practice:** Are they comfortable saying "I'm not sure about this one"? (Humility)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Research a 10th brain region not in the Are-Self architecture (amygdala, cerebellum, basal ganglia) and hypothesize why it was left out

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a pre-filled version of Worksheet A with the biology column completed; they fill in only the software column
- Pair with a partner for the mapping activity

## Day 2: Mapping the Territory

### Objective

Students will create a detailed visual map comparing biological brain anatomy to the Are-Self architecture, identifying structural parallels and differences.

### Materials Needed

- Large paper or poster board (one per pair)
- Colored pencils or markers
- Printed brain diagrams (lateral and medial views, unlabeled)
- Access to the Are-Self documentation site (projected or on devices)
- [Week 1 Worksheet B: The Parallel Map](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-b.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Day 1

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Yesterday someone said [reference a student's exit ticket]. Who agrees? Who disagrees? Why?"**

Use this to surface the early hypotheses. Don't resolve anything — just show that disagreement is welcome.

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Build the Map (25 minutes)

In pairs, students create a side-by-side visual map. Left side: the biological brain, labeled and color-coded by region. Right side: the Are-Self architecture, with each Django app in the corresponding position. Between them: lines connecting each pair, with a short note about what's parallel and what's different.

Circulate. Ask questions that push thinking:
- "Why do you think they put the Hypothalamus in charge of cost? What does that have in common with temperature regulation?"
- "The Thalamus routes sensory input. The software thalamus routes chat messages. Is that the same job?"

#### Part 2: Gallery Walk (10 minutes)

Post the maps. Students walk, observe, and leave one sticky note on a map that isn't theirs with something they noticed or a question.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Did anyone see something on another pair's map that made them think differently?"**

<!-- Definition of Done -->

### Exit Ticket

Complete the sentence: "The strongest parallel between the real brain and Are-Self is ___ because ___. The weakest is ___ because ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Are the maps anatomically and architecturally accurate?
- **Engagement:** Do the connecting notes show real thinking, not just "they're the same"?
- **Habit practice:** Are students noticing gaps and asking why? (Inquiry)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Add the four supplementary systems (Parietal Lobe, Occipital Lobe, Neuroplasticity, Synaptic Cleft) to their map

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a partially completed map template with regions pre-positioned

## Day 3: The Software Brain, Live

### Objective

Students will observe Are-Self reasoning in real time, identify which brain regions activate during a reasoning session, and record their observations.

### Materials Needed

- Computer running Are-Self, projected
- A pre-built Identity with a clear personality and tools
- [Week 1 Worksheet C: Reasoning Observation Log](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-c.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Are-Self is installed, running, and tested
- Students have completed Days 1–2

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Ask: **"If I asked your brain to plan a weekend trip with $50, which brain regions would activate? In what order?"**

Let them speculate. Then: "Let's watch software do the same thing."

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Live Reasoning Session (15 minutes)

Run a reasoning session with a relatable prompt. Give the AI a task and narrate what you see:
- "The Frontal Lobe just started a reasoning loop — see the tool calls?"
- "It's checking the Hippocampus — looking for memories that might help."
- "The Hypothalamus picked a smaller model for this step. Why do you think it did that?"

Students fill in their observation logs as they watch.

#### Part 2: Observation Discussion (10 minutes)

In small groups, students compare observation logs. What did they notice? What surprised them? What was missing that a real brain would do?

#### Part 3: Class Discussion (10 minutes)

Bring it back to the whole class. Focus on one question: **"What did a real brain do while watching this that the software brain never did?"**

Answers might include: felt bored, got distracted, wondered about lunch, had an emotional reaction to the answer. This is the first crack in the metaphor — and it's the thread that runs through the rest of the course.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"You just watched code think. Or at least, do something that looks like thinking. By the end of this course, you'll have a much sharper answer to whether that word is right."

### Exit Ticket

"One thing the software brain did that surprised me: ___. One thing my brain did while watching that the software never could: ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Can students identify which regions activated and why?
- **Engagement:** Are the observation logs detailed, or surface-level?
- **Habit practice:** Are students sitting with uncertainty about what "thinking" means? (Humility)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Run a second prompt and predict which regions will activate before the session starts

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a simplified observation log with checkboxes for "which regions did you see?"

## Day 4: Where the Biology Gets Real

### Objective

Students will study one brain region in depth (biological side only), prepare a short presentation, and teach their classmates what that region actually does in a living brain.

### Materials Needed

- Biology textbook or curated online resources (per region)
- [Week 1 Worksheet D: Deep Dive Research Notes](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-d.md)
- Presentation materials (whiteboard, poster, or slides — student choice)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Days 1–3

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Today, you become the expert. Each pair picks one brain region. You're going to learn what it really does — not the one-liner from Day 1, but the real biology. Tomorrow, you'll teach it to the class."

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Region Selection and Research (25 minutes)

Pairs choose a region (first come, first served — ensure all nine are covered). Using textbooks and approved resources, they research:
- Detailed biological function
- Physical location and size
- What happens when it's damaged (case studies if available)
- How it connects to other regions
- One thing about it that surprised them

They record findings on [Worksheet D](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-d.md).

#### Part 2: Prepare the Teach-Back (10 minutes)

Each pair prepares a 3-minute presentation. They must explain the biology clearly enough that a classmate who didn't research that region can understand it.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

Quick check: "Who found something about their region that changed how they think about the software version?" No presentations yet — just teases.

### Exit Ticket

"The most important thing about [my region] that the software version probably can't capture is ___."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Are the research notes accurate and specific?
- **Engagement:** Did they go beyond the textbook basics?
- **Habit practice:** Are they connecting biology to the software metaphor unprompted? (Inquiry)

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Research a clinical case study involving their region (e.g., HM for hippocampus, Phineas Gage for frontal lobe)

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a structured research guide with specific questions to answer for each region

## Day 5: Teach-Back and First Hypothesis

### Objective

Students will teach their classmates about their brain region, then collaboratively form their first hypothesis about where the brain-software metaphor breaks down.

### Materials Needed

- Student presentations from Day 4
- Whiteboard for hypothesis collection
- [Week 1 Worksheet E: My First Hypothesis](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-e.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Days 1–4 and prepared presentations

### Warm-Up (3 minutes)

"Today two things happen: you teach, and you think. By the end of class, everyone will have a hypothesis — a testable idea about where the brain-software metaphor fails. That hypothesis will guide the rest of this course."

### Main Activity (37 minutes)

#### Part 1: Teach-Backs (25 minutes)

Each pair presents their region (3 minutes each, strict time). Audience members take notes — they need this information for their hypothesis.

After each presentation, one question from the audience. Keep it moving.

#### Part 2: Hypothesis Formation (12 minutes)

Individually, students write their first hypothesis on [Worksheet E](./worksheets/week-1-worksheet-e.md):

"I hypothesize that the Are-Self [region] cannot [specific biological function] because [reasoning]. I could test this by [proposed method]."

Collect the worksheets. These become the through-line for the course.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

Read 3–4 hypotheses aloud (anonymized if preferred). Ask: "Do you agree? Disagree? Want to test it? That's what the next five weeks are for."

### Common Questions & Troubleshooting

**Q: My students think the AI "thinks" and I'm not sure how to handle that.**
A: Don't resolve it for them. This is the central question of the course. Ask them: "What would you need to see to be convinced? What would change your mind?" That's Humility and Inquiry working together.

**Q: Students are more interested in the software than the biology.**
A: Great — use it. The software is the hook. Every software feature has a biological counterpart, and the gaps are where the biology gets interesting. Let the software pull them into the anatomy.

**Q: I don't know much about Are-Self myself.**
A: You don't need to. The course is designed so that you and the students learn the software together. Your expertise is the biology. Theirs is the curiosity.

## Summary

Week 1 builds the foundation. Students meet the nine brain regions twice — once in biology, once in software — and start to notice that the mirror isn't perfect. The teach-back forces deep engagement with at least one region, and the hypothesis formation gives every student a personal stake in the five weeks that follow. The question "is this thinking?" is planted but deliberately left unresolved.
