# Week 6: Where the Metaphor Breaks

The capstone. Students choose the gap they've been circling all course — the place where the brain-software metaphor fails most significantly — and build a case for it. They draw on five weeks of experiments, observations, and evidence. They present to the class, defend their hypothesis under questioning, and leave with a sharper understanding of both neuroscience and AI than they had walking in.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Capstone — synthesis, argumentation, and the limits of analogy

**Primary Variable:** All twelve. This is where they all come together.

<!-- Definition of Ready — perspective -->

**Why This Week Matters:**
The entire course has been building toward this question: where does the metaphor break, and why does that matter? Students who can identify the limits of an analogy — and defend that claim with evidence — have a skill that transfers to every scientific discipline and every technology debate they'll ever encounter.

<!-- Definition of Ready — assertions -->

**Learning Goals:**
- Select a specific gap in the brain-software metaphor and articulate it precisely
- Construct an evidence-based argument using data from their experiments and research
- Present findings clearly and defend them under questioning
- Evaluate classmates' arguments and offer constructive critique
- Reflect on their own learning and growth across the six weeks

<!-- Definition of Ready — outside -->

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This is NOT a test — there are no right answers about where the metaphor breaks most
- Students are NOT expected to solve the gap — only to describe it and explain why it matters
- This week does NOT introduce new neuroscience content

## Day 1: Choose Your Gap

### Objective

Students will review their Week 1 hypothesis (and all updates), select their strongest claim about where the metaphor breaks, and begin building their argument.

### Materials Needed

- All previous worksheets and experiment data (returned to students)
- [Week 6 Worksheet A: Argument Builder](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-a.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Students have completed Weeks 1–5
- All experiment data and worksheets are available

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Five weeks ago you wrote your first hypothesis about where the brain-software metaphor fails. Pull it out. Read it. How much has changed?"

### Main Activity (35 minutes)

#### Part 1: Hypothesis Review (10 minutes)

Students read their original Week 1 hypothesis and all the updates they've made. In pairs, they discuss: "What's your strongest claim right now? What evidence do you have?"

#### Part 2: Argument Construction (25 minutes)

Using [Worksheet A](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-a.md), students build their argument:
1. **Claim** — one clear sentence stating where the metaphor breaks
2. **Evidence** — at least three pieces of evidence from their experiments, the case studies, and the class discussions
3. **Reasoning** — why this gap matters (for neuroscience, for AI, for both)
4. **Counterargument** — what someone who disagrees might say, and why they'd be wrong (or partly right)

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Tomorrow you refine. Wednesday you rehearse. Thursday and Friday you present. This is yours — your claim, your evidence, your voice."

### Exit Ticket

"My claim: The brain-software metaphor breaks most significantly at ___ because ___."

## Day 2: Build the Presentation

### Objective

Students will build their final presentation, organizing evidence into a clear, compelling structure.

### Materials Needed

- Presentation materials (poster, slides, whiteboard — student choice)
- [Week 6 Worksheet B: Presentation Outline](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-b.md)
- All previous experiment data

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Argument built on Day 1

### Warm-Up (3 minutes)

"You have 5 minutes to present and 2 minutes to defend. That means every sentence has to earn its place. Build tight."

### Main Activity (40 minutes)

Students build their presentations. Structure:
1. The claim (30 seconds)
2. The biological evidence (1 minute)
3. The software evidence (1 minute)
4. Why the gap matters (1 minute)
5. The counterargument and your response (1.5 minutes)

Teacher circulates, pushing for precision: "Can you say that in fewer words?" "What's your strongest piece of evidence?" "What will someone challenge?"

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Tomorrow you rehearse with a partner. They're going to ask you the hardest question they can think of. Be ready."

### Exit Ticket

"The part of my presentation I'm most confident about is ___. The part I'm most worried about is ___."

## Day 3: Rehearse and Refine

### Objective

Students will rehearse their presentations with a partner, receive critical feedback, and refine their arguments.

### Materials Needed

- Presentation materials
- [Week 6 Worksheet C: Peer Feedback](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-c.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Presentations built on Day 2

### Warm-Up (3 minutes)

"Today you present to one person. They're going to be honest. That's a gift."

### Main Activity (37 minutes)

#### Part 1: Partner Rehearsal (20 minutes)

Pairs present to each other. The listener fills out [Worksheet C](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-c.md):
- Was the claim clear?
- Was the evidence convincing?
- What's the strongest part?
- What's the weakest part?
- What question would stump them?

Then switch.

#### Part 2: Revise (17 minutes)

Based on feedback, students revise. This is where presentations get sharp.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Tomorrow, first half presents. Friday, second half. Remember: the audience isn't just listening — they're evaluating. And they'll ask questions."

### Exit Ticket

"Based on my partner's feedback, I changed ___ because ___."

## Day 4: Presentations (First Half)

### Objective

First half of students present their capstone arguments and defend them under questioning.

### Materials Needed

- Presentation setup (projector if using slides)
- [Week 6 Worksheet D: Audience Evaluation](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-d.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- Presentations rehearsed and refined

### Warm-Up (2 minutes)

"Presenters: be clear, be brave, be ready. Audience: listen hard, ask real questions."

### Main Activity (43 minutes)

Each presenter: 5 minutes to present + 2 minutes of Q&A from the audience.

Audience members complete [Worksheet D](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-d.md) for each presentation — evaluating the claim, evidence, and reasoning.

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (3 minutes)

"What's one argument you heard today that made you rethink something?"

### Exit Ticket

"The strongest argument I heard today was ___'s because ___."

## Day 5: Presentations (Second Half) and Course Reflection

### Objective

Second half of students present, then the class reflects on the entire six-week journey.

### Materials Needed

- Presentation setup
- [Week 6 Worksheet E: Course Reflection](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-e.md)

### Prerequisites for This Day

- First half presented on Day 4

### Warm-Up (2 minutes)

"Last day. Second half presents, then we reflect on all of it."

### Main Activity (43 minutes)

#### Part 1: Presentations (28 minutes)

Remaining presenters. Same format: 5 + 2.

#### Part 2: Course Reflection (15 minutes)

Students complete [Worksheet E](./worksheets/week-6-worksheet-e.md):
- "The most important thing I learned about the brain is ___"
- "The most important thing I learned about AI is ___"
- "My original hypothesis was ___. Now I think ___"
- "The scipraxian Variable that mattered most to me was ___ because ___"
- "One question I still have is ___"

### Reflection / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

"Six weeks ago, you knew that brains think and software computes. Now you know it's not that simple. The brain is messier, more integrated, more emotional, and more mysterious than any software that tries to imitate it. And the software — when it's honest about what it's doing — teaches us things about the brain we wouldn't have noticed otherwise."

"The grid is free. The brain is open-source. Go find out more."

### Common Questions & Troubleshooting

**Q: Some presentations are much stronger than others.**
A: That's expected. Evaluate growth, not absolute quality. A student who started confused and ended with a clear, evidence-based claim has succeeded regardless of polish.

**Q: Students are repeating each other's claims.**
A: That's fine if the evidence is different. Two students can claim "emotion is the biggest gap" and support it with completely different experiments and reasoning.

**Q: The Q&A is awkward or silent.**
A: Seed it. Have 2-3 questions ready to ask if the audience doesn't engage. Model what a good question sounds like.

## Summary

Week 6 is the payoff. Students take ownership of a claim, build an argument, and defend it. The diversity of answers — some will say emotion, some memory, some consciousness, some the binding problem — demonstrates that the course worked. They're not repeating what they were told. They're arguing from evidence about questions that don't have clean answers. That's science. That's inquiry. That's the whole point.
