# Module 1: The Operating System

## What Experience Master Is

Experience Master is an operating system for teams. Not a methodology. Not a framework. Not a set of best practices you can adopt piecemeal. An operating system.

The distinction matters. A methodology tells you what to do. A framework gives you a structure and lets you fill it in. An operating system runs. It has a clock. It has a cycle. It has processes that execute whether you pay attention to them or not. When you install an operating system on a computer, the computer runs it continuously — it does not "do" the operating system during business hours and stop on weekends.

Experience Master works the same way. Once your team adopts it, it runs. The two-week Iteration clock ticks. The daily standup happens. The ceremonies execute on schedule. The operating system creates rhythm, and rhythm creates reliability.

Michael Clark and Andrew Piper designed Experience Master after observing what goes wrong with other agile approaches. The most common failure mode: teams adopt ceremonies without understanding why. They hold standups that turn into status meetings. They estimate stories that will never be estimated accurately. They hold retrospectives that produce action items no one acts on. The ceremonies become rituals — performed out of habit, devoid of meaning.

Experience Master prevents this by tying every ceremony to a specific purpose, every role to a specific responsibility, and every artifact to a specific need. Nothing is optional because nothing is decorative.

## The Iteration

The fundamental unit of time in Experience Master is the **Iteration** — a two-week cycle during which the team commits to, executes, and delivers a defined scope of work.

Two weeks. Not one. Not three. Not "whatever feels right." Two weeks, every time, without exception.

Why two weeks?

- **One week is too short.** By the time you plan, start, hit problems, recover, and deliver, the week is over. There is no room for the unexpected, and there is always something unexpected.
- **Three weeks is too long.** Feedback loops stretch. Urgency dissipates. The beginning of the Iteration feels disconnected from the end. People lose the thread.
- **Two weeks is the rhythm of a paycheck.** People already think in two-week cycles. Experience Master aligns with existing mental models.

The Iteration has a defined beginning (Planning) and a defined end (Demo and Retrospective). Between them, the team executes. The clock is absolute — the Iteration ends on its scheduled date regardless of whether all work is complete. Incomplete work rolls to the next Iteration. The team does not extend the Iteration. They learn from the miss.

## The Daily Standup

Every working day, the team holds a standup. This is not a status meeting. It is not a place to report to the PM what you did yesterday. It is a synchronization point.

Each Worker answers three questions:

1. **What did I complete since the last standup?** Not "what did I work on." What did I complete? Completion is the unit of progress.
2. **What will I complete before the next standup?** Not "what will I work on." What will I complete? This is a commitment, not a plan.
3. **What is blocking me?** Not "what is hard" or "what is annoying." What is preventing completion? Blocks are the PM's responsibility to remove.

The standup is fifteen minutes. It is not a discussion forum. If a topic requires discussion, it is taken offline — meaning two people talk after the standup, not during it. The standup is for synchronization: everyone hears everyone else's state, blocks are surfaced, and the PM knows where to focus.

PMs do not answer the three questions. PMs listen. PMs take notes on blocks. PMs act on blocks immediately after the standup. The PM's job during the standup is to absorb information, not to give it.

## The Rhythm

Experience Master creates a predictable rhythm:

**Week 1 of the Iteration:**
- Monday: Planning (first day of the Iteration)
- Tuesday-Friday: Executing, with daily standups

**Week 2 of the Iteration:**
- Monday-Wednesday: Executing, with daily standups
- Thursday: Demo (present completed work)
- Friday: Retrospective (the most important meeting — Module 4 explains why)

Between Iterations:
- Sifting happens in the gap between the Retrospective and the next Iteration's Planning. This is where work is examined, clarified, and prepared for the next Iteration.

The rhythm is the point. Teams that execute with rhythm are more productive, more predictable, and less stressed than teams that operate in an ad hoc manner. The rhythm removes the need to decide "what should we do today?" The operating system already knows.

## Why "Operating System" and Not "Methodology"

Three reasons:

**1. An operating system runs.** A methodology is a set of ideas. You can read about a methodology, agree with it, and never do anything. An operating system executes. If you install Experience Master and do not run the ceremonies, it is obvious — the way a computer without an operating system is obviously broken.

**2. An operating system is a platform.** You run applications on an operating system. In Experience Master, the "applications" are your team's actual work — the features, bugs, improvements, and experiments you execute each Iteration. The operating system does not dictate what work you do. It provides the structure in which you do it.

**3. An operating system can be upgraded.** Experience Master is not static. The Retrospective (Module 4) is the upgrade mechanism. Every two weeks, the team examines the operating system itself and decides what to change. Over time, each team's version of Experience Master diverges from the default — and that is correct. The operating system adapts to the team, not the other way around.

## The Scipraxian Context

Experience Master is rooted in scipraxianism — the integration of scientific inquiry with practical action. Several of the twelve Variables are directly visible in the operating system:

- **Time** — the two-week Iteration is a time box. Time is not flexible. Work fits within the time, or it does not.
- **Responsibility** — every piece of work has an owner. Every ceremony has a purpose. Every role has defined accountability.
- **Humility** — the Retrospective assumes the operating system can always be improved. Humility is the willingness to examine your own process and admit it is imperfect.
- **Perseverance** — Iterations repeat. Every two weeks, the team starts again. The rhythm sustains effort over months and years.
- **Inclusion** — the standup ensures everyone is heard. The Retrospective ensures every voice can shape the process.

## Think About It

- What is the "operating system" your team currently runs? Is it explicit (documented, named, followed) or implicit (habits, assumptions, unspoken rules)?
- If you switched to a two-week Iteration tomorrow, what would be the hardest adjustment? What would be the easiest?
- The standup question is "what will I complete" not "what will I work on." What is the difference between working and completing? Why does Experience Master care about the distinction?
