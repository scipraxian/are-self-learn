# Day 5: Show and Tell + Big Questions

**Objective:** Campers present their experiments, the group tackles the week's biggest questions together, and everyone goes home with a certificate and a brain full of new ideas.

**Materials:**
- Printed certificates (one per camper — "Brain Scientist and AI Explorer" or similar)
- Whiteboard or large paper for the Big Questions discussion
- The Big Question Board from Day 2 (with campers' sticky-note questions)
- Timer for presentations
- Treats for the party (optional but strongly recommended — it's the last day of camp)
- Camera (optional, for group photo)

---

## Setup (5 minutes)

Post the Big Question Board from Day 2 where everyone can see it. Set up the presentation area — a spot where each pair can stand and talk to the group. Have the timer ready. Put the certificates somewhere out of sight until the end.

Set out the party treats but tell campers they're for after presentations. Motivation works.

---

## Activity 1: Experiment Presentations (20 minutes)

### Ground Rules (2 minutes)

"Today you share what you found. Rules for presenting: just tell us what you did and what happened. Rules for listening: be respectful, be curious, and save questions for the end of each presentation."

### Presentations (18 minutes)

Each pair presents for 2 minutes, followed by one question from the audience (30 seconds).

With 2-minute presentations and 30-second Q&A, you can fit 7-8 pairs in 18 minutes. If you have more pairs, shorten to 90 seconds each.

**Instructor role:**
- Keep time. Hold up a "30 seconds left" signal.
- After each presentation, lead the applause.
- If a pair is nervous, stand next to them and ask prompting questions: "So what did you test? What happened?"
- Note recurring themes for the Big Questions discussion.

**Things to listen for and highlight:**
- Experiments where the AI surprised them (better or worse than expected)
- Experiments where the human side was clearly stronger
- Creative experiment designs
- Honest admissions of "we're not sure what this means"

After all presentations: "Every single one of you designed an experiment, ran it, and reported results. That's more science than most adults do in a year."

---

## Activity 2: The Big Questions (20 minutes)

### Revisit the Question Board (3 minutes)

Pull out the Big Question Board from Day 2. Read some of the questions aloud.

"Let's see how many of these we can answer now — or at least answer better than we could on Day 2."

Go through 3-4 of the posted questions. Let campers answer them using what they've learned. Don't answer for them. If a question is still open, say so: "We don't know yet. Nobody does. And that's okay."

### The Two Big Lists (10 minutes)

On the whiteboard, create two columns:

**What can brains do that AI can't?**

**What can AI do that brains can't?**

Go around the room. Each camper contributes one item to either column. Use evidence from the experiments, the memory wars, the token activity, and the demos.

Expected items might include:

*Brains can:*
- Feel emotions
- Make personal connections to memories
- Understand sarcasm and humor intuitively
- Be creative in a truly original way
- Know what it's like to experience something
- Change their mind based on a gut feeling
- Care about things

*AI can:*
- Remember things perfectly
- Process information faster
- Work without getting tired
- Handle huge amounts of data at once
- Not get distracted
- Be copied and run in multiple places at once
- Follow instructions precisely every time

### The Hardest Question (7 minutes)

"Here's the last question. There's no right answer, and smart people disagree about it."

**"Will AI ever be able to do everything a brain can do? Or is there something about brains that software can never copy?"**

Open discussion. Let campers argue, agree, disagree, and wonder. Don't steer toward a conclusion. The goal is for every camper to have a position — even if that position is "I don't know, and I think that's the most honest answer."

If the discussion is strong, let it run. If it stalls, prompt with:
- "What about consciousness? Does AI need to be conscious to do everything a brain does?"
- "What about emotions? Can you program feelings?"
- "What about dying? Your brain knows it won't last forever. Does that change how it thinks?"

That last one touches on Permadeath — the twelfth Variable. You don't need to name it. Just let the idea sit.

---

## Activity 3: Certificates and Party (15 minutes)

### Certificates (5 minutes)

"You spent a week studying the most complicated object in the known universe and comparing it to the most impressive technology humans have ever built. You ran experiments. You asked hard questions. You earned this."

Hand out certificates one at a time. Use each camper's name. If you remember something specific they said or did during the week, mention it: "Jordan, you asked Are-Self if it dreams — that was one of the best questions of the week."

### The Party (10 minutes)

Treats. Conversation. Let campers look at their brain drawings from Day 1 one last time. Take a group photo if they're up for it.

If campers want to ask Are-Self one last question as a group, let them. It's a good way to end.

### The Last Word

"You know more about your brain than you did on Monday. You know more about AI than most adults. And you know the difference between an opinion and an opinion backed by evidence. Whatever you do with that, it's yours."

---

## Take-Home Thought

*"The question isn't whether AI is smarter than you. The question is whether 'smarter' is even the right word. Your brain and AI are good at different things — and knowing which is which might be the most important skill of the next fifty years."*
