# Day 2: Can Computers Think?

**Objective:** Campers learn what AI actually is (and isn't), understand tokens through a physical activity, and watch a live AI system in action.

**Materials:**
- A bag of small candy or physical tokens (at least 100 pieces — M&Ms, Skittles, blocks, poker chips)
- Small cups or bowls (one per camper or pair)
- Computer running Are-Self with projector
- Whiteboard or large paper for recording questions and observations

---

## Setup (5 minutes)

Put the candy/tokens in a central bowl. Have cups ready. Open Are-Self. Write on the board: "Can computers think? We'll answer this by Friday."

---

## Activity 1: What Is AI, Really? (20 minutes)

### The Myth Buster (5 minutes)

"Before we talk about what AI is, let's talk about what it isn't. Raise your hand if you agree with each statement. Don't worry — there are no grades here."

- "AI is a robot." (Some hands. Discuss: most AI has no body at all.)
- "AI is alive." (Fewer hands. Discuss: what would it need to be alive?)
- "AI understands what it's saying." (Split hands. Discuss: this is the hard question. We're not going to answer it today.)
- "AI could take over the world." (Some hands. Discuss: the AI you'll meet today has trouble remembering what you told it five minutes ago. World domination seems unlikely.)

### So What IS It? (7 minutes)

Explain AI simply:
- AI is software that finds patterns in data and uses those patterns to make predictions.
- A language model (like the one Are-Self uses) was trained on billions of words. It learned patterns: what words tend to follow what other words.
- When you ask it a question, it's not "looking up" the answer. It's predicting what words should come next, based on patterns it learned.

Analogy: "Imagine you've read every book in a library. You don't remember specific pages, but you have a strong feeling for what kind of sentence comes after what kind of question. That's what the AI does — at enormous scale and speed."

### Tokens: The Currency of Thought (8 minutes)

"Every time AI reads or writes a word, it costs something. The currency is called a token."

Give each camper (or pair) a cup with 20 candy pieces. Each candy piece is a token.

"A token is roughly one word, but not exactly. Short words are one token. Long words might be two or three. Let's practice."

Read a sentence: "The quick brown fox jumped over the lazy dog." Count the tokens together — roughly 9 tokens for 9 words (simplified for camp purposes).

"Reading that sentence cost 9 tokens. Answering a question about that sentence might cost 50 tokens. A long conversation might cost thousands."

"Now here's the catch: tokens cost real money. Not much per token — fractions of a cent — but it adds up. Remember the hypothalamus? The brain region that manages your body's resources? Are-Self has a hypothalamus app that manages token spending. If the budget runs out, the AI stops thinking."

Campers "spend" tokens from their cup for each question they'd want to ask Are-Self. When the cup is empty, they're done. Which questions are worth spending on?

---

## Activity 2: Talk to the Machine (20 minutes)

### The Live Demo (12 minutes)

"Let's see this thing in action. We're going to ask Are-Self some questions and watch what happens."

Take questions from campers. Ask at least 5 different types:
1. A factual question ("What's the tallest mountain?")
2. A creative question ("Write a haiku about brains")
3. A tricky question ("What's your favorite color?" — it doesn't have preferences the way we do)
4. A reasoning question ("If I have 3 apples and give away 2, then find 4, how many do I have?")
5. A meta question ("Are you thinking right now?")

After each answer, ask the group:
- "Was that a good answer?"
- "Did it understand the question, or did it just produce words that sound right?"
- "Could you tell which 'brain region' was working?"

Point out what Are-Self is doing when it's visible: "See that? It checked its memory — that's the hippocampus. Now it's reasoning — that's the frontal lobe. The hypothalamus just picked which AI model to use."

### Camper-Driven Interaction (8 minutes)

Let campers come up and type their own questions. One at a time, each camper (or pair) gets to ask one question and see the response.

Encourage them to try to break it. Ask something weird. Ask something it shouldn't know. Ask it to do something impossible. The failures are as interesting as the successes.

---

## Activity 3: What Did We Learn? (15 minutes)

### Pattern Spotting (8 minutes)

On the whiteboard, create two columns: "AI is good at" and "AI is bad at."

From the demo, fill them in together:
- Good at: factual recall, generating text quickly, following instructions, being polite
- Bad at: having genuine opinions, understanding context the way humans do, knowing when it's wrong, being creative in a truly original way

"This list is going to change by Friday. You're going to add to it and cross things off based on what we discover."

### The Big Question Board (7 minutes)

Give each camper a sticky note. Write one question they want answered by the end of the week.

Post all questions on a "Big Question Board." Read a few aloud. "These are your questions. By Friday, we'll see how many we can answer."

---

## Take-Home Thought

*"AI is not magic. It's math finding patterns in data. The real question isn't whether it's smart — it's whether 'smart' is even the right word."*
