# Brains, Bytes, and Big Questions

**A one-week summer camp where 7th and 8th graders spend an hour a day poking at the biggest questions in science: What's inside your head? Can computers think? Who remembers better — you or the machine? This is not school. This is camp.**

## What This Is

Five days. One hour each day. Hands-on activities, live AI interaction, zero homework. Campers walk away knowing more about their own brain than most adults, having talked to a real AI system, and having an opinion about whether machines can think — backed up by evidence they gathered themselves.

This is a summer camp. Fun is the primary variable. If campers aren't having a good time, nothing else matters.

## Prerequisites

**None.** Ages 12-14. Curiosity required; everything else is optional.

## Who It's For

7th and 8th graders (ages 12-14). No prior knowledge required. Campers who've never thought about brains or AI are welcome. So are the ones who've been building chatbots in their bedroom since 5th grade — there's enough depth here for both.

## What to Bring

**Campers need:**
- A working brain (required)
- Curiosity (required)
- A notebook and pen (helpful but we'll have extras)

**Instructors need:**
- One computer running Are-Self with a projector or large screen
- A bag of small candy or tokens (for Day 2's token activity)
- A tray and 20 small common objects (for Day 3's memory test)
- Printed certificates (for Day 5)
- Sticky notes, markers, large paper
- Good energy

## What Campers Will Walk Away With

By Friday, every camper will be able to:
1. Name the major brain regions and say what each one does — in their own words, not textbook language
2. Explain what AI actually is to a friend or family member without using the word "robot"
3. Describe one specific thing that human brains do better than AI, with evidence
4. Describe one specific thing that AI does better than human brains, with evidence
5. Have an informed opinion about whether computers can think

## Instructor Notes

**Pace:** One hour goes fast. Every activity has a time budget. Stick to it. If something runs long, cut the next thing short rather than running over. Campers who are still talking at the end is a sign of success, not a problem.

**Tone:** This is camp. Be enthusiastic. Be a little silly. Use first names. Sit in a circle if you can. No one raises their hand unless they want to — just talk.

**Participation:** Not every camper will want to talk. That's fine. Pair work and small-group activities let quieter campers contribute without being on the spot. The only mandatory public speaking is the 2-minute presentation on Day 5, and even that can be done in pairs.

**AI interaction:** Every day includes at least one moment where campers interact with Are-Self (or watch the instructor do it). This is the hook. Use it.

**The three habits:** Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry are the three kid-scale scipraxian habits from *Mira and the Are-Self*. They apply here too. Include everyone. Admit when you don't know. Ask better questions. You don't need to teach them explicitly — just model them.

**Flexibility:** If the campers are deeply engaged in a discussion and you need to skip an activity, skip it. Follow the energy. The objectives matter more than the specific activities.
