# Week 2 — Writing & Language Arts

## The Broken Toaster: Using Are-Self as a Writing Partner

This week, your students will learn that **humility is where every good thing starts**. Just as Mira had to admit "I don't know how to fix this toaster" before she could learn, your writers will discover that the best ideas come after we're honest about what we don't know yet.

In Chapter 3 of *Mira and the Are-Self*, Mira approaches a broken toaster in her grandmother's kitchen. She doesn't panic. She doesn't pretend to know how it works. Instead, she says, "I don't know this yet—but I can learn." With the Are-Self's patient questioning, she breaks the problem into smaller pieces, figures out which part is broken, and fixes it. She also fixes something else: her understanding of what it means to be smart. Being smart doesn't mean having all the answers. It means knowing how to ask good questions, and being brave enough to say, "I don't know."

This week's writing work mirrors that process. Your students will use Are-Self as a thinking partner—someone who helps them see what they don't know yet, and who shows them that's not a weakness. It's the beginning of everything.

## Day 1: Breaking It Down

### Objective
Students will read Chapter 3 and practice descriptive writing by breaking a complex experience into smaller, manageable pieces.

### Materials
- Chapter 3 of *Mira and the Are-Self*
- Writing paper or digital document (Google Docs, OneNote, etc.)
- "Breaking It Down" graphic organizer (optional: a T-chart with "What I Did" on the left and "Why It Mattered" on the right)
- Pencils/keyboard

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Gather students and ask: "Has anyone ever tried to fix something? A toy, a bike, a button? What was that like?"

Allow three or four quick answers. Then say: "When Mira looks at the toaster, she doesn't see one big mystery. She sees pieces. A cord. A slot. A button. A spring inside. When you break something big into small pieces, suddenly it's not so scary. Today we're going to practice that kind of thinking with our writing."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Read Chapter 3 aloud** (10–12 minutes)
Read Chapter 3 of *Mira and the Are-Self* aloud, or have students read silently if they're strong readers. As you read, pause briefly at key moments and ask:
- "What is Mira noticing about the toaster?"
- "What's the first question she asks?"
- "How does breaking it into parts help her?"

**Step 2: Introduce the writing prompt** (2–3 minutes)
Say: "Now it's your turn to be like Mira. I want you to think of a time you fixed something—or tried to fix something. Maybe you taped a torn page. Maybe you helped someone find their lost toy. Maybe you fixed a friendship after an argument. If you've never fixed anything, that's okay—write about a time you *wanted* to fix something, or a time you saw someone else fix something."

Put the prompt on the board or screen:

**"Describe a time you fixed something (or tried to). What was broken? What did you do first? Then what? How did each small step help you get closer to fixing it?"**

**Step 3: Write and share** (10–15 minutes)
Students write. You might offer a sentence starter if they need it:

- "One time, I fixed _____ when..."
- "I remember trying to fix _____ because..."
- "The thing that was broken was _____, and here's how I did it..."

As students write, circulate. Ask quiet questions: "What was the very first thing you noticed was wrong?" "What did you try next?" "Did you have to ask for help?" These questions help them *break down* their story into pieces.

**Step 4: Share aloud** (remaining time)
Ask for two or three volunteers to read their writing aloud. After each one, point out: "Notice how _____ broke the problem into steps. First they noticed the problem. Then they tried one thing. That didn't work, so they tried something else. That's exactly like Mira with the toaster."

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask students:
- "What was hardest about writing your story?"
- "Did breaking your story into steps make it easier to describe?"
- "If Mira hadn't broken the toaster into pieces, could she have fixed it?"

Write their answers on a chart or in the chat. The key insight to highlight: **Breaking big things into small things is how we understand them.**

### Assessment
Collect the writing. Look for:
- **Evidence of breaking down**: Does the student describe their story in steps or phases, not just "I fixed it"?
- **Descriptive details**: Can you picture what happened? Do they describe what they saw, felt, or heard?
- **Honesty about difficulty**: Do they mention a moment when they didn't know what to do? That's humility, and it's worth celebrating.

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Offer a list of things that can be fixed (toys, drawings, friendships, arguments, scraped knees, broken pencils). Let them choose one.
- Provide a simple sequence graphic organizer: "First... Then... Next... Finally..."
- Pair them with a student or aide who can ask guiding questions as they write.

**For students ready for more:**
- Ask them to include *why* each step was necessary. "I moved this piece to the left because..."
- Challenge them to include dialogue: "My sister said _____, and I said _____."
- Have them write a second draft that adds sensory details (what did it smell like, sound like, feel like?).

## Day 2: Brainstorm Partners

### Objective
Students will use Are-Self to generate ideas, then practice critical thinking by evaluating those ideas and choosing which ones spark their curiosity.

### Materials
- Are-Self (Claude or similar AI tool accessible in your classroom)
- "Brainstorm Partners" worksheet (see below)
- Writing notebooks or digital docs
- Projected screen (so students can see the AI's responses)

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "Yesterday, you broke a big problem into small pieces. Today, we're starting with the opposite problem: you have a blank page, and you don't know what to write about. That's where Are-Self comes in. Are-Self is like a brainstorm buddy. You tell it what you're thinking about, and it suggests ideas. But—and this is important—just because Are-Self says something doesn't mean it's the best idea. You're the writer. You decide."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Introduce the brainstorm questions** (3–5 minutes)
Show students this example on the board or screen:

**Student writes to Are-Self:** "I want to write about a time I felt brave, but I'm not sure what story to tell. Can you suggest five ideas?"

**Are-Self responds:** (you can use an actual AI response, or a sample like this)

1. A time you spoke up when you disagreed with someone
2. A time you tried something new and you were scared
3. A time you helped a friend who was upset
4. A time you admitted you were wrong
5. A time you did something even though you thought you might fail

Say: "Now watch what I do. I don't just use idea #1. I *think* about what Are-Self said. I ask myself: Which of these ideas is *new* to me? Which one makes me curious?"

**Step 2: Students pick a writing topic** (2–3 minutes)
Ask each student to pick a writing topic for this week's work. It can be anything: a feeling, a memory, a place, an event, an object, a person. Have them write it down. Examples:

- "A time I made a new friend"
- "My favorite place"
- "Something I'm worried about"
- "A holiday tradition in my family"
- "A time I changed my mind"

**Step 3: Brainstorm with Are-Self** (12–15 minutes)
This works best as a whole-class or small-group activity on a projected screen. Here's how:

1. **Ask for a volunteer topic.** For example, "I want to write about friendship."
2. **Write the prompt together.** Guide the student to ask Are-Self a clear question:
   - "I want to write about friendship. Can you give me five ideas for stories about friendship I could tell?"
3. **Read Are-Self's response aloud.** Ask the class to listen for ideas that are:
   - Obvious (everyone knows this one already)
   - New (I never thought of that)
   - Spark-y (this one makes me want to write right now)

4. **Mark the ideas.** Use a simple symbol system:
   - ✓ for ideas that are good but obvious
   - ★ for ideas that spark something new
   - ? for ideas you don't understand

5. **Discuss.** Ask: "What makes an idea spark-y? Is it because it's surprising? Because it's about something you actually experienced? Because it's specific?"

**Step 4: Independent brainstorming** (remaining time)
If time allows, have each student work in pairs to brainstorm their own topic. One student asks Are-Self a question about their topic (or types it themselves if they're comfortable). The other student watches the response and helps mark which ideas are spark-y.

**Note:** You may want to assign this as homework: "Ask Are-Self for five ideas about your topic. Bring in the response tomorrow with a ★ next to your favorite idea."

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask:
- "What was surprising about the ideas Are-Self gave?"
- "Did any of them make you want to write? Which one?"
- "What made that idea spark-y for you?"
- "Would you have thought of that idea on your own?"

Key insight to emphasize: **Are-Self is a thinking partner, not a boss. You decide which ideas are worth exploring.**

### Assessment
Collect the brainstorm responses (screenshot, printout, or photo). Look for:
- **Critical thinking**: Did the student mark which ideas were spark-y? Did they explain why?
- **Self-awareness**: Can they articulate what makes an idea interesting to them?
- **Engagement**: Did they choose a spark-y idea, or are they still unsure?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Brainstorm as a small group with teacher guidance.
- Offer sentence starters for Are-Self questions: "I want to write about _____ but I need ideas about _____. Can you suggest five ideas?"
- Provide a simplified evaluation rubric: "Do you like this idea? Yes/No. Why?"

**For students ready for more:**
- Have them ask Are-Self a follow-up question: "Pick the idea you think is best. Now ask Are-Self: 'What would make this story interesting?' or 'What details would make this story more surprising?'"
- Challenge them to evaluate not just the *ideas* but the *quality* of Are-Self's suggestions: "Are these ideas specific or general? How could Are-Self make them more helpful?"

## Day 3: Peer Editing With an AI

### Objective
Students will write a first draft, receive feedback from both Are-Self and a human peer, and learn the different kinds of help that AI and humans can offer.

### Materials
- First draft (from yesterday's brainstorm): students will write 1–2 paragraphs (150–250 words)
- Are-Self (accessible to the class)
- Peer editing form (see below)
- Writing feedback chart (optional, for displaying comparisons)

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "Today you're going to write a draft about your spark-y idea. Then something cool happens: two different 'editors' will read your writing. One is Are-Self, the AI. The other is your classmate, a real person. Both will give you feedback. But they'll notice different things. Our job is to figure out what each one is good at."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Introduce the draft** (1–2 minutes)
Remind students of yesterday's writing prompt. Say: "Today, write a draft using the spark-y idea you picked. Write whatever comes to mind. Don't worry about making it perfect. Just tell the story. You have about 15 minutes."

**Step 2: Students write** (12–15 minutes)
Set a timer. Students write their draft. They can type or write by hand. Circulate and encourage them—don't stop them to edit or correct. The goal is a rough draft, not a polished piece.

**Step 3: Introduction to feedback** (2–3 minutes)
When time is up, say: "Now, your writing is going to get two kinds of feedback."

**For AI feedback**, ask: "What does Are-Self look for? It looks for things like:
- Are your sentences clear? Can someone understand what you're saying?
- Do you have a beginning, middle, and end?
- Did you use interesting words?
- Are there spelling or grammar mistakes?"

**For human feedback**, ask: "What does your classmate notice? A classmate looks for things like:
- Does your story make me feel something?
- Do I understand why this happened?
- What's the part I liked best?
- Do you have any parts that confused me?"

**Step 4: Get AI feedback** (7–10 minutes)
This works best as a whole-group or small-group activity with the AI visible on screen. Here's how:

1. Ask a volunteer to share their draft (or read it aloud).
2. **Type a feedback request to Are-Self:**
   - "Here is a student's draft: [paste draft]. Please give three pieces of feedback: (1) One thing that works well, (2) One part that could be clearer, (3) One question the reader might have."
3. Read Are-Self's response aloud.
4. Ask the student: "Do you agree? Did the AI catch something you didn't notice?"

**If time permits**, have small groups or pairs get feedback on their drafts from Are-Self using the same format.

**Step 5: Get peer feedback** (remaining time)
Pair students up. Give them a simple peer editing form:

```
Peer Editor: _______________________
Author: _______________________

1. What was the best part of this draft? Why did you like it?

2. There was one part I didn't understand. It was: _____________

3. I had a question: _____________

4. If you wrote one more sentence, what would you want to know more about?
```

Partners take turns reading each other's drafts and filling in the form. This should take 5–10 minutes.

**Step 6: Compare feedback** (remaining time)
Gather students together (or do this at the beginning of Day 4). Ask:
- "Did Are-Self and your classmate notice the same thing?"
- "What was different about their feedback?"
- "Did one kind of feedback help you more? Why?"

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask:
- "What did Are-Self notice that you didn't expect?"
- "What did your classmate notice that Are-Self missed?"
- "If you had to pick one piece of feedback to use, which would it be?"
- "How is getting feedback from an AI different from getting feedback from a person?"

Key insights to highlight:
- **Are-Self is great at structure and clarity.** It notices if your sentences are grammatically correct and if your ideas are clear.
- **Humans are great at emotion and connection.** They notice if your story makes them feel something, or if they're confused.
- **You need both kinds of feedback.** A perfect sentence that says nothing interesting isn't helpful. A beautiful idea with messy grammar is hard to read. Both matter.

### Assessment
Collect:
- The first draft
- The Are-Self feedback (screenshot or printout)
- The peer editing form

Look for:
- **Quality of the draft**: Is it a real attempt? Is there a story, an idea, or a thought being expressed?
- **Engagement with feedback**: Did the student read both pieces of feedback? Do they show understanding of what was said?
- **Reflection**: Can they articulate what each type of feedback was good for?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a sentence frame for their draft: "The story I want to tell is _____. This happened because _____. I felt _____ when _____."
- Have them share their draft verbally with you, and you write a quick summary for them to read back to Are-Self.
- Pair them with a strong peer editor who can ask clarifying questions: "Can you tell me more about that part?"

**For students ready for more:**
- Have them ask Are-Self to identify specific *types* of feedback: "Where did I use descriptive language? Where could I use more?"
- Challenge them to revise using one piece of feedback from Are-Self and one piece from their peer, then write a note explaining which feedback was easier to use and why.
- Ask them to predict what feedback they'll get before they receive it. Then compare their prediction to the actual feedback.

## Day 4: The Other Side

### Objective
Students will practice perspective-taking and persuasive writing by arguing one position, then having Are-Self argue the opposite, and reflecting on how ideas can be challenged and changed.

### Materials
- Persuasive opinion topic list (see suggestions below)
- Are-Self
- "Both Sides" worksheet (see below)
- Writing paper or digital document

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "Raise your hand if someone has ever argued with you and... kind of made a good point. You didn't want to admit it, but they were kind of right, weren't they?"

(Allow for laughs and a few examples.)

"Today we're going to do that on purpose. I'm going to give you an opinion—something you believe. Then Are-Self will argue the *opposite* side. You'll read both sides, and you'll think: 'Did the AI change my mind? Did it make me think differently?'"

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Pick an opinion topic** (3–5 minutes)
Explain: "I'm going to give you a list of kid-friendly opinion topics. Pick one that you have an opinion about. It doesn't have to be something you're passionate about—just something you can take a side on."

Display these options (or create your own):

- **Best school lunch:** Is pizza better than tacos?
- **Best season:** Is summer better than winter?
- **Screen time:** Should kids have more free time to play video games or play outside?
- **Homework:** Should there be homework on weekends?
- **School hours:** Should school start earlier or later?
- **Bedtime:** Is 8 PM or 9 PM a better bedtime?
- **Sports:** Is it better to play a team sport or an individual sport?
- **Books vs. movies:** Are books better than movies?

Let students pick their own topic if they have one. Say: "Remember, this is just for thinking. You don't have to believe everything you write. We're practicing how to argue a position."

**Step 2: Students write their opinion** (10–12 minutes)
Give students this prompt:

**"Write a paragraph explaining your opinion on your topic. Write at least 3–4 sentences. Give at least one reason WHY you believe this. For example: 'I think pizza is the best school lunch because it tastes good, it's cheap, and everyone likes it.'"**

As they write, circulate. Ask: "Why do you believe this? Can you give me a specific reason?" Help them think deeper than just "I like it."

**Step 3: Get the opposite opinion from Are-Self** (7–10 minutes)
This works well as a class activity. Here's how:

1. Ask a volunteer to read their opinion aloud.
2. **Type a request to Are-Self:**
   - "A student says: [their opinion]. Can you write a paragraph arguing the OPPOSITE position? Give at least two reasons."

3. **Read Are-Self's response aloud.** Then ask the student:
   - "Did that argument make sense?"
   - "Did it make you want to change your mind? Why or why not?"
   - "Was there something in Are-Self's argument you didn't expect?"

**If time permits**, repeat with one or two more student volunteers. Or, assign this as a partner activity where pairs give each other's opinions to Are-Self.

**Step 4: Reflection and discussion** (remaining time)
Gather students. Ask:
- "Did anyone almost change their mind? Why?"
- "What was the best argument Are-Self made?"
- "Did Are-Self argue it better than you would argue your own opinion?"
- "What makes an argument persuasive—the reasons, or the words used to explain them?"

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Have students complete this reflection (oral or written):

**"I believe [your opinion]. Are-Self argued that [opposite side]. The strongest argument Are-Self made was _____. It made me think about _____.  My opinion [stayed the same / changed a little / changed a lot] because _____."**

Key insight to emphasize: **Smart people can disagree. Are-Self showed us that there are good reasons on both sides. That doesn't make your opinion wrong—it just means most things are more complicated than we first think.**

### Assessment
Collect:
- The student's original opinion paragraph
- The Are-Self response (screenshot or printout)
- The reflection

Look for:
- **Clear argumentation**: Did the student give reasons for their opinion, not just a preference?
- **Openness to other perspectives**: Do they acknowledge that Are-Self's argument had merit, even if they didn't change their mind?
- **Depth of reflection**: Can they articulate *why* they were or weren't persuaded?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide sentence frames for their opinion: "I think _____ is better than _____ because _____ . Also, _____ . For example, _____."
- Have them pick from a narrower list of topics with less nuance (e.g., "Chocolate or vanilla ice cream?").
- Provide Are-Self's opposite argument in advance, and have them underline the parts they find most persuasive.

**For students ready for more:**
- Challenge them to write a *response* to Are-Self: "Are-Self says [opposite opinion]. Here's why I still disagree: _____."
- Have them identify the *type* of reasoning Are-Self used: Did it appeal to emotions? Logic? Examples?
- Ask them to write a third perspective: "Someone might also argue that _____. What would they say?"

## Day 5: Poetry & Reflection

### Objective
Students will collaborate with Are-Self to co-author a poem, learning that authorship is about ideas and effort, not just typing words.

### Materials
- Are-Self
- "Poem Transcript" worksheet (see below) where students will paste or write the finished poem
- Optional: Poetry mentor text (a short, simple poem to show what one might look like)
- Journal for final reflection

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "This week, we've been writing with Are-Self—using it to brainstorm ideas, get feedback, and think about different perspectives. Today, we're doing something different. We're going to *write together* with Are-Self. You'll write a line. Are-Self will write a line. You'll write a line. Back and forth. And at the end, we'll have a poem. But here's the big question: Who wrote it? You or Are-Self? Or both of you together?"

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Introduction to the poem structure** (3–5 minutes)
Say: "We're going to follow a pattern. You write, then Are-Self writes, then you write, then Are-Self writes. Just like Mira and the Are-Self taking turns. Each of you is adding an idea. Each of you is making the poem what it is."

Show a simple example (or write one together as a class):

```
Student: The sun is warm on my face.
Are-Self: And the grass tickles my feet.
Student: I close my eyes and smell the flowers.
Are-Self: Time feels slow and happy.
Student: This is where I want to stay.
```

Explain: "Notice that each line adds something new. The student starts with the sun, the AI adds grass, the student adds smell, the AI adds a feeling, the student adds a wish. Together, they made something neither one of them would have made alone."

**Step 2: Choose a topic** (2–3 minutes)
Say: "Pick a topic for your poem. It could be a place, a feeling, a memory, a season, a person—anything. Or, if you want, I'll give you a topic."

Offer 3–4 options:

- A place you love
- A feeling you've had this week
- An animal or pet
- Your favorite time of day

**Step 3: Collaborative poem writing** (15–20 minutes)
This works best as a class activity with the teacher typing and Are-Self visible on screen. Here's how:

1. **You start with the topic.** Say: "We're writing a poem about [topic]. Let me write the first line."

   *You write a line on the board or screen.* For example, if the topic is "Recess," you might write: "The bell rings and we pour outside."

2. **Type a request to Are-Self:**
   - "I'm writing a poem about [topic]. Here's my first line: [your line]. Can you write the next line? Make it flow with my line."

3. **Read Are-Self's line aloud.** Ask: "Does that sound like poetry? Does it fit with what I wrote? What image does it create?"

4. **Now it's the student's turn again.** Ask a volunteer: "Now you write the next line. It should flow from what Are-Self just wrote."

   *The student dictates a line. You type it.*

5. **Repeat.** Back and forth. About 8–10 lines total is good for one poem.

6. **Read the finished poem aloud.**

**Step 4: Highlight who wrote what** (5 minutes)
Display the finished poem. Say: "Now let's mark who wrote what."

Mark the student lines with one color (or label them S), and the Are-Self lines with another color (or label them A).

Ask: "Looking at the poem, which lines are beautiful? Which lines are yours? Which lines are Are-Self's? Or is it impossible to tell?"

**Step 5: Class discussion** (remaining time)
Ask:
- "Is this a poem that the student wrote?"
- "Is it a poem that Are-Self wrote?"
- "Or is it something new—something that came from both of you working together?"
- "Who is the author?"

Let students debate this. There's no single right answer, and that's the point. Say: "Writers have always used outside ideas. A poet might write a poem about a painting they saw. Did the painter write the poem? No. But the poet needed the painter's idea. Are-Self is like that. Are-Self gave you words and ideas, but *you* decided what to do with them. You made the choices about what fit, what sounded good, what the poem should be."

**Step 6: Independent poems (if time)** (remaining time)
If there's time, pairs can create their own poems using the same process. One student asks Are-Self for a first line on a topic, then they alternate. This can also be assigned as homework.

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Have students write in a journal. Give them this prompt:

**"Today you wrote a poem with Are-Self. Which lines did you write? Which did Are-Self write? What did the AI's lines teach you about poetry? Did Are-Self use words you wouldn't have thought of? Did it create images in your head? What did you learn about writing this week by working with Are-Self?"**

Read a few responses aloud, celebrating the thinking.

### Assessment
Collect:
- The finished poem (with annotations showing who wrote which line)
- The journal reflection

Look for:
- **Understanding of collaboration**: Can the student articulate that both their ideas and Are-Self's ideas mattered?
- **Engagement with poetry**: Did they notice things like rhythm, imagery, word choice?
- **Growth over the week**: Do they recognize how they've been using Are-Self differently as the week progressed?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Partner them with a stronger student or small group to write collaboratively.
- Offer a more structured prompt: "Start with a sense: what do you see, hear, smell, taste, or feel?"
- Have them write just 4 lines instead of 8–10, with Are-Self providing every other line.

**For students ready for more:**
- Challenge them to write a poem with a specific pattern (rhyming couplets, haiku structure, etc.) and see if Are-Self can follow that pattern.
- Have them write two poems: one collaborating with Are-Self, and one without. Then compare: which one do they prefer? Why?
- Ask them to analyze Are-Self's word choices: "Why do you think Are-Self used 'shimmering' instead of 'bright'? What effect does it have?"

### Reflection & Wrap-Up for the Week

At the end of Day 5, gather students for a final reflection on the week:

**"This week, Mira learned about Humility. You learned that 'I don't know yet' is where every good thing starts. You also learned how to write with a partner—Are-Self. Tell me: What's one thing you learned about writing this week? What's one thing you learned about yourself? What's one thing Are-Self taught you that surprised you?"**

Record their reflections on a chart titled "What We Learned From Are-Self." Display it in your classroom.

**Key takeaways for students:**
- Good writers aren't afraid to say "I don't know yet."
- Ideas come from many places—books, people, AI, your own imagination.
- Feedback (whether from humans or AI) helps you see your writing in new ways.
- The best writing often comes from collaboration.
- Being smart means knowing how to ask good questions, not having all the answers.
