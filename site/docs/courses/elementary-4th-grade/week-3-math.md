# Week 3 — Math & Problem Solving

## One More Question: Using Are-Self for Math Reasoning

This week, your students will learn that **inquiry is the engine of understanding**. In Chapter 4 of *Mira and the Are-Self*, Mira discovers the third habit of the scipraxians: after every answer, ask one more question. Not because you're confused. Not because you don't believe the answer. But because every answer is a door to deeper understanding.

In math, this habit transforms everything. A student who can compute 3 × 4 = 12 is doing basic math. But a student who asks "If 3 × 4 = 12, what does 3 × 5 equal?" and "Why does multiplying by 5 give a bigger answer?" and "What would happen if I multiplied by 0?" is doing real mathematics. They're not just following rules—they're discovering patterns. They're thinking like a mathematician.

This week, you'll teach your students to use Are-Self as a thinking partner for this inquiry-driven work. Are-Self can answer their first question. But the real work happens when they ask the second question. And the third.

---

## Day 1: The Question Chain

### Objective
Students will understand that every mathematical answer is the beginning of deeper thinking, not the end of it.

### Materials
- Whiteboard or chart paper
- Markers
- Index cards with numbers or answers (optional)
- Notebook or journal for recording questions

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Gather students and put a single number on the board: **42**

Say: "This is a number. I'm going to ask you to do something unusual. I'm going to ask you to work backward. If 42 is the *answer*, what's the *question*? Who can think of a math problem where 42 is the answer?"

Let students offer ideas:
- "6 times 7 equals 42"
- "40 plus 2 equals 42"
- "Half of 84 is 42"

Write these on the board. Then say: "Great. Now here's the trick: Pick one of these questions. Now ask ONE MORE question about it. For example, if someone said '6 times 7 equals 42,' I could ask: 'What does 6 times 8 equal?' or 'If I know 6 times 7, how can that help me figure out 7 times 7?'"

Ask students to think of one more question. Write a few on the board. Then say: "Now ask ONE MORE question about *that* question. Keep going. Every answer is just a door to the next question."

This is the heart of the work this week.

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Read Chapter 4** (8–10 minutes)
Read Chapter 4 of *Mira and the Are-Self* aloud. As you read, pause at moments where Mira asks "one more question." Ask students:
- "Why does Mira keep asking questions?"
- "Does she not understand the answer?"
- "Or is she trying to understand it more deeply?"

**Step 2: Build a question chain** (12–15 minutes)
Pick a simple math fact that your students know. Examples:
- A basic fact: "2 + 3 = 5"
- A multiplication fact: "5 × 4 = 20"
- A division fact: "15 ÷ 3 = 5"
- A fraction: "1/2 of 8 = 4"

Put it on the board. Say: "We're going to build a chain of questions starting from this fact."

**Level 1 (The answer):** Write the math fact.

**Level 2 (First question):** Ask: "If 2 + 3 = 5, what's one more question we could ask?"

Students might say:
- "What does 2 + 4 equal?"
- "What does 3 + 3 equal?"
- "What does 2 + 2 equal?"

Write one of their suggestions and label it "Level 2."

**Level 3 (Second question):** Ask: "Now, based on what we just asked, what's ONE MORE question?"

This might be:
- "If 2 + 3 = 5 and 2 + 4 = 6, what's the pattern?"
- "Does adding 1 to one of the numbers always add 1 to the answer?"
- "What if I added 2 to both numbers? What would happen?"

Write this as "Level 3."

**Level 4 (Third question):** Continue one more level.

**Discussion:** Ask: "Look at our chain. We started with a simple fact: 2 + 3 = 5. But by asking one more question each time, where did we end up? Are we still thinking about the same fact, or are we thinking about something deeper?"

The goal is for students to see that by asking questions, they go from *memorizing facts* to *understanding patterns*.

**Step 3: Students build their own chains** (remaining time)
Give students an index card with a math fact on it (or let them pick one from a list). Working in pairs or small groups, they build a question chain:

1. **Write the fact** (given).
2. **Ask one more question** (write it).
3. **Ask one more question** based on that (write it).
4. **Ask one more question** based on that (write it).

Circulate and help students ask deeper questions. Good follow-up questions often start with:
- "What if...?"
- "Why does...?"
- "How is this similar to...?"
- "What would happen if...?"

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask:
- "What was the easiest level of questions to come up with?"
- "What was hardest?"
- "Did any of your questions surprise you—questions you didn't expect to wonder about?"
- "Do mathematicians ask questions like this?"

Key insight: **The deepest thinking in math comes from asking one more question.**

### Assessment
Collect the question chains. Look for:
- **Depth of questioning**: Do the questions get progressively more sophisticated?
- **Genuine inquiry**: Are the questions real wonderings, or are they obvious next steps?
- **Evidence of thinking**: Can you see the student's reasoning in the progression?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide the first two levels of the chain, and have them generate Level 3 and 4.
- Offer question stems: "What if we added a different number?" "What if we used subtraction instead?" "How is this like another fact we know?"
- Work with them one-on-one or in a small group.

**For students ready for more:**
- Challenge them to build a longer chain (5 or 6 levels).
- Have them try branching questions: At each level, generate two possible questions instead of one.
- Ask them to look for patterns in their questions: "Do your questions always ask about bigger numbers? About operations? About relationships?"

## Day 2: Explain It to Me

### Objective
Students will deepen their understanding of a math concept by explaining it in their own words, teaching it to someone younger or less experienced.

### Materials
- "AI Said / I Say" two-column worksheet (see below)
- Are-Self (accessible)
- List of math concepts (see suggestions below)
- Paper for writing explanations
- Optional: videos or textbook excerpts explaining the concept

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "If I asked you to explain how to add fractions to your little brother or sister, could you? Or would you just say, 'You find the common denominator'? Probably they wouldn't understand. The best way to really understand something is to explain it in words a younger kid would get."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Students pick a math concept** (2–3 minutes)
Show a list of math concepts your students are currently learning (or have recently learned):

- **Operations:** Multiplication, division, fractions, adding with regrouping, place value
- **Geometry:** Angles, perimeter, area, symmetry
- **Measurement:** Converting units, measuring length or weight
- **Data:** Bar graphs, picture graphs, interpreting data
- **Word problems:** Solving multi-step problems, estimating

Say: "Pick one concept that you've learned but that still feels a little confusing—or that you'd like to understand better."

**Step 2: Ask Are-Self to explain** (5–7 minutes)
Work as a class or in small groups. Here's the process:

1. **A student says the concept:** "I want to understand how to multiply two-digit numbers."

2. **Type the request to Are-Self:**
   - "I'm learning about [concept]. Can you explain it in a way that a 4th grader would understand? Use examples. Don't use complicated words."

3. **Read Are-Self's explanation aloud.** As you read, pause and ask:
   - "Does that make sense?"
   - "Did it use words we understand?"
   - "Did the examples help?"
   - "Is there a part you still don't get?"

**Step 3: Rewrite in your own words** (10–15 minutes)
Say: "Now I want you to rewrite what Are-Self said, but in *your* words. Pretend you're explaining this to a younger kid. Use examples from your own life, if you can. Make it simple."

Give students the "AI Said / I Say" worksheet:

```
CONCEPT: _________________

AI SAID:
[Paste or summarize Are-Self's explanation here]

I SAY:
[Rewrite the explanation in your own words]


My example: 
[Create a simple example that helps explain the concept]

One thing I still don't understand:
[If there's something confusing, write it here—we'll ask Are-Self a follow-up]
```

Students work independently or in pairs. Circulate and ask:
- "Can you say that in an even simpler way?"
- "What's an example from your life that shows this concept?"
- "If your little brother asked 'Why?' would you be able to answer?"

**Step 4: Share and discuss** (remaining time)
Ask for volunteers to read their "I Say" explanations aloud. After each one, ask the class: "Did that make sense? Could you teach this to someone else based on what they said?"

Celebrate explanations that are simple, clear, and use good examples.

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask:
- "What was different between how Are-Self explained it and how you explained it?"
- "Did writing it in your own words help you understand it better?"
- "Is there a part of the concept that's still fuzzy?"
- "What would make the explanation even clearer?"

Key insight: **Understanding isn't just knowing the steps. It's being able to explain why the steps work.**

### Assessment
Collect the "AI Said / I Say" worksheets. Look for:
- **Clarity**: Is their explanation understandable to someone unfamiliar with the concept?
- **Simplification**: Did they make the concept simpler without losing accuracy?
- **Evidence of understanding**: Do they use correct examples? Do they explain the *why*, not just the *how*?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a partially completed worksheet with part of Are-Self's explanation already written.
- Offer sentence stems: "This works because _____." "You do it like this: _____." "An example is _____."
- Have them dictate their explanation to you or a peer, and you write it down. Then they can edit.

**For students ready for more:**
- Ask them to compare Are-Self's explanation to the textbook's explanation: "What's the same? What's different? Which one is clearer? Why?"
- Have them create a visual explanation (diagram, comic strip, video script) in addition to the written one.
- Challenge them to explain two connected concepts: "How is multiplication like repeated addition? How are they different?"

## Day 3: Problem Creator

### Objective
Students will learn that clear mathematical communication is essential by writing problems, having Are-Self solve them, and analyzing whether Are-Self understood their intent.

### Materials
- Are-Self
- Math problems students are currently learning to solve (for reference/models)
- "Problem Creation" template (see below)
- Paper for writing problems
- "Error Analysis" worksheet (see below)

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "Today, you're going to be the problem-writer instead of the problem-solver. You'll write a math problem. Then Are-Self will try to solve it. If Are-Self gets it right, great! But if it gets it wrong, your job is to figure out: Did Are-Self make a math mistake, or did your problem not make sense?"

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Model the process** (5 minutes)
Write a simple word problem on the board. Say: "I wrote this problem. Let me ask Are-Self to solve it."

Type the problem to Are-Self and read the response. If Are-Self solves it correctly, say: "Yes! Are-Self understood what I was asking."

Then write a second problem, intentionally unclear or ambiguous:
- **Example (unclear):** "Some apples were in a bowl. Then something happened. How many apples are there now?"

Ask Are-Self to solve this one. It will probably say something like "I don't have enough information" or give a confused answer.

Say: "See? When my problem wasn't clear, Are-Self couldn't solve it. That's because I didn't give enough information. To be a good problem-writer, I need to be specific."

**Step 2: Students write their first problem** (8–10 minutes)
Say: "Write a word problem based on math you're learning right now. Here are some tips:
- Use a real situation (going to the store, sharing snacks, playing a game).
- Give all the information someone needs to solve it.
- Have a clear question at the end.
- Check it: Can *you* solve it?"

Provide this template:

```
MY PROBLEM:
[Write your word problem here]

THE ANSWER I GOT: ___________

MY WORK:
[Show your thinking]

MY CHECK:
Does my problem make sense? 
[ ] Yes [ ] Kind of [ ] No

Is there any information missing?
[ ] No [ ] Yes—I should add:

Is the question clear?
[ ] Yes [ ] No
```

Students work. Circulate and ask:
- "Can I understand what's happening in your problem?"
- "Do I know all the numbers I need?"
- "Is it clear what you're asking me to find?"

**Step 3: Give problems to Are-Self** (8–10 minutes)
This works best as a class activity with Are-Self visible on screen. Here's how:

1. Ask a volunteer to read their problem aloud.
2. **Type it to Are-Self:** "Here's a word problem a student wrote: [problem]. Can you solve it? Show your work."
3. **Read Are-Self's response.** Ask the student:
   - "Did Are-Self solve it correctly?"
   - "Did Are-Self understand what you were asking?"
   - If wrong: "Did Are-Self make a math mistake, or was your problem confusing?"

**Step 4: Error analysis (if needed)** (remaining time)
If Are-Self got it wrong, use this worksheet:

```
MY PROBLEM: [paste problem]

ARE-SELF'S ANSWER: ___________

THE CORRECT ANSWER: ___________

WHY DID ARE-SELF GET IT WRONG?
[ ] The problem was confusing (Are-Self didn't understand what I was asking)
[ ] The problem was missing information
[ ] Are-Self made a math mistake
[ ] Other: ___________

HOW I WOULD FIX THE PROBLEM:
[Rewrite it more clearly or with more information]
```

Have the student reflect: "What made the problem unclear? How would a better problem-writer fix this?"

### Reflection (10 minutes)
Ask:
- "What's harder: solving a problem or writing one?"
- "What makes a good word problem?"
- "What information do you always need in a word problem?"
- "Did any problems fool Are-Self in an interesting way?"

Key insight: **Clear communication in math is as important as getting the right answer.**

### Assessment
Collect:
- The original problem and work
- Are-Self's response
- The error analysis (if applicable)

Look for:
- **Clarity of writing**: Is the problem clearly stated?
- **Mathematical accuracy**: Is the answer correct?
- **Critical thinking**: If Are-Self got it wrong, can the student analyze why?
- **Communication skills**: Can the student explain what makes a good problem?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a problem template: "There are _____ [objects]. Then _____. How many _____?" Have them fill in the blanks.
- Give them 2–3 numbers and have them write a problem using all of them.
- Start with simpler problems (single-operation, fewer numbers).

**For students ready for more:**
- Challenge them to write a multi-step problem (requires two operations to solve).
- Have them write a problem that has extra information (information that looks important but isn't needed).
- Ask them to write two versions of the same problem: one that's clear and one that's confusing. Then have a classmate try to solve both.

## Day 4: Data Detectives

### Objective
Students will collect real data, use Are-Self to help analyze it, and practice asking "one more question" to deepen their understanding of what data shows and what it doesn't.

### Materials
- Data collection tools (survey templates, measurement tools, observation sheets)
- Are-Self
- Graphing paper or digital graphing tools (Sheets, Numbers, etc.)
- Chart paper for displaying graphs
- "One More Question" reflection sheet (see below)

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "I want to know something about your class. Let's say I want to know: 'How many siblings do you have?' I could ask everyone, and then make a graph. But once I have that graph, the real thinking starts. I don't just look at the graph and say, 'Cool.' I ask one more question: 'Why are some numbers bigger? What does this tell us?'"

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Collect class data** (5–7 minutes)
Choose something to measure about your class. Options:
- **Shoe size:** "What size shoe do you wear?"
- **Favorite color:** "What's your favorite color?"
- **Siblings:** "How many siblings do you have?"
- **Pets:** "Do you have a pet? What kind?"
- **Hobbies:** "Do you prefer reading, sports, art, or gaming?"
- **Bedtime:** "What time do you usually go to bed?"
- **School lunch vs. packed lunch**
- **Distance from school:** "How many minutes does it take you to get to school?"

Go around the room quickly and collect responses. Write them on the board or in a spreadsheet.

Say: "We have data! It's just a list of answers right now. But we're going to make it useful by creating a graph."

**Step 2: Make a graph** (8–10 minutes)
Create a simple bar graph or picture graph as a class. If you have digital tools, use them. If not, use chart paper and markers.

Once the graph is complete, ask:
- "What does this graph show?"
- "Which category has the most?"
- "Which has the least?"
- "Is anything surprising?"

**Step 3: Ask Are-Self to help analyze** (7–10 minutes)
Say: "Now we have a graph. Let me ask Are-Self to help us ask one more question."

Type to Are-Self:
- "Here's data from my class: [describe the data]. We made a graph showing [what the graph shows]. Now I want to understand this data better. What patterns do you see? What's surprising? What would you want to know more about?"

Read Are-Self's response. Discuss: "Do you agree? Did Are-Self spot something you didn't notice?"

**Step 4: Ask one more question** (remaining time)
Say: "Are-Self answered our first question. Now we ask one more question. Let's think about what else we want to know."

Examples:
- If the data was "favorite hobbies": "Why do you think so many kids like sports? What's the difference between kids who like reading and kids who like gaming?"
- If the data was "siblings": "Does the number of siblings affect anything else, like whether you have a pet, or how long it takes to get to school?"
- If the data was "shoe size": "Is there a pattern between shoe size and something else about us?"

Ask Are-Self one of these follow-up questions. Then ask: "What would we need to measure to answer this question?"

This teaches students that data always raises new questions.

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Have students complete the "One More Question" sheet:

```
OUR DATA WAS ABOUT: ___________

WHAT THE GRAPH SHOWED:
___________________________

ONE QUESTION ARE-SELF HELPED US ASK:
___________________________

ONE MORE QUESTION WE THOUGHT OF:
___________________________

TO ANSWER THAT QUESTION, WE WOULD NEED TO:
[ ] Collect more data about ___________
[ ] Measure ___________
[ ] Ask someone to explain _____________
[ ] Something else: ___________

MY REFLECTION:
The most interesting thing about our data was __________.
```

### Assessment
Collect:
- The graph
- Are-Self's analysis (screenshot or printout)
- The "One More Question" reflection

Look for:
- **Data interpretation**: Can students read the graph and describe what it shows?
- **Critical thinking**: Do they ask questions that go beyond the surface?
- **Understanding of limitations**: Can they articulate what the data does and doesn't show?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Collect simpler data (yes/no questions, or 2–3 categories).
- Provide the graph pre-made, and focus only on interpretation.
- Offer question stems: "Why might _____?" "What if _____?" "What does this tell us about _____?"

**For students ready for more:**
- Have them collect data themselves (design their own survey question, ask classmates, record responses).
- Challenge them to make two graphs from the same data (different types of graphs) and compare: "Which graph tells the story better?"
- Ask them to make predictions: "Based on this data, I think if we asked a different class, we would find _____. Why?"

## Day 5: Math Journals

### Objective
Students will deepen their understanding of challenging concepts by writing about them using the "One More Question" technique, practicing the habit of inquiry to grow their mathematical thinking.

### Materials
- Math journals or notebooks
- Are-Self (optional—students may reference it, but this is primarily student thinking)
- "One More Question" journal prompt (see below)
- Pencils or keyboard

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)
Say: "This week, we've learned that every answer is just a door to another question. The habit of asking 'one more question' is how mathematicians think. Today, you're going to use your math journal to practice this habit. You'll pick something hard you learned this week, and you'll write about it using the 'one more question' technique."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

**Step 1: Identify the hardest concept** (3–5 minutes)
Say: "Think about this week's math. What was the hardest thing? Not the thing you got wrong. The thing that made you think hard. It could be:
- A concept that didn't make sense at first, but you figured it out.
- Something that felt confusing but now makes more sense.
- Something you still find tricky.
- Something that surprised you."

Students think and identify one concept.

**Step 2: Introduce the journal format** (2 minutes)
Say: "Your journal entry will have four parts:

1. **The concept:** Explain what you're thinking about.
2. **One more question:** Ask a question about it.
3. **My answer:** Try to answer your own question.
4. **One MORE question:** Ask another question, based on what you just answered.

Each part builds on the last. You're digging deeper with each question."

**Step 3: Model the process** (5 minutes)
Work through an example with the class. Say: "Let's say the hard concept this week was fractions. Here's how I'd fill in my journal:

**The concept:** I'm thinking about fractions. I learned that 1/2 of 8 is 4, and 1/3 of 12 is 4. But why are the answers the same if the fractions are different?

**One more question:** How does the size of the fraction relate to the size of the whole number?

**My answer:** If the fraction is bigger (like 1/2 instead of 1/3), then I'm dividing into fewer pieces, so each piece is bigger. So I get a bigger answer. But wait—in both problems, the answer was 4. That means a smaller fraction of a bigger number can equal a bigger fraction of a smaller number.

**One MORE question:** If 1/2 of 8 = 4, and 1/3 of 12 = 4, can I find other pairs of fractions and whole numbers that equal 4?

See? Each question takes us deeper. We started with confusion and ended with discovery."

**Step 4: Students write independently** (12–15 minutes)
Students open their journals and write using this format:

```
WHAT I'M THINKING ABOUT:
[Explain the hardest thing you learned this week]

ONE MORE QUESTION:
[Ask a question about it]

MY ANSWER:
[Try to answer your own question. Use examples if it helps.]

ONE MORE QUESTION (Based on what I just learned):
[Ask another question that came from thinking about your answer]

REFLECTION:
By asking one more question, I discovered that _________________.
```

Circulate and help students who are stuck:
- "What about this concept made you think hard?"
- "What would you want to understand better?"
- "If you answered that question, what would you want to know next?"

**Step 5: Share and celebrate** (remaining time)
Ask for volunteers to read one part of their journal aloud (maybe just the concept and first question). Celebrate the thinking: "Notice how _____ started with something confusing and ended up asking a really smart question."

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Ask students:
- "Did asking one more question help you understand the concept better?"
- "Did you discover anything new by writing in your journal?"
- "What would happen if you asked one more question to that last question?"
- "Is this something mathematicians do?"

Key insight: **The most important habit for a mathematician is curiosity. Asking one more question.**

### Assessment
Collect the journals. Look for:
- **Depth of thinking**: Does each question build on the last? Does the thinking get progressively more sophisticated?
- **Mathematical accuracy**: Is their explanation correct? Do their examples make sense?
- **Genuine inquiry**: Are these real wonderings, or just next-obvious-steps?
- **Growth**: Can you see their understanding deepening as they write?

### Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide sentence stems for each section:
  - "I'm thinking about _____ because _____."
  - "One question I have is: Why _____?"
  - "I think the answer is _____ because _____."
  - "Another question is: What if _____?"
- Focus on one question and answer, rather than multiple rounds.
- Allow them to draw a picture or diagram instead of words to show their thinking.

**For students ready for more:**
- Challenge them to extend the chain: Ask 3 or 4 successive "one more questions" instead of 2.
- Have them write about two connected concepts and explore the relationship between them: "How does _____ connect to _____?"
- Ask them to think about the *source* of their questions: "Why is this question worth asking? What would a mathematician want to know?"

### Wrap-Up: Week 3 Reflection

At the end of Day 5, gather students for a final reflection on the week:

**"This week, you learned Inquiry—the habit of asking one more question. You used questions to:
- Break open math facts and look for patterns.
- Understand concepts more deeply.
- Write clearer problems.
- Explore data.
- Keep your own thinking growing.

Tell me: What's one question you asked this week that taught you something new? How did asking that question change what you understood?"**

Record responses on a chart titled "Questions That Taught Us." Display it in your classroom.

**Key takeaways for students:**
- Every answer is the beginning, not the end.
- The best mathematicians ask one more question.
- Asking questions doesn't mean you're confused—it means you're thinking.
- One question leads to another, and that's how we understand deeply.
- "I wonder..." is the beginning of real learning.

## Connecting Week 3 to the Arc

As you teach this week, continually link back to Mira's journey and the scipraxian habits:

- **Week 1 (Perspective):** You learned to see from different angles.
- **Week 2 (Humility):** You learned to say "I don't know yet" with confidence.
- **Week 3 (Inquiry):** You learned to ask one more question—because every answer opens a door.

These three habits—perspective, humility, inquiry—work together. With perspective, you can see the problem from many angles. With humility, you admit what you don't know. With inquiry, you ask questions to keep learning. Together, they're powerful. They're how Mira fixes the toaster. They're how mathematicians discover new things. And they're how your students will become thoughtful, curious learners.
