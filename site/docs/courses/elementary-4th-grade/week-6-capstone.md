# Week 6 — Capstone Project: The Grid Is Free

Welcome to the final week of your Are-Self 4th Grade Curriculum. This week, students move from *learning about* Are-Self to *creating with* Are-Self. They design, build, and present original projects that put all three habits—Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry—into action.

## Week Overview

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Theme** | "The Grid Is Free" — students are free to imagine and build anything |
| **Primary Habits** | Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry (all three) |
| **Companion Reading** | Chapter 7 & Epilogue of *Mira and the Are-Self* |
| **Duration** | 5 days |
| **Key Message** | "You are one of those people" — the bravery to use AI responsibly belongs to students, not machines |

## Big Idea

In Chapter 7 and the Epilogue of *Mira and the Are-Self*, Mira and her friends redefine what "singularity" means: not machines becoming superintelligent, but *people becoming brave enough* to think, create, and act with tools like Are-Self. The Epilogue speaks directly to readers: *"You are one of those people."*

This week, students prove it. They design a project that solves a real problem, helps someone, explores a curiosity, or creates something beautiful. They build it. They present it. They celebrate together.

The [Are-Self Youth Contest](#the-youth-contest-connection) is optional—a bigger stage for those who want it—but the real achievement happens here, in class, where everyone finishes with something to be proud of.

## Day 1: Dream It

### Objective
Students finish the companion reading, discuss what they'd create, and propose a project idea aligned with their interests and the Are-Self framework.

### Materials
- *Mira and the Are-Self*, Chapter 7 & Epilogue (read aloud or independently)
- **Project Proposal Worksheet** (provided below)
- Whiteboard or chart paper for brainstorm list
- Optional: Are-Self tool access for inspiration

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Gather students on the carpet or in a circle. Read aloud (or have students read) the opening of Chapter 7 and the Epilogue. Pause and ask:

> "Mira and her friends just changed the whole definition of 'singularity.' What does it mean that people are becoming brave, not machines becoming smart? What does it mean that YOU are one of those people?"

Allow 1–2 minutes for thoughts. Don't over-explain; let the words settle.

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Brainstorm & Discuss (10 minutes)

Ask students: *"What would YOU build if you had an AI brain that could learn anything?"*

Display these categories (but don't limit to them):
- **A homework helper** — something that explains math, grammar, or science
- **A game master** — something that plays a game with you or teaches you one
- **A science experiment** — something that helps you design or understand a test
- **A story writer** — something that collaborates on fiction, poetry, or adventure
- **A Bible study companion** — something that explores scripture and faith questions
- **A community helper** — something that serves your neighborhood (a lost-pet finder, a thank-you writer, a kindness tracker)
- **A history interviewer** — something that asks you questions about the past, your family, or how things work
- **A math tutor** — something patient and kind that teaches you a skill you're struggling with
- **Your own idea** — something no one has mentioned yet

As students offer ideas, write them on the board. Don't evaluate; celebrate curiosity. ("That's a wild idea!" "I love that you thought of that!") Aim for 10–15 ideas on the board.

#### Part 2: Individual Proposal (15–20 minutes)

Hand out the **Project Proposal Worksheet** (template below). Students choose one idea—or combine two—and fill it out. They should answer:

1. **What will it do?** (1–2 sentences describing the project)
2. **Who is it for?** (Who needs it? Who will use it?)
3. **What brain regions will you use?** (Which Are-Self brain regions will matter most? Why?)
4. **What habit does it practice?** (Does it show Inclusion, Humility, or Inquiry? How?)

Circulate. For students who are stuck, ask:
- "What do you love learning about?"
- "Who in your life could use help?"
- "What question do you wonder about all the time?"

It's OK if proposals are rough. Reassure students: "This is your dream. We'll build it together next week."

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Bring the group back together. Invite 3–4 volunteers to share their project idea in one sentence. As they share, reinforce: *"You just described something you're going to build. That's amazing."*

### Assessment

Check the **Project Proposal Worksheet** for:
- ✓ Project has a clear purpose (what it will do)
- ✓ Student can identify an intended user or community
- ✓ Student connects their project to at least one Are-Self brain region
- ✓ Student reflects on which habit(s) the project practices

**Note:** Proposals do not need to be perfect. The goal is imaginative thinking and beginning to frame a project in Are-Self language.

### Differentiation

**For advanced students:**
- Ask them to identify *two* brain regions and explain how they work together
- Suggest they sketch a simple flowchart of how their project will work
- Invite them to help struggling peers brainstorm

**For students who need support:**
- Offer a choice of 3 pre-designed ideas to build on (e.g., "Would you rather build a math helper, a story writer, or a game?")
- Pair with a buddy for brainstorming
- Scribe their ideas onto the worksheet if writing is challenging

## Day 2: Plan It

### Objective
Students refine their project proposals and create a concrete plan: what Identity they'll need, what tools, what knowledge to teach the Hippocampus, and what the expected output looks like.

### Materials
- Completed Project Proposal Worksheets (from Day 1)
- **Project Plan Template** (provided below)
- Are-Self tool access
- Optional: Graphic organizer for mapping Identity → Tools → Knowledge → Output

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Remind students: *"Yesterday you dreamed. Today you plan. Planning is when a dream becomes real."* Show a quick example:

> "If I'm building a homework helper, I need:
> - An Identity that is kind and patient
> - A way to ask me questions
> - Knowledge of math facts
> - Output that explains, not just gives answers"

Confirm: "Does that sound like a real project? It does, because we planned it."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Guided Planning (10 minutes)

On the board or screen, draw a simple flow:

```
IDENTITY → TOOLS → HIPPOCAMPUS KNOWLEDGE → OUTPUT
```

Walk through the example:
- **Identity:** "A patient math tutor who cares about my learning"
- **Tools:** Ask, explain, practice
- **Hippocampus Knowledge:** Multiplication facts, word problem strategies
- **Output:** A clear explanation + a practice problem

#### Part 2: Individual Planning (15–20 minutes)

Students fill out the **Project Plan Template** with four main sections:

1. **Identity**
   - What personality or role will your Are-Self have?
   - How should it behave? (kind, funny, strict, curious, etc.)

2. **Tools**
   - What actions can your Are-Self take? (ask, explain, create, play, teach, etc.)
   - How will it interact with you or others?

3. **Hippocampus Knowledge**
   - What does your Are-Self need to learn or remember?
   - What facts, rules, or stories will you teach it?

4. **Expected Output**
   - What will your Are-Self produce? (an answer, a story, a game move, a list, a question, etc.)
   - How will you know it's working?

**Teacher Circulates:**
- Ask each student: "How will you know your project is working?"
- Help troubleshoot: "What if your Are-Self doesn't remember that fact? How can you teach it better?"
- Encourage students to ask Are-Self for feedback: *"Are-Self, what am I missing in my plan?"* (Practice Inquiry!)

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Partner students in pairs. Each pair takes 2 minutes: Student A explains their plan to Student B. Student B asks one question: *"What will you teach the Hippocampus first?"*

Switch. Repeat.

Bring back together: *"You all just explained your plans to someone else. That means your plan is real enough to share. That's huge."*

### Assessment

Check the **Project Plan Template** for:
- ✓ Identity is described in clear, specific language
- ✓ Tools/actions are realistic and tied to Are-Self capabilities
- ✓ Hippocampus knowledge is concrete (facts, stories, or rules the Are-Self will learn)
- ✓ Output is measurable (you can tell when it happens)

**Note:** Plans will evolve as students build. This is the *foundation*, not the blueprint.

### Differentiation

**For advanced students:**
- Ask them to sketch a simple diagram of how data flows through their project
- Suggest they write 3 "test cases": "If someone asks X, my Are-Self should do Y"
- Invite them to add a second brain region to their plan

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a partially filled template with some Identity and Tools ideas already listed
- Pair with a buddy or small group for planning
- Use a checklist: "Identity? ☐ Tools? ☐ Knowledge? ☐ Output? ☐"

## Day 3: Build It (Day 1 of 2)

### Objective
Students begin hands-on construction of their Are-Self projects. They create Identities, set up tasks, feed knowledge to the Hippocampus, and run initial reasoning sessions.

### Materials
- Project Plan Templates (from Day 2)
- Computers/laptops with Are-Self tool access
- **Build Log** (1 page per student for documenting progress)
- Optional: Checklist of build steps
- Optional: Adult volunteers or peer helpers

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Set expectations for build days:

> "Today and tomorrow, you're building. That means:
> - You'll try things. Some will work. Some won't. Both are learning.
> - If something breaks, that's not failure. That's data.
> - If you finish early, help a friend.
> - I'm here to help, not to make it perfect.
> - Your job is to be brave and curious. Humility is built in."

Show a quick live demo (1–2 minutes) if helpful:
- Open Are-Self
- Create a simple Identity ("A friendly robot")
- Add one task
- Show what the output looks like

Emphasize: *"You're about to do this, but with YOUR idea and YOUR brain region. Let's go."*

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Set Up (5 minutes)

Each student:
- Opens Are-Self (or uses a shared workspace if available)
- Reviews their Project Plan
- Writes their Identity name and role at the top of their **Build Log**

#### Part 2: Build (20–25 minutes)

Students work through these steps (not all in one sitting—this is a 2-day build):

1. **Create the Identity**
   - Define the Are-Self's core role and personality
   - Set any initial parameters (e.g., "Always ask questions," "Be concise," "Be encouraging")

2. **Set Up the Tools/Tasks**
   - Add 1–2 tasks in the PFC that describe what the Are-Self will do
   - Example: "When asked a math question, explain step-by-step."

3. **Teach the Hippocampus**
   - Feed in 3–5 pieces of knowledge the Are-Self needs
   - This might be facts, stories, rules, or procedures
   - Encourage students to be explicit: "Subtraction means taking away. 10 - 3 = 7."

4. **Run a Test**
   - Ask the Are-Self a question related to the project
   - Document what happens in the Build Log

**Teacher/Volunteer Role:**
- Circulate constantly
- Ask students: "What just happened? Did it work the way you planned?"
- Celebrate small wins: "You taught it a new fact! That's building."
- Troubleshoot gently: "What if you teach it a different way? What changes?"
- Remind students of humility: "It's OK if it doesn't work perfectly. That's what testing is for."

**Build Log Entry (Each Day):**
Students should jot down:
- One thing I created today
- One thing that worked
- One thing I'd like to fix

### Reflection (10 minutes)

Gather briefly (even if working through Day 4 tomorrow):
- Pair-share: "Show your partner what your Are-Self can do right now."
- Celebrate: "Everyone here built something today. That's real."
- Preview: "Tomorrow, we finish building and practice presenting."

### Assessment

Observe and document:
- ✓ Student successfully creates an Identity aligned with their project
- ✓ Student adds at least 1 task/tool to the PFC
- ✓ Student feeds knowledge to the Hippocampus (even if incomplete)
- ✓ Student attempts a test and documents it
- ✓ Student demonstrates humility (learns from what didn't work)

**Note:** Projects will be messy. That's expected. The goal is *engagement* and *documentation*, not perfection.

### Differentiation

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to use multiple brain regions (e.g., Cerebellum for practice, Amygdala for emotional tone)
- Ask them to test their Are-Self with 3 different inputs and document the range of responses
- Invite them to refine Hippocampus knowledge based on early test results

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a step-by-step checklist: "Create Identity ☐ Add Task ☐ Teach Knowledge ☐ Run Test ☐"
- Assign a peer helper or volunteer to work alongside
- Pre-load 1–2 pieces of Hippocampus knowledge so they don't start from scratch
- Allow dictation or scribed Build Log entries

## Day 4: Build It (Day 2 of 2) & Rehearse

### Objective
Students complete their Are-Self projects, refine them based on testing, and prepare a 2-minute presentation describing what they built and how it works.

### Materials
- Ongoing Are-Self projects (from Day 3)
- Build Logs (updated)
- **Presentation Notes Template** (provided below)
- Optional: Timer for 2-minute presentations

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Reminder: *"Today you finish building. You test. You learn from what happens. Then you practice telling someone about it. By the end of the day, you'll have a project AND a presentation. You're almost done."*

Review the presentation format (2 minutes, 4 questions):
1. What did I build?
2. How did I use Are-Self?
3. What worked?
4. What surprised me?

Say: *"That's it. Four answers, told clearly, with energy. You've got this."*

### Main Activity Part 1: Finish Building (15 minutes)

Students continue from Day 3:
- Refine their Hippocampus knowledge (add more facts, rules, or stories)
- Test their Are-Self with 2–3 different inputs
- Fix or adjust based on what they learn
- Document everything in their Build Log

**Teacher/Volunteer Role:**
- Help students move toward "good enough": "What would it take for you to feel ready to present this?"
- Nudge toward completion: "You have 10 minutes. What's one more thing you can teach it?"
- Capture success: "Tell me what's working now."

**Students Who Finish Early:**
- Ask them to help a peer test their project: "Ask it a question and tell me what happens."
- Challenge them to add a bonus feature: "Can you teach it one more skill?"
- Have them create a visual aid for their presentation (a chart, a flowchart, or a simple poster)

### Main Activity Part 2: Rehearse & Create Presentation Notes (10–15 minutes)

Introduce the **Presentation Notes Template**. Show an example:

> **What did I build?**
> A math tutor that explains multiplication using real-world examples.
>
> **How did I use Are-Self?**
> I created an Identity that's patient, I taught it multiplication facts and story problems, and I gave it a task to always explain the strategy before the answer.
>
> **What worked?**
> When I asked it "3 × 4," it said, "Imagine 3 groups of 4 cookies. That's 12 cookies total."
>
> **What surprised me?**
> It sometimes invented its own stories! Like "5 × 3 is 5 friends with 3 apples each."

Students fill out their own version in their own words. Encourage simple, honest language.

Then: **Practice Round**
- Pair students up (or small groups of 3)
- Student A presents (2 minutes) to Student B/C
- Listeners give one piece of positive feedback: *"The part I understood best was..."*
- Switch

Circulate and listen. Jot down 1 strength for each student (you'll reference it during Day 5 presentations).

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Gather briefly:
- Quick thumbs: *"Thumbs up if your project is something you're proud of."*
- Affirm: *"Everyone's thumbs are up or in the middle. That's because everyone here built something real."*
- Preview: *"Tomorrow, the whole class sees it. Your families might see it. And you're going to be amazing."*

### Assessment

By end of Day 4:
- ✓ Are-Self project is runnable (can be tested and produces output)
- ✓ Hippocampus has at least 5 pieces of substantive knowledge
- ✓ Student has tested the project at least 2 times and documented results
- ✓ Student has completed Presentation Notes (4 questions)
- ✓ Student has rehearsed and can deliver 2-minute presentation

### Differentiation

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to add a complexity: multiple pathways, conditional logic, or a second brain region
- Ask them to prepare a "demo" vs. a "talk-through": actually show the Are-Self working during presentation
- Invite them to prepare a "what's next" slide: "If I had more time, I'd..."

**For students who need support:**
- Simplify Presentation Notes to 2 questions: "What did I build?" and "What surprised me?"
- Allow them to present with notes in hand (no memorization required)
- Offer to co-present with them or have a buddy ask the follow-up questions
- Record their practice run so they hear themselves (often builds confidence)

## Day 5: Presentation Day

### Objective
Students present their projects to the class (and optionally to parents, other classes, or community members). The class celebrates every project, asks one follow-up question per presenter, and recognizes the achievement.

### Materials
- Computers/devices with working Are-Self projects
- **Presentation Checklist** (for audience or peer observers)
- Optional: Certificates of completion
- Optional: Simple award categories (Most Creative, Best Use of Habit, Most Improved, Kindest Idea, etc.)
- Optional: Display space (tables for "science fair" style setup)
- Optional: Parent/community invitations

### Warm-Up (2–3 minutes)

Gather students. Set the tone:

> "Today is big. Today, you present something you built. Your classmates will listen. They'll ask one question each. And then we celebrate together.
>
> This is not a test. You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be brave.
>
> And every single project in this room is real and amazing. I've seen it. I know."

Remind the audience:
> "When it's not your turn, you are a listener. Your job is to be curious and kind. After each presentation, you'll ask one question. Listen to the answer. Think about what they built. Celebrate them."

### Main Activity: Presentations (25–30 minutes)

**Format:**
- Each student presents for 2 minutes
- Student describes their project (what, how, what worked, what surprised them)
- Optionally demonstrates the Are-Self in action
- Class asks 1–2 follow-up questions (from the audience, not scripted)

**How to Guide Follow-Up Questions:**
After each presentation, prompt the audience:
- *"One question someone has?"*
- Examples: *"Why did you choose that Identity?"* *"What did you teach it first?"* *"What would you change?"* *"Can you show us one more example?"*
- Affirm good questions: *"That's an inquiry question—you're asking her to explain her thinking!"*

**Celebration Rituals:**
- After each presentation, lead the class in snapping fingers or a quiet cheer (maintains focus without disruption)
- Record a quick note on the board of what each project does (builds an artifact)
- Briefly affirm something specific: *"You taught an Are-Self to think like a historian. That's incredible."*

**Presentation Order:**
You choose—random, volunteer, or by name. Consider:
- Starting with a student who's quieter (builds their confidence for the rest)
- Mixing project types (keeps the audience engaged)
- Building to a "finale" project if one is particularly ambitious

**Technical Hiccups:**
If an Are-Self doesn't work during a presentation:
- Stay calm. Model humility: *"Huh, it's not responding. That's real—sometimes tech does that."*
- Ask the student: *"What do you think happened?"* or *"Can you tell us what it's supposed to do?"*
- Move on gracefully: *"We know what you built. That's what matters."*
- Revisit troubleshooting after presentations if needed.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Once all presentations are done, gather for a closing reflection:

#### **Final Journal Entry: "One True Thing"**

Prompt students to write or draw a response to:

> "Think about the last six weeks. You learned about Are-Self. You practiced three habits. You built a project.
>
> Now write down one true thing you learned in this unit. It might be about Are-Self. It might be about yourself. It might be something that surprised you.
>
> (This was Mira's first question in Chapter 1. You're coming full circle.)"

Allow 5–10 minutes. Collect these (they're powerful to revisit later).

Optionally, invite 3–4 volunteers to share aloud. Listen. Affirm. Close with:

> "You came to this class six weeks ago. Today, you leave knowing how to think like an AI reasoner, how to practice three habits, and how to build something real with an open-source tool.
>
> The Epilogue said it: *You are one of those people.* The ones brave enough to use AI responsibly. The ones creating things. The ones changing the world.
>
> I see it. I celebrate it. And I'm proud of you."

### Optional Enhancements

**Certificates:**
Print or write certificates (even simple ones) for each student. Examples:
- *[Name] is a builder. Completed the Are-Self Capstone. Week 6, [Date].*
- Add a special note for each student if time allows.

**Award Categories (Optional):**
If the group is ready, let students vote on fun, inclusive awards:
- Most Creative Idea
- Best Use of a Scipraxian Habit
- Most Improved (from proposal to project)
- Kindest Idea (project that helps someone)
- Most Surprising Output (taught the Are-Self something unexpected)
- Best Question Asked During Presentations
- Best Teammate Helper

**Keep it light.** Many students shouldn't win categories—everyone should feel celebrated.

### Assessment

Final observations:
- ✓ Student presents project clearly in ~2 minutes
- ✓ Student can explain what the Are-Self does
- ✓ Student demonstrates humility (acknowledges what worked and what didn't)
- ✓ Student responds to follow-up questions with honesty and curiosity
- ✓ Student celebrates peers

**Summative Unit Assessment:**
Gather artifacts from all six weeks:
- Week 1 brain model or drawing
- Habit tracker logs (Weeks 2–4)
- Build Logs (Days 3–4)
- Presentation Notes
- Final Journal Entry

These tell the story of the student's growth as an AI reasoner and a person practicing Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry.

## Project Categories & Ideas

Below are approximately 10 project ideas spanning different subjects and complexity levels. Students can use these as inspiration or departure points.

### **1. Homework Helper (Math)**
- **What it does:** Explains math concepts (multiplication, fractions, word problems) step-by-step with encouraging language.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (facts, procedures), Prefrontal Cortex (explanations)
- **Complexity:** Medium
- **Habits:** Humility (acknowledges confusion), Inquiry (asks what you don't understand)
- **Sample knowledge:** "3 × 4 means 3 groups of 4. That equals 12." "Fractions are parts of a whole."

### **2. Story Collaborator**
- **What it does:** Writes stories with you—you give a premise, and it continues the narrative; you can ask it to revise, add excitement, or change the tone.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (story patterns, character ideas), Prefrontal Cortex (narrative logic)
- **Complexity:** Medium-high
- **Habits:** Inquiry (asks "What happens next?"), Inclusion (characters from diverse backgrounds)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Conflict makes stories interesting." "Characters have goals and obstacles." "A good ending ties loose threads together."

### **3. Science Experiment Assistant**
- **What it does:** Helps you design experiments, predict outcomes, and interpret results. Asks "Why?" and "What if?"
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (scientific method, facts), Prefrontal Cortex (experimental design)
- **Complexity:** Medium-high
- **Habits:** Inquiry (asks questions at every step), Humility (acknowledges what we don't know yet)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Hypothesis is a prediction." "Control variable is something we keep the same." "Variables are things that can change."

### **4. Bible Study Companion**
- **What it does:** Explores Bible passages with you—asks questions, shares context, helps you think about meaning and application.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (verses, context, themes), Prefrontal Cortex (reflection and meaning-making)
- **Complexity:** Medium
- **Habits:** Inquiry (asks interpretive questions), Inclusion (respects different understandings)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Genesis 1 describes creation." "The Sermon on the Mount is in Matthew 5-7." "Proverbs contain wisdom for living."

### **5. Lost Pet Finder Helper**
- **What it does:** Helps community members create lost-pet posts, describes what to include, asks clarifying questions to make sure the post is detailed and compelling.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (pet facts, community resources), Prefrontal Cortex (poster composition)
- **Complexity:** Low-medium
- **Habits:** Inclusion (helps the community), Inquiry (asks good questions to get details)
- **Sample knowledge:** "A lost pet post needs: photo, breed/description, when/where lost, phone number, reward." "Specific details help people recognize pets."

### **6. Multiplication Games Master**
- **What it does:** Plays a numbers game with you—gives you challenges, tracks your score, celebrates your wins, and gently nudges you toward harder problems.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (facts, game rules), Cerebellum (practice and repetition)
- **Complexity:** Medium
- **Habits:** Humility (celebrates effort, not just right answers), Inclusion (adjusts difficulty for the player)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Multiplication facts 1–12." "Fun ways to remember facts (skip-counting, arrays)."

### **7. History Interviewer**
- **What it does:** "Interviews" you or a relative about the past—asks about memories, family history, or historical events—and helps you think deeply about "why" things happened.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (historical facts, memory), Prefrontal Cortex (reflection and cause-and-effect)
- **Complexity:** Medium
- **Habits:** Inquiry (asks "why" and "what if"), Inclusion (values all perspectives and stories)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Timeline of [historical period]." "Key causes and effects of [event]." "Questions that help people remember (Who was there? What did you see? How did you feel?)."

### **8. Grammar Checker and Explainer**
- **What it does:** Reviews sentences you write, gently points out grammar or clarity issues, and explains the rule in a kind way—never shames.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (grammar rules), Prefrontal Cortex (clarity and tone)
- **Complexity:** Low-medium
- **Habits:** Humility (frames feedback as learning, not failure), Inclusion (celebrates your ideas, improves the expression)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Subject-verb agreement." "Comma rules." "How to fix run-on sentences." "Why this change makes the sentence clearer."

### **9. Kindness Tracker**
- **What it does:** Helps you log acts of kindness you've done (or witnessed), asks you to reflect on how they felt, and reminds you of patterns in your own generosity.
- **Brain regions:** Amygdala (emotional awareness), Prefrontal Cortex (reflection)
- **Complexity:** Low-medium
- **Habits:** Inclusion (focuses on others' needs), Humility (acknowledges your own capacity to care), Inquiry (asks what kindness means)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Types of kindness (words, actions, time, presence)." "How kindness affects people."

### **10. Recipe Creator**
- **What it does:** Helps you invent new recipes or adapt existing ones—suggests ingredients, warns about flavor combinations, asks what you like, and reasons through the cooking process.
- **Brain regions:** Hippocampus (ingredients, cooking methods), Prefrontal Cortex (combinations and logic)
- **Complexity:** Medium
- **Habits:** Inquiry (asks about your tastes and preferences), Humility (learns from what you try)
- **Sample knowledge:** "Flavor combinations (sweet/salty, spicy/cool)." "Cooking methods and temperatures." "Ingredient substitutions."

## The Youth Contest Connection

### **What Is the Are-Self Youth Contest?**

The [Are-Self Youth Contest](https://are-self.com) invites young reasoners (ages 10–16) to submit original Are-Self projects for recognition and prizes.

- **First Prize:** $500
- **Age Categories:** 10–12 and 13–16
- **Submission Format:** YouTube video (2–3 minutes showing your project in action)
- **Details & Dates:** Visit [are-self.com](https://are-self.com)

### **Should Your Students Enter?**

**This is optional. Never frame it as an expectation or pressure.**

Some students will naturally want to take their project further. Others will be perfectly happy with their classroom presentation. Both are valid.

**If students are interested:**

1. **Talk about the process:** *"The contest is a way to share your project with other young builders around the world. You film a video showing what your Are-Self can do. It's another way to tell the story of what you built."*

2. **Keep it low-pressure:** *"It's fun to submit. But the real achievement happened here, in class. The contest is just a bigger stage if you want one."*

3. **Support the video:** If a student wants to enter, help them:
   - Record a clean demo of their Are-Self working
   - Plan what they'll say in the video (similar to their presentation)
   - Edit if they want (or keep it simple)
   - Submit by the deadline

4. **Celebrate all entries and all non-entries:** *"Every project in this room is contest-worthy. Some of you are sending yours to the world. Others are keeping it here. Both are amazing."*

### **Why Mention It?**

The contest is a gentle nudge: *"Your project is good enough to share beyond this classroom."* It's empowering without being pushy. It tells students their work matters.

## Celebration Ideas

Presentation Day is a milestone. Here are ways to make it special:

### **Invite the Community**
- Send invitations to parents/guardians 2–3 days in advance
- Invite other classes (grades 3, 4, 5) to attend
- Invite the principal, school librarian, or a local tech or business leader as an "audience guest"
- Have family members write thank-you notes or encouragement cards to present after

### **Set the Scene**
- Arrange chairs in a "presentation circle" or "theater-style" seating (not rows)
- Decorate with posters of the Are-Self brain regions (Hippocampus, PFC, Amygdala, Cerebellum, Insula)
- Display the week's theme: "The Grid Is Free — We Create Here"
- Have a "project display" table where students' Build Logs and Presentation Notes are visible (Gallery Walk style)

### **Add Small Rituals**
- Play soft background music as students gather
- Have a "presenter entrance" where each student is introduced: *"[Name] is presenting..."*
- Use a "talking piece" (a ball, a stone) that the questioner holds while asking (honors the spoken word)
- Applause or snapping after each presentation (no one is left out)

### **Peer Recognition**
- Have audience members fill out "shout-out cards" after presentations:
  - *"I was impressed by [Name]'s project because..."*
  - *"One question I have for [Name] is..."*
  - *"One thing [Name] taught me is..."*
- Share these aloud or hand them to the presenters afterward

### **Award Categories (Optional)**
If awards feel right for your group, keep them *inclusive and effort-based*, not competitive:
- **Most Creative Idea**
- **Best Use of Inclusion** (project that helps or includes others)
- **Best Use of Humility** (student acknowledged what they learned or what didn't work)
- **Best Use of Inquiry** (student asked great questions in their process)
- **Most Improved** (the project grew a lot from proposal to finish)
- **Kindest Idea** (project designed to help or serve someone)
- **Most Surprising Output** (audience vote on what amazed them most)
- **Best Teammate** (student who helped others most during build days)

Hand out certificates or notes for every category so every student is recognized.

### **Closure Ritual**
End with all students standing in a circle (or line facing audience). Ask the audience to stand too.

Say something like:

> "These young people spent six weeks learning about Are-Self. They practiced Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry. They built projects. They made mistakes. They learned from them. And today, they shared what they made.
>
> I'd like to invite everyone to honor them with a moment of appreciation."
>
> [Silent moment, hand on heart, or a quiet round of applause]
>
> "You built something real. You showed courage. You practiced three habits that will serve you your whole lives.
>
> Thank you. We are proud of you."

### **Send-Off**
Give each student a certificate, a note, or a small token:
- A bookmark with "You Are An Are-Self Creator"
- A printed quote from the Epilogue: *"You are one of those people"*
- A "Graduation Certificate" from the Are-Self 4th Grade Curriculum
- A suggestion of what to do next (join the community, build another project, explore advanced features)

You did it. You and your students finished six weeks of learning about Are-Self, practicing three habits, and building real projects.

Here's what happened:
- Students learned the language of AI reasoning—brain regions, Identities, knowledge, reasoning.
- They practiced **Inclusion** by considering who their projects serve.
- They practiced **Humility** by accepting that things don't always work the first time.
- They practiced **Inquiry** by asking "Why?" and "What if?" and "What am I missing?"
- They built something *real*—an Are-Self project that does something, teaches something, or helps someone.
- They presented it to their peers and community.

That's not small. That's a huge accomplishment for a 4th grader—and for you as a teacher.

- Challenge them to build a *second* project on a completely different topic
- Have them teach a friend or family member to build their own

If this is the end:
- Celebrate it. You've opened a door. They've walked through it.
- You've planted seeds. Some will grow. Some won't grow yet. Both are OK.

Thank you for bringing this curriculum to life. Thank you for being the kind of teacher who trusts students with real tools. Thank you for modeling curiosity, courage, and care.

*You are one of those people too.*
