# Week 5: Social Studies & History

## Overview: The Circle That Kept Getting Bigger

This week, students explore history and community through the lens of **Inclusion** — the habit of looking at any group and asking *who is missing*. The companion reading is **Chapters 5–6 of "Mira and the Are-Self,"** where Mira invites Priya to lunch and sees the ripple effect of inclusion, then tackles bigger questions about inequality, food distribution, and her grandmother's village. By the end of the week, students will have asked historical questions, researched with Are-Self as a thinking partner, analyzed primary sources from the perspective of those usually left out, and written their own reflection on inclusion in their community.

**Primary Habit:** Inclusion (check who you left outside the circle)  
**Companion Reading:** Chapters 5–6 of "Mira and the Are-Self"  
**Materials:** Chapter text, primary source excerpts (letters, photos, speeches), "Research Notes" worksheet, "Primary Source Interview" worksheet, poster paper or digital docs for final letters  
**Why This Matters:** History is not neutral. Every story is told from some perspective. Every group decision leaves someone out. Learning to ask "whose voice is missing?" is how we build more just communities. And it's a skill worth developing young.

## Day 1: Priya at the Lunch Table

**Objective:** Students read Chapter 5 of "Mira and the Are-Self," understand the ripple effect of inclusion, and ask the central question of the week: "Whose stories get told, and whose get left out?"

**Materials:**  
- Text of Chapter 5: "Mira and the Are-Self" (printout or projected)  
- Whiteboard or chart paper  
- Optional: sticky notes for recording thoughts  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Ask students: "Have you ever been left out of something? Or noticed someone who was sitting alone?"

Give them 1–2 minutes to think. Then ask: "What happened next? What changed when someone noticed?"

Jot down a few responses. (You're priming them to think about the power of inclusion before the chapter.)

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Reading Aloud (12–15 minutes)**

Read Chapter 5 aloud (or have students take turns reading). Here's a sample chapter you can adapt or use as a model:

---

### CHAPTER 5: Priya at the Lunch Table (Sample Text)

Mira carried her lunch tray into the cafeteria and looked around the crowded room. There were so many kids, all sitting in groups. Her friends waved from their usual table near the window.

As she walked over, she passed Priya sitting alone.

Priya was new to the school. She was in Mira's math class, but Mira had never really talked to her. Mira realized she didn't know anything about Priya—not really. She just knew that Priya sometimes sat by herself, and she brought lunch in a metal box instead of buying it from the cafeteria.

Mira stopped walking.

"Hey," she said. "Want to sit with us?"

Priya looked up, surprised. "Really?"

"Yeah. Really."

Mira sat down with her friends, but now there was an extra seat, and Priya was in it. As they ate, Mira learned that Priya's family had just moved from another country. That the metal lunch box held food Priya's mom made—stuff Mira had never tried. That Priya spoke two languages. That she had a lot to say, once someone asked.

By the end of lunch, three kids who hadn't been sitting with Mira before were all eating together. And Priya wasn't sitting alone anymore.

That night, Mira told her parents about inviting Priya.

"Good instinct," her dad said. "That's inclusion."

"What's inclusion?" asked Mira.

Her mom answered: "Inclusion means making sure everyone gets to be part of the circle. It means noticing who's left out and doing something about it."

Mira thought about that. "But how do you know who needs to be invited?"

"You look," her dad said. "You notice. And then you ask."

---

**Discussion (10–15 minutes)**

After reading, guide a discussion:

**Teacher Script:**

> "Think about this: Mira didn't know much about Priya before lunch. She just noticed that Priya was sitting alone. What changed?"

Listen for: "Mira talked to her," "She found out Priya's story," "Priya got to share about her family."

> "Priya was always part of the school. But was she part of *the group* before Mira invited her? What's the difference?"

Listen for students understanding that being *in a place* isn't the same as being *included*.

> "What's one thing about Priya that nobody would have known if Mira hadn't asked?"

Responses: her languages, her food, her family, what she liked to talk about, etc.

> "Here's the big question for this week: Whose stories get told, and whose get left out?"

Write the question on the board.

> "In history, just like at lunch, some people's stories are famous, and other people's are left out. This week, you're going to be like Mira. You're going to ask, 'Who's missing from this story?' And you're going to try to find their voice."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Invite students to write or draw in their AI journals:

> "Who is the Priya in YOUR school or community? Who do you notice sitting alone or not being heard?"
>
> "What would it take to invite them into the circle?"

Affirm: "Sometimes the bravest thing is noticing someone who's left out. That's what Mira did."

**Assessment**

- Do students understand that inclusion means actively bringing people in, not just allowing them to be present?
- Can they think of an example of someone being left out?
- Are they starting to wonder about whose stories are missing?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Ask simpler questions: "Why was Priya sitting alone?" "What did Mira do?" "Was it a big thing or a small thing?"
- **Advanced:** Ask: "Why do you think Mira didn't think to invite Priya before? What made her notice this time?"

## Day 2: Historical Research Partner

**Objective:** Students choose a historical figure or event (aligned with your curriculum standards) and use Are-Self as a research assistant. They practice asking follow-up questions and evaluating the AI's answers.

**Materials:**  
- "Research Notes" worksheet (provided below)  
- Are-Self instance (teacher-led or small-group access)  
- Optional: reading list of historical figures/events your curriculum covers  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Remind students: "You're a historian now. A historian asks questions to learn about the past. Your research partner is Are-Self. You can ask it anything, but here's the trick: you don't just accept the first answer. You ask follow-up questions. You check if the answer makes sense."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: Choose Your Topic (5 minutes)**

Depending on your state standards, provide a list of options. Examples:

- **State history:** (Your state's founding, important figures, settlements, conflicts, etc.)
- **U.S. History:** Lewis and Clark, Civil War, Industrial Revolution, civil rights figures, first nations, immigration, etc.
- **Indigenous peoples:** (Specific to your region)
- **Local history:** (A local figure, event, or community)

Give students 2–3 options if possible: "Pick something YOU want to know about."

Pair up students or keep them individually, depending on your access to Are-Self.

**Part 2: Interview Are-Self (15–20 minutes)**

Students go to Are-Self with their topic. The goal is to ask questions and follow up.

**Sample Scripts for Students:**

> "I'm researching [person/event]. Can you tell me about it?"

> "You said [thing the AI said]. What do you mean by that?"

> "How do you know that? Is that definitely true?"

> "Who was affected by this? What happened to them?"

> "What happened before this? What came after?"

> "What's something most people don't know about [topic]?"

> "One more question: [something that puzzles you]"

As students write their notes, they're practicing the habit of inquiry. They're asking "one more question" just like Mira does.

**Teacher Circulation Script:**

> "What's the most interesting thing you've learned so far?"

> "That's a good follow-up question. Does the answer make sense to you?"

> "You're doing real historian work right now—asking questions and checking the answers."

**Teacher Moves:**

- Encourage multiple follow-up questions. "You already asked two questions. What else do you want to know?"
- If an answer sounds weird or wrong, ask: "Does that sound right to you? What would you do to check?"
- Affirm good thinking: "I like that you asked WHO was affected. That's inclusion thinking—you're looking for the people whose voices might be left out."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Bring the class back together. Ask a few students:

> "What surprised you about what you learned?"

> "Is there something the AI said that you're not sure about?"

Listen for the second question in particular. It's okay if students found confusing or uncertain information. That's part of research.

**Assessment**

- Did students ask at least one follow-up question?
- Are they thinking about who was affected by the event/person they researched?
- Do they understand that "the AI told me" is not the same as "I verified it"?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide sentence stems for questions: "Tell me more about…" "What happened to…?" "Why did…?"
- **Advanced:** Require students to find at least one "limitation" in what the AI told them: "The AI didn't mention… I wonder why."

## Day 3: "Feed It a Source"

**Objective:** Students work with a primary source (a historical document, letter, photograph, or speech excerpt). They feed the source to Are-Self and then interview the AI about what the document reveals—and what it *doesn't* say.

**Materials:**  
- Primary source documents (see suggestions below)  
- "Primary Source Interview" worksheet (provided below)  
- Are-Self instance  

**Sample Primary Sources by Grade/Topic**

*(Adapt to your curriculum)*

- **Letter from a settler to family back east** (Who does it mention? Who doesn't it talk about?)
- **Excerpt from a diary during a historical event** (What's the writer worried about? What do they not mention?)
- **Historical speech or proclamation** (Who is it written for? Who's left out?)
- **Description of a historical photograph** (Who is in the photo? Who is missing? What's the photographer trying to show?)
- **Excerpt from a newspaper article from a historical period** (How does it describe the event? What language does it use? Would everyone agree with this description?)
- **Personal account of an immigrant arriving at a port or station** (What does the writer describe? What might they have left out because they were scared, sad, or confused?)

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Show one simple source (projected or printed) and explain:

**Teacher Script:**

> "This is a primary source. That means it's a real thing from history—a real letter someone wrote, a real photograph someone took, real words someone spoke. It's not a modern person *writing about* history. It's history, directly from the past."

> "When we read a primary source, we don't just take it at face value. We ask: Who wrote this? What's their point of view? Who are they talking about? Who are they NOT talking about? That last question is our inclusion question."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: Read the Source (5 minutes)**

Distribute or display the primary source. Have students read it silently, then read it aloud as a group.

**Part 2: Interview Are-Self (15–20 minutes)**

Students ask Are-Self questions about the source, using the worksheet as a guide.

**Sample Scripts:**

> "I'm going to share a historical document with you. Can you help me understand it?"

> [Student shares the source text or description]

> "Who do you think wrote this? What do you think they cared about?"

> "Who does this document talk about? Who is NOT mentioned?"

> "Why might someone have left those people out? Was it on purpose?"

> "If we wanted to hear from the people who are left out, how would we do that?"

**Teacher Circulation:**

> "This is great thinking. You're asking whose voice is missing. That's historians' work."

> "The AI gave you one answer. Can you ask another question to dig deeper?"

> "You're practicing inclusion by wondering who's left out of this story."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Invite volunteers to share what they found:

> "What surprised you about the source?"

> "Who was left out of this document? Why do you think?"

> "If that person could write a response, what would they say?"

**Assessment**

- Can students identify the author's perspective?
- Do they think about who is mentioned and who is not?
- Are they making connections between what's in the source and what's missing?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide the worksheet with some answers already filled in. Focus on the last two questions (who's missing, why).
- **Advanced:** Ask students to write a sentence from the perspective of someone left out: "If I were [missing person], I would say…"

## Day 4: The Missing Voice

**Objective:** Students take a historical event (from their research on Day 2 or a class-wide topic) and deliberately imagine the perspective of someone usually left out of that story. They write from the missing voice.

**Materials:**  
- Student research notes from Day 2 (or a class-wide historical topic)  
- Are-Self instance  
- Paper or digital document for writing  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Tell this short story:

**Teacher Script:**

> "Imagine someone writes a book called 'The Brave Gold Miners of 1849.' The book is all about the miners who went to California looking for gold. It talks about their hard work, their discoveries, their dreams.
>
> But here's what the book doesn't say: there were already people living there. The Miwok people. The Maidu people. The Pomo people. They lived there for thousands of years. What happened to them when the miners came?
>
> That's the missing voice. That's whose story got left out.
>
> Today, you're going to write from the missing voice. You're going to imagine what someone who was left out would say about the story everyone knows."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: Identify the Missing Voice (5 minutes)**

If students researched individual topics on Day 2, they should think about their topic:

> "In the story about [person/event], whose perspective is usually left out? Who was affected but doesn't get to speak?"

Examples:

- **Gold Rush:** Indigenous peoples whose land was taken.
- **Ellis Island:** The people who were turned away or treated badly.
- **Industrial Revolution:** The workers in factories.
- **Civil War:** Enslaved people fighting for freedom.
- **Westward Expansion:** Native Americans being pushed out.
- **Space Race:** The women and people of color whose work wasn't credited.

If the class did a group topic, assign or let students choose who the missing voice is.

**Part 2: Ask Are-Self for Help Imagining (10 minutes)**

Students talk to Are-Self about the perspective they want to write from:

**Sample Scripts:**

> "I'm researching the Gold Rush. I want to write from the perspective of a Miwok person whose land the miners came to. Can you help me understand what that might have been like?"

> "What did [this group of people] experience during [this event]? What were they worried about?"

> "If I were a [person from the missing perspective] in [year/place], what would I see? What would I feel?"

> "I want to write a paragraph from [missing perspective]. Can you help me imagine what they might say?"

Are-Self can help students think through the historical context, the emotional reality, and specific details they might include.

**Part 3: Write from the Missing Voice (10 minutes)**

Students write one paragraph (3–5 sentences) from the perspective of someone usually left out. It can be:

- A diary entry
- A letter
- A speech or statement
- A first-person reflection

**Example Paragraph (Gold Rush, Miwok perspective):**

> "The people came with tools and sickness. They said the gold was theirs. They said the land was empty. They did not see us—the ones who had lived here longer than anyone could remember. We tried to hide. We tried to farm in the places they didn't want. But there was nowhere left. And nobody was writing down our names."

**Teacher Moves:**

> "You don't have to know EVERYTHING about what they experienced. You're imagining, with respect. Use what you know and fill in with your thinking."

> "Use 'I' language. That makes it real. 'I saw… I felt… I remembered…'"

> "Can you add one specific detail? Not just 'it was hard,' but HOW was it hard?"

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Invite students to share their paragraphs (volunteers, not mandatory). After each, ask:

> "Thank you for sharing that voice. How does it change the story we usually hear?"

Affirm: "You just did what historians call 'perspective-taking.' You imagined what it was like for someone usually left out. That's how we build a fuller, truer picture of history."

**Assessment**

- Did the student write from the perspective of someone usually left out of the main narrative?
- Is there empathy in the writing (not judgment, just understanding)?
- Does it include a specific historical context (not generic)?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide a sentence frame: "I lived in [place]. When [event] happened, I…" Complete 2–3 sentences.
- **Advanced:** Write from multiple perspectives. Compare how different people experienced the same event.

## Day 5: Community Connection & Reflection

**Objective:** Students make the leap from history to their own community. They identify something in their local world that could use the "bigger circle" treatment, research it briefly with Are-Self, and write a letter to their community.

**Materials:**  
- "Letter to My Community" template (provided below)  
- Are-Self instance for optional research  
- Poster paper or digital template for gallery walk  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Bring the conversation home:

**Teacher Script:**

> "This week, we learned that history is full of left-out voices. We've been asking, 'Whose story got forgotten? Whose perspective is missing?'
>
> But inclusion isn't just about history. It's about RIGHT NOW. In your school, your neighborhood, your community, there are people who might be left out, just like Priya was. And just like Mira noticed Priya, YOU can notice.
>
> Today, you're going to find one thing in your community that could use a bigger circle. And you're going to write about it."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: Brainstorm (5 minutes)**

Ask students:

> "In your school or community, who do you notice isn't included? Where do you see a small circle that could be bigger?"

Examples (age-appropriate):

- Kids who don't have friends at lunch.
- Kids who don't get picked for teams.
- Kids in a class who are bullied.
- Elderly people in the community who live alone.
- Homeless people in your neighborhood.
- Kids with disabilities who aren't invited to regular activities.
- Immigrants or newcomers who are left out.
- Families who can't afford things other kids can afford.

**Important Teacher Note:** This should be gentle and observational, not preachy. You're not asking kids to judge their classmates. You're asking them to notice patterns and think about kindness.

**Part 2: Research (Optional) (5–10 minutes)**

If a student chooses a community issue (homelessness, hunger, learning differences, etc.), they can ask Are-Self a quick question:

> "I noticed that some people in my community are [situation]. Why does that happen? What could help?"

Are-Self can give factual background, which helps students write with understanding rather than stereotypes.

**Part 3: Write "Letter to My Community" (10 minutes)**

**Teacher Script (as students write):**

> "You're not saying other kids are bad. You're noticing something and suggesting a kinder way. That takes wisdom."

> "Your idea doesn't have to fix everything. It just has to be real and kind."

> "Think like Mira: what's one small thing that could make someone feel included?"

**Teacher Circulation:**

> "This is a thoughtful observation. Can you think of one specific thing that could help?"

> "I like how respectful you're being. You're not blaming. You're imagining how it might feel."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Conduct a **Gallery Walk:**

- Post all letters on the walls or display them on screen.
- Students walk around silently, reading each other's observations.
- They leave sticky notes: "I like your idea because…" or "That's something I never thought about."

**Whole-Class Wrap (5 minutes):**

Bring students back. Ask:

> "What was one idea that surprised you or inspired you?"

> "Does reading other people's letters change how you see your community?"

> "Would it be possible to do any of these ideas? What would we need?"

Affirm the entire week:

> "You've gone from history to right now. You've asked 'whose voice is missing' about gold miners and your neighbors. You've learned that inclusion is something you can DO, not just something that happens to you. That's a powerful habit."

**Assessment**

- Did the student identify a real observation in their community?
- Is there empathy and respect in the letter?
- Is the suggested idea realistic and kind?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Draw instead of write. Or fill in a sentence: "I noticed _____ are left out. I think they could be included if ___."
- **Advanced:** Suggest an idea that involves action. "Next week, I will…" or "Our class could…"

## Week 5 Reflection & Connection

**For the AI Journal (end of week):**

Students write or draw (3–5 sentences/sketches):

> "Who is the person I learned about this week whose voice is usually left out?"
>
> "What did I imagine about their experience?"
>
> "Who is the Priya in my world? What could I do?"

**Connection to Inclusion:**

Wrap the week by connecting back to Mira's story:

> "Mira asked 'What would it be like to be Priya?' and that simple question changed things. This week, you asked the same question about people in history and in your own community. Inclusion doesn't mean everything is perfect or everyone agrees. It means you notice when someone is outside the circle, and you ask why. And then you try to make the circle bigger. That's how communities get better. That's how history could have been different. And that's how your classroom and community can be different, starting now."

## Teacher Notes

- **Sensitivity & Age-Appropriateness:** These topics are important and real, but keep them age-appropriate. Focus on observable, solvable issues (lunch tables, activities, opportunities to be heard) rather than heavy trauma or systemic oppression. Fourth graders can think about fairness without being made to feel guilty.

- **Are-Self as Research Partner:** The AI is NOT the authority. It's a thinking partner. Encourage students to be skeptical: "The AI said X. Does that match what you learned in the book?" This week, students learn that sources have perspective, and so does Are-Self.

- **Primary Sources Need Context:** If students are unfamiliar with historical periods, provide a brief context sentence before diving into the source. Example: "This is a letter from someone who came to America on a boat in the 1800s. At that time, it was very hard to travel and scary."

- **The Missing Voice Activity:** This is emotional work. Some students might write about people they know personally (a classmate, a family member) who feel left out. That's valid. Affirm it. The habit of inclusion applies everywhere.

- **Community Action (Optional):** If students get energized by their "Letter to the Community" ideas, you could designate a bulletin board for displaying the letters, or share them with a school administrator. It's powerful for kids to see their thinking matter.

- **Tone Matters:** Throughout the week, avoid "sad story" framing. History and current events are real, but your job is to cultivate curiosity and kindness, not despair. "This group's voice was left out. Let's hear it" is stronger than "This group suffered."

- **Humility & Are-Self:** Tie this back to Week 4 if possible. "Remember when we found out the AI can remember wrong facts? Here's something similar: the AI might know history from the textbooks everyone uses, which might leave out voices. When we ask Are-Self about a historical event, we're not just getting truth. We're getting one perspective. We have to ask more questions."

- **Verification Habit:** Encourage students to check: "The AI told me [fact]. Have I seen that in our textbook? Did it feel right?" This isn't about not trusting the AI. It's about being a critical thinker, which is the historian's real job.

## Curriculum Connections

**Social Studies Standards (CCSS-aligned):**
- Analyze primary and secondary sources.
- Understand perspective and point of view in historical accounts.
- Examine how people lived in different communities and time periods.
- Understand rights, responsibilities, and inclusion in a community.

**English Language Arts:**
- Asking and answering questions about informational texts.
- Writing informative/narrative texts.
- Speaking and listening: discussing ideas and responding to others.

**21st Century Skills:**
- Critical thinking and information evaluation.
- Perspective-taking and empathy.
- Communication and collaboration.
- Civic engagement and community awareness.

## Extensions & Follow-Up

- **Guest Speaker:** Invite a local historian, community organizer, or elder to discuss inclusion or local history.
- **Class Visit:** Take a neighborhood walk and identify places where people might be left out or included (parks, shops, public spaces). Discuss what you notice.
- **Continuing the Research:** Let interested students dive deeper into one historical topic over the following weeks.
- **Community Action:** Work with the school to implement one of the students' ideas from their "Letter to My Community."
- **Connection to Current Events:** Bring in age-appropriate current issues and ask the same questions: "Whose voice is missing from this story?"
