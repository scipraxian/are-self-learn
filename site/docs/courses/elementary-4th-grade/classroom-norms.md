# Classroom Norms & the Three Habits

## Why Norms Matter

Before we hand a kid a laptop and say "go talk to an AI," we need to talk about *why* we talk to anything—human or software. AI is a tool. A really smart tool, sure, but still a tool. Without ground rules, it's like handing someone a calculator without teaching them what multiplication means.

Fourth graders are at that exact right moment: old enough to think critically, young enough to still believe what appears on a screen without question. Our job is to build habits *before* they touch the AI. These norms are the container that makes the learning real.

---

## The Three Scipraxian Habits

The Are-Self project rests on three core habits. These come from the storybook *Mira and the Are-Self*, and they show up in every classroom activity—whether we're using AI or not. Think of them as the backbone of what we're teaching.

### Habit 1: Inclusion

**What it means:** Whenever you draw a circle around a group, check who you left outside. Most of the time, it's the forgetting that does the damage.

**In plain talk for kids:** Look around. Who's in the circle? Who's standing outside? Think about whether they should be inside.

**Classroom example:**
When your class is brainstorming ideas for a project, Inclusion means asking: "Did we think about what kids who use wheelchairs might need?" or "Did we remember that some kids can't see the board?" or "Did we include the kids who are quiet?" It's not guilt—it's checking.

**5-minute practice activity:**
Show a picture of a birthday party with 8 kids. Ask: "Who's having fun? Who might not be? How would you change it so everyone could play?" Let them shout out ideas. No right answer—the muscle you're building is *noticing*.

**Connection to AI use:**
When the AI helps you write a story or solve a problem, Inclusion means asking: "Does this answer leave someone out? Does it make sense for kids who learn differently? Would this idea help the kid who's struggling, or just the kid who's already winning?" It's catching the AI before it accidentally erases someone.

---

### Habit 2: Humility

**What it means:** Knowing how much you don't know YET, and being okay with that. "I don't know yet" is the most valuable sentence in learning.

**In plain talk for kids:** You don't know everything. That's not bad. That's the whole point of learning.

**Classroom example:**
Sarah doesn't understand long division. Instead of saying "I'm bad at math," she says "I don't know long division yet." Instead of avoiding the AI, she uses it—but she asks it to *explain*, not just to give her the answer. Six weeks later, she gets it. That shift—from "I can't" to "I don't know yet"—is Humility.

**5-minute practice activity:**
Go around the room. Each kid completes this sentence: "I don't know how to _____ yet, but I want to learn." Write them down. Keep the list. Come back to it in three months. Mark off the ones they've learned. Watch their faces when they realize: learning is real, and time works.

**Connection to AI use:**
Humility means not pretending you understand something when you don't. If the AI gives you an answer and it doesn't make sense, that's not a problem—that's honest feedback. Instead of nodding and moving on, you say: "I don't understand that yet. Can you explain it differently?" You're using the AI as a tutor, not as a shortcut.

---

### Habit 3: Inquiry

**What it means:** After every answer, ask one more question. Not to be annoying—to find out if the answer was the whole shape of the thing.

**In plain talk for kids:** When someone tells you something, ask a follow-up. You might find out the first answer was only half the story.

**Classroom example:**
The AI tells you that the Civil War started in 1861. That's true. But if you stop there, you've learned a date, not history. Inquiry means asking: "Why did it start then? What happened the year before? Who didn't want war? What did they want instead?" Each question is a thread that pulls more truth out.

**5-minute practice activity:**
Read a short news story or fairy tale aloud. After you finish, ask: "Who has a question?" Take 5 questions. Then ask: "What did the questions make you wonder about? What else might be true?" You're showing kids that understanding isn't "I know the ending"—it's "I know what to ask next."

**Connection to AI use:**
When you use the AI, Inquiry means you don't just copy the first answer. You ask: "Is that really true? What about the other side? Can you give me an example? How do you know? What would someone disagree with?" This is how you catch when the AI is confident but wrong. This is how you learn, instead of just accumulating answers.

## Our AI Classroom Rules

These are your posted-on-the-wall, written-in-the-syllabus rules. Read them on Day 1. Come back to them when someone forgets. They're the rails.

**1. The AI is a thinking PARTNER, not an answer machine.**
If you just copy what it says, you're not learning—you're typing. The AI is a study buddy, like sitting next to a smart friend. A friend who's right a lot, but can mess up.

**2. Always verify what the AI tells you (AI can be wrong!).**
The AI will not say "I don't know." It will sound confident even when it's making something up. Your brain is the backup. If something feels wrong, it probably is. Check it.

**3. Your ideas come first—then ask the AI.**
You think. You try. You write down what you know. *Then* you ask the AI. Not before. Your brain doing the work is the whole point.

**4. Be honest about what the AI helped with.**
If the AI helped you write a paragraph, say so. If it helped you brainstorm, say so. This isn't cheating—it's truth. Honesty is how we all learn what actually happened.

**5. Ask "one more question" before accepting any answer.**
This is Inquiry in action. Don't stop at the first explanation. Keep going. Make sure you actually understand, not just that you have words.

**6. Treat the AI the way you'd treat a study buddy—with respect and with boundaries.**
The AI isn't a person, but it isn't nothing either. Use it. Learn from it. But don't let it make your decisions. You're the thinker. It's the tool.

**7. If something feels wrong or confusing, tell your teacher immediately.**
The AI might generate something mean, or scary, or just bonkers. You might get confused. Or you might feel weird about what the AI said. That's real data. Tell me. We'll figure it out together.

## Introducing AI to Students: Day 1 Talking Points

Here's a script you can adapt. Say it like you mean it—because you do.

---

**"Today we're meeting something new. It's called an AI. That stands for Artificial Intelligence. Don't let the name scare you.**

**An AI is software—code, instructions—that learned patterns from lots and lots of examples. It's kind of like how your brain works. Your brain learns patterns too. You see a dog, you see another dog, you see another dog. Pretty soon, you can recognize a dog without being told.**

**This AI has read millions of words. It's learned patterns about how language works, how ideas connect, how people talk. So when you ask it something, it doesn't look things up like Google does. It *generates* an answer. It predicts what word should come next, then the next word, then the next.**

**That's powerful. You can ask it questions. You can ask it to help you brainstorm. You can ask it to explain things. But here's the critical part: it can be wrong. It can be very confident and very wrong. Just like a friend who talks a lot.**

**Your job—all of our jobs—is to stay smarter than the tool. To think about what it says. To ask questions. To verify. To not let it think FOR you, but to use it to think WITH you.**

**We have rules about this. We do Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry—the three habits. Those apply to AI just like they apply to everything else.**

**Any questions? What's something you're curious about? What worries you?"**

---

Let them ask. Let them wonder. Some kids will be excited. Some will be nervous. Some will not care. All of that is fine. You're planting seeds, not forcing flowers.

## The Daily Check-In: 2-Minute Routine

Every time your class sits down to use the AI, start here. It takes two minutes. It's the difference between "kids messing with AI" and "kids learning with intention."

**Ask the group (or have them write down):**

1. **"What's our goal today?"** — Not "use AI." Specific. *"We're understanding how photosynthesis works"* or *"We're brainstorming characters for a story."*

2. **"What habit are we practicing?"** — Is it Inclusion (making sure we're thinking about everyone)? Humility (staying curious when we don't understand)? Inquiry (asking more questions)? Or maybe all three?

3. **"How will we know if we learned something real?"** — Not "the AI gave me an answer." Real looks like: *"I can explain it in my own words,"* or *"I thought of something the AI didn't,"* or *"I found out I was wrong, and now I know why."*

Write these three things on the board. Refer back to them when someone gets lost or stuck. At the end of the session, ask: "Did we hit our goal? How did we do with the habit we picked?"

This transforms AI from a black box into a thinking routine.

## Differentiation Notes

Fourth-grade classrooms are not one thing. Here's how to adapt.

### For Advanced Learners

These kids might want to take the wheel. That's fine—but you're still the driver.

- **More autonomy:** Let them design their own questions. Let them challenge the AI's answer and dig deeper.
- **Bigger challenge:** Instead of "Use the AI to write a paragraph," try: "Use the AI to brainstorm 10 ideas, pick the worst one, and figure out why it's worst." Or: "Ask the AI to explain your math concept three different ways. Which one made most sense?"
- **The guard rail:** They still do *their* thinking first. They still verify. They still do Inclusion (thinking about the people their ideas affect). Autonomy isn't freedom from structure—it's deeper structure.

### For Struggling Learners

These kids need more scaffolding, not less AI. The AI can be a lifeline.

- **More guidance:** Don't say "Ask the AI your question." Instead: "Here's a question I want you to ask the AI: _____. Now, read what it says. Do you understand it? If not, ask it to explain with a simpler word."
- **Humility as permission:** Some kids feel broken when they don't understand. Tell them explicitly: "Not understanding yet is the *best* place to ask the AI. That's what it's for." Model it.
- **Smaller steps:** Instead of one big task, break it into pieces. "First, ask the AI to explain what this word means. Then tell me what you heard. Then ask the AI for an example."
- **Verification as teaching:** When they ask the AI a question, don't just take its answer. Ask them: "Does that feel right? Why? Why not?" You're building the verification muscle.

### For Students Anxious About AI

Some kids are scared. Some think they'll "do it wrong." Some are worried the AI is watching them.

- **Demystify:** Let them see what happens when they ask the AI something. Type it yourself. Show them it's just text appearing. No magic. No judgment.
- **Small wins:** Start them with something low-stakes. "Ask the AI to write a funny poem about socks." Nothing important. Just: here's how you talk to it.
- **Normalize mistakes:** "I asked the AI this and it gave me a bad answer. That happens. Watch how I figured it out." Let them see you verify, disagree, ask harder questions.
- **Control:** Give them the choice. "Do you want to try the AI, or would you rather brainstorm with me first?" Some kids will come around faster if they feel they can opt in.
- **Check in quietly:** These kids might not want to say "I'm scared" in front of the class. Pull them aside. "How are you feeling about the AI?" Listen. Maybe they just need one-on-one practice. That's okay.

## The Bigger Picture

These norms are more than rules. They're a statement: *We think. We question. We include. We verify. We stay curious.* And we do that with AI, or without it, because thinking is the point.

When you set these up well, something shifts. Kids stop asking "Is the AI right?" and start asking "What's the *real* answer?" And they start looking to themselves—and to each other—for that answer. The AI becomes a tool for thinking, not a shortcut around it.

That's the move.
