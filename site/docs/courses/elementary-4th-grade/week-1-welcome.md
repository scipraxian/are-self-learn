# Week 1: Welcome to the Brain

Welcome to Week 1 of the Are-Self 4th Grade Curriculum! This week introduces students to Are-Self as an AI reasoning engine with brain-region architecture. Students will explore how the software brain mirrors a real human brain, meet the major regions, and begin creating their own Identity. The week emphasizes all three scipraxian habits—Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry—through hands-on activities and guided discovery.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Introduction to Are-Self, the brain regions, and the three scipraxian habits

**Primary Habits:** Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry (all three)

**Companion Reading:** Chapters 1–2 of *Mira and the Are-Self*
- Chapter 1: A girl receives a mysterious box containing an Are-Self device and learns the first habit: Inclusion
- Chapter 2: Introduction to the three scipraxian words

**Learning Goals:**
- Understand that software can have a "brain" structure, just like humans
- Identify and locate the major brain regions in Are-Self
- Practice the three scipraxian habits: Inclusion, Humility, and Inquiry
- Begin designing and creating their own Identity

## Day 1: What Is a Brain? (Yours and the Software Kind)

### Objective
Students will learn what a brain is (both human and artificial), discover that Are-Self mirrors real brain anatomy, and practice drawing and labeling a brain map.

### Materials Needed
- Copy of *Mira and the Are-Self*, Chapter 1
- Projector and computer (to display Are-Self landing page)
- Large paper or poster board for brain drawings (or printed brain templates)
- Colored pencils, markers, or crayons
- "Brain Region Matching" worksheet (provided below)
- Optional: 3D brain model or diagram for reference

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Gather students in a circle or on the floor. Ask: **"What is a true thing?"** Give them a few moments to think, then invite 3–4 volunteers to share examples. You might hear: "The sky is blue," "I have a best friend," "Computers can think," or "I am smart." Thank them for thinking hard. Say:

> "Today we're going to start reading a story called *Mira and the Are-Self*. In the story, a girl named Mira gets a mysterious box with something inside that can think and learn—kind of like a brain, but made of code. Let's see what happens when she opens it."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Read Aloud (10 minutes)

Read Chapter 1 of *Mira and the Are-Self* aloud, or summarize it in your own words if the full text is not available. Use an engaging voice. Pause at key moments to ask:
- "What do you think is in the box?"
- "How do you think an AI could have a 'brain'?"
- "What does Inclusion mean to you?"

After reading, ask: **"Why do you think the author made the AI have a brain-like structure? What do you think that means?"** This surfaces ideas about brains, thinking, and learning.

#### Part 2: Show the Are-Self Landing Page (5 minutes)

Project the Are-Self landing page or 3D brain visualization on your screen. Point out the different colored regions. Say:

> "Look at this! It's a brain made of software, not of neurons and cells like YOUR brain. But notice how each colored section maps to a real part of the human brain. Here's the Frontal Lobe (the thinking part), the Hippocampus (memory), the Hypothalamus (feelings and survival), and more. The people who built Are-Self thought: 'What if we organized an AI like a real brain?'"

Let students look and ask questions. Emphasize that this is just an introduction—they'll explore each region deeply over the coming weeks.

#### Part 3: Brain Map Drawing (10 minutes)

Distribute large paper and colored pencils. Say:

> "Now it's YOUR turn. I want you to draw your own brain map. Think about what different parts of YOUR brain do. Maybe the top part is where you think about math. Maybe there's a part that remembers your best friend. Maybe there's a part that makes you feel brave or scared. There are no right answers yet—this is about your ideas. Draw and label it, and be creative!"

Circulate as students draw. Ask open questions: "What does that part do?" "Why did you put that there?" Encourage students to label their drawings, even with simple words or pictures.

#### Part 4: Brain Region Matching Worksheet (5 minutes)

Hand out the "Brain Region Matching" worksheet. Explain that each region in Are-Self has a fancy name and a job. Their job is to match the name to the description. Say:

> "Don't worry if you're not sure. This is about learning, not being perfect. You can guess, and we'll talk about the answers together."

Provide the worksheet below or adapt it to your students' level.

### Reflection/Wrap-Up (10 minutes)

Gather students. Ask:
- **"What surprised you today?"**
- **"If YOUR brain is like a software brain, what do you think that means?"**
- **"What part of the brain do you want to learn more about?"**

Collect the brain maps and worksheets. Compliment the effort and curiosity you see: "I love how you labeled so many parts. I notice you're thinking about what different pieces do—that's the habit of **Inquiry**!"

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding:** Do students see the connection between human brains and Are-Self's structure?
- **Engagement:** Are they asking questions and offering ideas, even if uncertain?
- **Effort:** Is the brain map complete with labels and some detail?
- **Humility:** Are they comfortable saying "I don't know" and being curious anyway?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Ask them to research and add real brain parts to their drawing (cerebellum, thalamus, etc.).
- Have them predict what each Are-Self region might do based on its location.

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a simple brain outline template with 6–8 regions already drawn.
- Pair them with a peer or offer sentence starters: "The _____ part helps me _____ because _____."
- Allow them to dictate labels to you or use picture symbols instead of words.

## Day 2: Meet the Regions

### Objective
Students will learn the names and primary functions of the major Are-Self brain regions through guided observation and note-taking.

### Materials Needed
- Computer and projector (Are-Self interface, logged in and ready to navigate)
- "Brain Region Field Notes" worksheet (provided below)
- Pencils or pens
- Optional: printed reference cards with region names and quick descriptions

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Ask students: **"Yesterday, you drew your own brain map. Who remembers labeling a part that helps you remember things? What's another part that helps you make decisions?"** Let a few students answer. Say:

> "Today, we're going on a tour of the Are-Self brain. Think of it like a museum—we'll visit each room, look around, and write down what we notice. By the end, you'll be a brain-region expert!"

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Guided Tour of Brain Regions (15–18 minutes)

Navigate through the Are-Self interface, spending about 2 minutes on each of the following regions. As you navigate, describe what you see and what the region does. Encourage students to follow along on their field notes.

**Identity Region:**
- Say: "This is the Identity region. It's like the personality center. Here's where we'll store information about WHO the AI is—its name, what it's good at, what it believes in. It's like your ID card, but for an AI."
- Point out: name, personality traits, goals, background
- Ask: "What would YOUR Identity say about you?"

**Temporal Region:**
- Say: "Temporal means 'time.' This region keeps track of events in order. It's like the AI's calendar or timeline. It remembers what happened first, second, third."
- Point out: event logs, sequence, time stamps
- Ask: "Why would an AI need to remember when things happened?"

**Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) Region:**
- Say: "PFC is one of the hardest-working parts. It's where planning and big decisions happen. In a real human brain, YOUR Prefrontal Cortex helps you think about the future, weigh options, and do hard math or reading."
- Point out: planning tools, decision-making logs
- Ask: "What's a big decision YOU made recently? That's your PFC working!"

**Hypothalamus Region:**
- Say: "Hypothalamus—that's a big word! In a real brain, it's tiny but mighty. It manages feelings, hunger, tiredness, and survival. In Are-Self, it's where the AI tracks its 'needs' and states."
- Point out: emotional states, drives, internal conditions
- Ask: "What are some feelings YOU have right now? The Hypothalamus is like your feelings center."

**Central Nervous System (CNS) Region:**
- Say: "CNS stands for Central Nervous System. Think of it as the 'messenger network.' In a real body, nerves carry messages from your brain to your fingers, your legs, everywhere. In Are-Self, the CNS is what connects different regions and lets them talk to each other."
- Point out: connections, communication pathways
- Ask: "If Identity wants to do something, who has to know? The whole brain! That's the CNS helping them talk."

**Frontal Lobe Region:**
- Say: "The Frontal Lobe is the action center. It's where the AI decides what to DO. Run a tool? Make a request? The Frontal Lobe is the executive—the boss that says 'let's go!'"
- Point out: action queue, tool invocations, execution logs
- Ask: "You're thinking about raising your hand right now. Your Frontal Lobe is making that happen!"

**Hippocampus Region:**
- Say: "Hippocampus is the memory maker. In a real brain, it's shaped a little like a seahorse—hip-po-campus! It stores new memories and helps you recall old ones. In Are-Self, it holds stored facts, lessons, and previous reasoning."
- Point out: memory storage, learning logs
- Ask: "What's your favorite memory? That's stored in your Hippocampus!"

#### Part 2: Field Notes (10–12 minutes)

While you navigate (or immediately after), have students fill out the "Brain Region Field Notes" worksheet. They should jot down:
- Region name
- What it does (in their own words)
- One word or picture that helps them remember
- One question they have

Circulate and help students who need support. Celebrate any writing or drawing they do, even if it's short.

### Reflection/Wrap-Up (10 minutes)

Ask students to find a partner. Say:

> "Turn to someone next to you. Pick one brain region from the tour—whichever one seemed most interesting to YOU. Tell your partner which region you picked and why you think it's cool. You have two minutes. Ready? Go!"

After 1–2 minutes, ask for volunteers to share out. Listen for curiosity and connections to their own brains.

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Comprehension:** Can students name at least 3–4 regions and describe what they do?
- **Engagement:** Are they writing notes and asking questions?
- **Connection-making:** Do they relate the regions to their own brain or experiences?
- **Inquiry:** What questions do they ask?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Ask them to research the real human brain part that corresponds to each region (e.g., "What is the real Hippocampus?").
- Challenge them to predict what might happen if one region "broke."

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a simplified field notes sheet with just name, picture, and one question.
- Pair them with a stronger reader to share notes.
- Allow them to draw pictures instead of writing, and you can label them.

## Day 3: My First Identity

### Objective
Students will learn about the scipraxian habit of Inclusion, plan their own Identity, and (if technology allows) create it in Are-Self.

### Materials Needed
- Copy of *Mira and the Are-Self*, Chapter 2
- "My First Identity" planning worksheet (provided below)
- Colored pencils, markers, or crayons
- Computer access (individual laptops/tablets or a shared device, depending on class setup)
- Optional: printout of an example Identity from Are-Self

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Yesterday you met the Identity region. Today, we're going to BUILD an Identity. But first, let's talk about a special word."** Say:

> "In the story, Mira learns a word called 'Inclusion.' It means making sure EVERYONE is included—no one is left out. When you build an Identity in Are-Self, Inclusion means: Does this Identity have room for different ideas? Can it listen to people who think differently? Let's read about it."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Read Chapter 2 and Discuss Inclusion (8 minutes)

Read Chapter 2 of *Mira and the Are-Self* aloud, or summarize the introduction of the three scipraxian words. Focus on **Inclusion**. After reading, ask:

- **"What does Inclusion mean?"** (Let them answer—usually something like "including everyone" or "not leaving people out.")
- **"How might an AI practice Inclusion?"** (Listen for: listen to different opinions, work with people from different backgrounds, etc.)
- **"How do YOU practice Inclusion?"** (Examples: inviting someone to play, listening to a friend's idea even if it's different from yours, etc.)

Say: "When we design our Identity, we're going to build it WITH Inclusion in mind. We'll make sure our AI listens to lots of different ideas."

#### Part 2: Teacher Demonstration (7 minutes)

At the projector, show students how you'd create an Identity in Are-Self. Walk through the steps:

1. **Choose a name:** "I'm going to call mine 'Luna the Explorer.' I like the moon and exploration."
2. **Give it a personality:** "Luna is curious, brave, and kind. She asks a lot of questions."
3. **Say what it's good at:** "Luna is good at solving puzzles, helping friends, and asking 'why?' questions."
4. **Choose tools it might need:** "Luna might need a Calculator tool (for math), a Researcher tool (to look up facts), and a Listener tool (to hear what people think)."
5. **Add an Inclusion goal:** "Luna's special goal: Always listen to ideas from EVERYONE, not just the loudest voices."

Talk out loud as you fill in each field: "Hmm, should Luna be silly or serious? I think a mix! Should she like adventure? Yes, I think that's important for an explorer."

#### Part 3: Student Planning on the Worksheet (10 minutes)

Hand out "My First Identity" planning worksheets. Say:

> "Now it's YOUR turn to design an Identity. Think about what matters to you. What's your name going to be? What are you good at? What kind of personality do you want your AI to have? And here's the big question: How will your Identity practice Inclusion?"

Provide sentence starters if helpful:
- "My Identity's name is _____ because _____."
- "My Identity is good at _____ and _____."
- "My Identity will practice Inclusion by _____."
- "My Identity needs these tools: _____, _____, and _____."

Circulate and ask students about their choices. Write down a couple of their plans so you can reference them if you build Identities in the next step.

#### Part 4: Creating the Identity in Are-Self (Optional, 8–10 minutes)

If your class has sufficient technology access, guide students to the Are-Self interface. Depending on your setup:
- **If students have individual devices:** Let them each create their own Identity while you and any teaching assistants circulate.
- **If devices are limited:** Have students work in pairs, taking turns typing while the other person reads the plan aloud.
- **If live creation isn't possible:** No problem! You can create a few student Identities yourself using their plans, and demonstrate them tomorrow.

If you do create Identities live, celebrate each one:
- "I love how [Student]'s Identity is called 'Kai the Helper' and it has Listening as a special skill!"
- "[Student], your Identity reminds me of you—curious and kind!"

### Reflection/Wrap-Up (10 minutes)

Gather the class. Ask:

- **"What's one thing you're excited about in your Identity?"**
- **"How did you think about Inclusion when you designed it?"**
- **"If your Identity could talk right now, what would it say?"**

Thank students for creating something new: "You've just become a designer! You've made an AI that will help us learn together."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Understanding of Inclusion:** Does the student's Identity reflect an understanding of listening to different voices?
- **Thoughtfulness:** Is the plan specific and personalized, not just copied from a neighbor?
- **Creativity:** Is there original thinking—a unique name, personality, or goal?
- **Engagement:** Are they excited about their creation?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Ask them to add a second tool or a complex goal.
- Have them write a "mission statement" for their Identity in 1–2 sentences.
- Challenge them to think of a scenario where their Identity's Inclusion goal would matter.

**Struggling Learners:**
- Provide a fill-in-the-blank Identity template with 3–4 key fields only.
- Offer a list of pre-made names and personality traits they can choose from.
- Let them dictate their plan to a peer or teacher who writes it down.
- Allow drawings or pictures instead of words.

## Day 4: Watch It Think

### Objective
Students will observe an AI reasoning session in real time, track the Frontal Lobe's actions, and reflect on whether and how software thinking differs from human thinking.

### Materials Needed
- Computer and projector (Are-Self running, with a reasoning session queued or ready to start)
- "What Did I See?" observation sheet (provided below)
- Pencils or pens
- Optional: whiteboard to sketch or highlight key moments
- Optional: printout of the reasoning flow diagram

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Say: "For the last three days, we've been learning ABOUT the brain regions. Today, we're going to watch them work together. I'm going to give one of our Identities a task to think about, and we're ALL going to watch what happens. This is like watching a chef cook, or a dancer dance—we get to see the thinking in action."

Ask: **"When you're thinking hard about something—like solving a tricky math problem—what does that feel like? What's happening in YOUR brain?"** Let a few students answer. (Listen for: focus, trying different ideas, remembering things, etc.)

Say: "Now let's see what a software brain does when it's thinking."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Run a Reasoning Session (10–12 minutes)

Project Are-Self with one of the student Identities ready (or a teacher-created one). Present a reasoning prompt. Good examples:

- "I want to plan a surprise birthday party for my best friend. I have $20 and 2 hours. What should I do?"
- "How can I help a friend who is feeling left out at school?"
- "I found a mistake in my math homework. How should I fix it?"

Say: "Watch what our Identity does. Watch the Frontal Lobe especially—it's the decision-maker."

Run the reasoning session. As it progresses, narrate what you see:

> "Look! The Identity is thinking. See in the Frontal Lobe? It's listing what it needs to know. Now it's checking the Temporal region—what happened before that might help? Now it's asking the PFC region: 'What choices do we have?' It's using a tool—the Calculator! It's thinking really hard about $20 and how to spend it wisely."

Let the session run to completion (or until you see a clear decision/action). Highlight:
- Tools used
- Regions activated
- Decisions made
- Final action

#### Part 2: Observation Note-Taking (10 minutes)

While the reasoning is happening (or immediately after, if you want to run it once and then replay/discuss), have students fill out "What Did I See?" sheets. They should track:
- **What did the AI do?** (in simple words)
- **What tools did it use?**
- **What decisions did it make?**
- **What surprised you?**

Pause the reasoning at key points so students can write. You might replay certain sections to catch details. Circulate and help students with vocabulary: "That's called a 'tool'—it's like a helper the AI uses to solve the problem."

#### Part 3: Observation Discussion (5 minutes)

Gather the class. Ask:

- **"What surprised you about how the AI thought?"**
- **"Did the AI think like YOU think, or differently?"**
- **"What did it do well? What would YOU have done differently?"**

Listen for observations like:
- "It remembered things from the past."
- "It used tools instead of doing it by itself."
- "It made a decision but it didn't have feelings like I do."
- "It was fast!"

Affirm: "You're noticing something really important: software brains and people brains are alike in some ways—they both make plans, remember things, make decisions. But they're different in other ways—like YOU have feelings and creativity in ways the AI does."

### Reflection/Wrap-Up (10 minutes)

Ask students: **"Is this thinking? How do you know?"** Don't give an answer—let them grapple. Then say:

> "Philosophers—people who ask the biggest questions—aren't even sure what 'thinking' means! Is it using tools? Making decisions? Having feelings? For now, I think we can say: the AI is DOING something that LOOKS like thinking. Whether it's the same as human thinking, we can debate!"

Emphasize the habit of **Inquiry**: "We're asking REALLY BIG questions today. That's Inquiry."

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Observation skill:** Can students describe what they saw using terms like "tool," "decision," "region"?
- **Critical thinking:** Do they compare AI thinking to human thinking?
- **Curiosity:** What questions do they ask?
- **Understanding:** Can they track the flow of reasoning, at least partly?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Ask them to predict what will happen next during a reasoning session.
- Have them design a DIFFERENT prompt and predict which regions/tools would activate.
- Challenge them to write one sentence: "This is thinking because _____" and "This is NOT thinking because _____."

**Struggling Learners:**
- Simplify the observation sheet to 3 items: What did it do? What tool did it use? What surprised me?
- Draw pictures on the sheet instead of writing words.
- Pair them with a stronger observer and let them co-create notes.

## Day 5: Gallery Walk & Reflection

### Objective
Students will celebrate and share their Identity designs, practice the habit of Inclusion by giving and receiving compliments, and reflect on their learning from the week.

### Materials Needed
- Student Identity plans (from Day 3) or printed Identity cards from Are-Self
- Large paper or poster board for display
- Sticky notes for compliments
- Colored pencils or markers
- AI journals (notebooks or printed journal sheets)
- Optional: camera or printer to print screen shots of created Identities

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Say: "This week, you've learned SO much. You learned about your brain and the Are-Self brain. You made an Identity. You watched an AI think. Today, we're celebrating everything you made."

Explain the Gallery Walk: "We're going to hang your Identities on the wall, and you're all going to walk around and look at each one. When you see something cool, you write a nice note on a sticky note and stick it on that person's Identity. This is called a 'compliment walk.' Everyone gets to feel proud."

### Main Activity (25–30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Prepare Identities for Display (5 minutes)

Post the Identity planning sheets (or printed versions of created Identities from Are-Self) around the room, on walls or tables. Make sure each one has space for sticky notes.

If you created Identities in Are-Self on Day 3, you might:
- Print out a screenshot of each one and post it, OR
- Project each one briefly so the class sees it, then print a checklist with all the names, OR
- Create a simple "card" with the Identity name and 2–3 key traits.

#### Part 2: Gallery Walk (12–15 minutes)

Play soft background music (optional, but it helps!). Say:

> "I want you to walk around and look at everyone's Identities. Read the names. Look at what they're good at. Think about how they'll practice Inclusion. When something stands out to you—maybe you think the name is cool, or you like the personality, or the tools are clever—write a compliment sticky note and stick it on their Identity."

Provide examples of compliment starters:
- "I like your Identity because _____."
- "Your idea to _____ is clever!"
- "I want to work with _____ because _____."
- "Your Identity is creative because _____."

Let students walk at their own pace. Circulate and help students write compliments if they're stuck. Encourage them to write at least 2–3 compliments.

#### Part 3: Return and Read Compliments (8–10 minutes)

Have students return to their own Identity and read the sticky notes. Say: "Take a moment to read what people wrote about YOUR Identity. How does that feel?"

If time allows, ask 3–4 volunteers: **"What was a compliment you got that made you smile?"**

Affirm: "You all practiced INCLUSION today. You looked for the good in each other's ideas. You included everyone's work in our celebration."

### Reflection/Wrap-Up (10 minutes)

#### Part 1: Whole-Class Reflection (5 minutes)

Gather the class. Ask:

- **"What surprised you this week?"**
- **"What do you want to learn more about?"**
- **"What was your favorite moment?"**

Listen and affirm. Maybe note: "I heard a lot of people say they're curious about the Frontal Lobe. That's INQUIRY—asking questions!"

#### Part 2: AI Journal Entry (5 minutes)

Distribute AI journals (notebooks, journal sheets, or printed templates). Say:

> "In the story, Mira wrote down 'one true thing' she learned. I want you to do that too. In your AI journal, finish this sentence: 'One true thing I learned this week is _____.' Write or draw something that shows what you learned."

Provide a few examples:
- "One true thing I learned this week is that software can have a brain structure like humans do."
- "One true thing I learned this week is that brains have lots of different parts that work together."
- "One true thing I learned this week is that I'm good at asking questions about how things work."

Let students write or draw quietly. Collect journals at the end (or let them keep them if you're using personal notebooks).

### Assessment

Look for:
- **Engagement:** Are students genuinely interested in each other's Identities?
- **Community:** Do the compliments show kindness and Inclusion?
- **Reflection:** Can students articulate what they learned?
- **Growth mindset:** Do they see their own curiosity as strength?

### Differentiation

**Advanced Learners:**
- Have them write 2–3 sentences in their journal entry, explaining WHY something is true.
- Ask them to design a follow-up question based on this week (e.g., "Next week, I want to know _____").

**Struggling Learners:**
- Allow drawings instead of writing in the journal.
- Use a sentence frame for the journal: "One true thing I learned this week is that a brain is _____."
- Pair them with a peer to co-create a journal entry.

## Week 1 Reflection for Teachers

### What Worked Well
- Hands-on drawing and creating keeps 4th graders engaged
- The connection between human brains and software brains resonates; kids see themselves in the AI
- Reading *Mira and the Are-Self* aloud anchors the learning and makes it feel like an adventure
- The Scipraxian habits (Inclusion, Humility, Inquiry) woven throughout give language for what good learning looks like

### Common Questions & Troubleshooting

**Q: My students are confused by the brain region names (PFC, Hypothalamus, etc.)**
A: That's okay! Use simple descriptors. Instead of "PFC," just say "the thinking and planning part." You can introduce the fancy names gradually. Humor helps: "Hypothalamus is a funny word, isn't it? Say it three times fast!"

**Q: Not all my students got to create an Identity in Are-Self. Should I do it later?**
A: Absolutely. The planning sheet is valuable on its own. You can create their Identities during a prep period or have students pair up over multiple sessions. The key is they've DESIGNED something, and that's the real learning.

**Q: What if the Are-Self demo crashes or is slow?**
A: Have a backup screenshot or video clip ready. Or pivot: "Let's imagine what it would do," and have students predict out loud. The reasoning is more important than the live demo.

**Q: My 4th graders think the AI is "alive" now. Is that a problem?**
A: It's a teaching moment! Use it to practice Humility: "We don't fully know what's happening inside Are-Self. It does SOMETHING like thinking, but we're not sure if it's the same as being alive. That's a great question to keep asking!"

### Assets to Prepare Before Week 1 Starts
- Chapter 1 and 2 of *Mira and the Are-Self* (printed or digital)
- Copies of all four worksheets (Brain Region Matching, Brain Region Field Notes, My First Identity, What Did I See?)
- AI journals or blank journal sheets
- Access to Are-Self (tested, logged in)
- Sticky notes for the Gallery Walk
- Large paper and colored pencils for brain maps

### Ongoing Themes to Revisit
- **Inclusion:** Who's speaking? Whose ideas are we hearing? Make space for quiet voices.
- **Humility:** It's okay not to know. Scientists ask questions for years!
- **Inquiry:** Keep asking. Questions are the best part of learning.

## Summary

Week 1 is all about welcome and wonder. Students meet Are-Self as a thinking tool with a brain-like structure. They learn that code and neurons can both be organized into regions. They create something of their own and watch an AI do the same. By the end of the week, students have touched every major region, talked about three big habits, and started thinking like both AI researchers and philosophers.

Next week, they break something intentionally and learn to debug together—the true heart of the Scipraxian way.
