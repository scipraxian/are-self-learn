# Week 4: Science Exploration

## Overview: The Hippocampus Experiment

This week, we flip the script. Rather than following Mira through a chapter, **the Hippocampus (memory system) itself becomes the subject of investigation.** Students use the scientific method to design and run an experiment about how Are-Self learns and remembers. The week cultivates **Inquiry** and **Humility** — the twin habits that underpin all good science: the willingness to ask "one more question" and the wisdom to know that being wrong is part of discovery.

**Primary Habits:** Inquiry + Humility  
**Materials:** Are-Self instance (teacher or shared), "Experiment Design" worksheet, "Data Collection" worksheet, poster paper or digital document template  
**Why This Matters:** Science isn't a set of facts to memorize. Science is a *way of thinking* — controlled testing, careful observation, and honest analysis of results. By designing their own experiment, students learn that they themselves can do science. And when they ask Are-Self for help designing the procedure, they see the AI as a thinking partner, not an oracle.

## Day 1: What Is the Scientific Method?

**Objective:** Students understand that the scientific method formalizes the habit of inquiry. They connect Mira's "one more question" to the way scientists actually work.

**Materials:**  
- Whiteboard or chart paper  
- Are-Self instance for optional warm-up conversation  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Display this question on the board or screen:

> "What would happen if we taught the AI a wrong fact? Would it remember it?"

Give students 1–2 minutes to think-pair-share. Then ask: "How could we actually TEST that idea? What would we need to do?"

Jot down student ideas on the board. Look for phrases like "teach it," "test it," "check if it works," "try it again."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

Introduce the scientific method as the **formal way to ask questions and test ideas.** Write the five steps on the board:

1. **Question:** What do you want to know?  
2. **Hypothesis:** What do you *think* will happen? (This is a guess based on what you already know.)  
3. **Experiment:** The fair test you design to answer the question.  
4. **Data:** What you observe or measure. The facts you collect.  
5. **Conclusion:** What your data tells you. Was your hypothesis right?

**Teacher Script:**

> "When Mira asks 'one more question,' she's doing something that scientists do all the time. Scientists are curious, just like Mira. But scientists also have a system for testing their curiosity so they don't believe wrong stuff. That system is the scientific method. Let's look at an example together."

Example on the board:

| Step | Our Example |
|------|---|
| **Question** | If we teach the AI a wrong fact, will it remember it as true? |
| **Hypothesis** | The AI will probably remember it as true because it doesn't know better. |
| **Experiment** | Teach it a made-up fact. Wait. Ask it the same question and see what it says. |
| **Data** | Did the AI say the made-up fact? Yes or no? How did it phrase the answer? |
| **Conclusion** | The AI remembered the wrong fact / caught the mistake / gave a mixed answer. |

Work through another example together. (Example: "If we ask the AI the same question two different ways, will it give the same answer?" Design how you'd test that. What data would you collect?)

Then, return to the warm-up question: "How could we test whether the AI remembers wrong facts?" Have students sketch a rough experiment on paper — not perfect, just the basic steps. Circulate and ask follow-up questions: "How will you know if it worked? What will you measure?"

**Reflection (10 minutes)**

Invite 3–4 students to share their rough ideas. Affirm the thinking: "I like how you're planning to test it the same way twice so you can compare."

Ask the class: "Why is a hypothesis a guess and not a promise? What's the difference?"

Listen for: "A hypothesis is what you think might happen. The experiment tells you what REALLY happens. Sometimes you're wrong, and that's okay — that's actually how science works."

**Assessment**

- Listen for students using the five-step language: question, hypothesis, experiment, data, conclusion.
- Do students understand that the experiment is the part where you *test* the hypothesis (not just observe)?
- Can they name what "data" means in the context of this week's work? (What you actually see or measure.)

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide a sentence frame for the hypothesis: "I think the AI will _____ because _____."
- **Advanced:** Ask students to design an experiment for a *different* question about how Are-Self learns. Example: "If we ask the AI a question about something we taught it yesterday, will it still remember?"

## Day 2: Design the Experiment

**Objective:** Students work in pairs or small groups to design their own experiment using Are-Self as the subject (and as a thinking partner).

**Materials:**  
- "Experiment Design" worksheet (provided below)  
- Are-Self instance  
- Pencils and paper for drafting  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Remind students: "Yesterday we learned the five steps of the scientific method. Today, YOU are the scientists. You're going to design a real experiment."

Quick review: Ask three students, one each: "What's a hypothesis?" "What's data?" "Why do we test twice instead of once?"

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: The Setup (10 minutes)**

Explain that each pair or group will teach Are-Self a *single fact* (either true or made-up). Some groups will teach a TRUE fact. Some groups will teach a WRONG fact on purpose. This variety will make the class data more interesting.

Divide the class into groups of 2–3. Assign or let groups choose:

- **Group 1:** Teach a true fact about animals  
- **Group 2:** Teach a true fact about geography  
- **Group 3:** Teach a wrong fact (e.g., "Penguins live in the North Pole")  
- **Group 4:** Teach a wrong fact (e.g., "Paris is in Spain")  
- etc.

Give each group the "Experiment Design" worksheet:

---

**Part 2: Getting Help from Are-Self (10 minutes)**

Here's where the magic happens. Tell students: "You can ask Are-Self to help you design your experiment. That's okay. Scientists ask for help too."

**Sample scripts for students to use with Are-Self:**

> "We're designing an experiment to see if you remember facts. Does our plan make sense? Should we ask the question the same way we taught the fact, or differently?"

> "We want to teach you that [our fact]. What's the best way for us to do that so you really learn it?"

> "If we teach you something, wait five minutes, and then ask you a new question, will that be a good test?"

Circulate while groups work. Help groups refine their procedures. Nudge them toward clarity: "Is that exactly how you'll say the question? Will the AI understand?"

**Teacher Script (as you circulate):**

> "I like how you're testing whether the AI remembers EXACTLY or just the general idea. That's real science. Let's make sure you write down exactly what you're going to teach it so it's fair."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Bring the group back together. Ask each group: "What's one thing you're curious to find out?" or "Are you nervous about your experiment? What might go wrong?"

Normalize uncertainty: "Scientists don't know what will happen. That's why they run the experiment. If we knew everything already, we wouldn't need to test."

**Assessment**

- Does each group have a clear question they can test?
- Does the hypothesis make sense as a prediction?
- Is the procedure detailed enough that another group could follow it?
- Are students thinking about what counts as "data"?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide a template with some steps already filled in. Pairs with an adult or peer mentor.
- **Advanced:** Ask these groups to design a *control* experiment. Example: "Your test teaches the AI a fact. For the control, you ask it a fact WITHOUT teaching it first. Compare the results."

## Day 3: Run It

**Objective:** Students execute their experiments. They teach Are-Self a fact, wait, query it, and record their data carefully.

**Materials:**  
- "Data Collection" worksheet (provided below)  
- Are-Self instance  
- Group experiment designs from Day 2  
- Pens/pencils  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Quick check-in: "Today we're actually doing the experiment. Remember: the data is what you SEE and HEAR the AI say. Write it down exactly, even if it's a little messy or different from what you expected. Scientists write down what REALLY happens, not what they wanted to happen."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

Students execute their experiments in order. Depending on your setup:

- **One shared computer:** Groups take turns at the board or screen, running their experiment while the rest of the class observes or works on a related task.
- **Multiple devices:** Groups work simultaneously.

**The Flow:**

1. **Teach the AI the fact.** Group member reads the fact aloud or types it into Are-Self, exactly as written on their design worksheet.

   *Example:* "Did you know that the mantis shrimp can see more colors than any other animal?"

2. **Wait.** Set a timer. 2–5 minutes is enough. (The "wait" mimics how long we might wait before remembering something.)

3. **Ask the question.** Group asks Are-Self a related question, designed to see if it recalls the fact.

   *Example:* "Which animal can see the most colors?" or "Tell me about mantis shrimp vision."

4. **Record the response.** Write down or screenshot exactly what Are-Self says.

**Teacher Moves During Execution:**

- Encourage precision: "Write down what it actually said, even if it's long."
- If an experiment doesn't work as planned, that's DATA: "The AI didn't remember it. That's interesting! Write that down."
- If a group finishes early, ask: "Want to teach it a second fact and test again? Or ask a follow-up question?"

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

When groups finish, invite quick shares: "What surprised you?" or "Did your hypothesis come true?"

Affirm surprises: "I love that the AI did something different from what you expected. That's exactly what scientists find out when they run experiments."

**Assessment**

- Is the data written down clearly? (Can you read what the AI actually said?)
- Did the group answer the question they designed in Day 2?
- Are students recording honest results, not just what they expected?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide a scribe (peer or adult). Simplify the question to yes/no.
- **Advanced:** Ask students to re-run the experiment with slight variations and compare. "What if you ask it a week later?" or "What if you phrase the question very differently?"

## Day 4: Analyze & Discuss

**Objective:** Students look at the data from all groups, identify patterns, and discuss what the results mean. This is where the science becomes sense-making.

**Materials:**  
- All completed "Data Collection" worksheets  
- Whiteboard or chart for tallying results  
- Are-Self instance for pattern-finding (optional)  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Display the question: "Look at all the data we collected. Did the AI always remember? Did it remember true facts better than made-up facts?"

Quick think-pair-share. Then say: "Today we're going to figure out what our experiment teaches us."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

**Part 1: Compile the Results (8 minutes)**

Create a table on the board or chart paper:

| Group | Fact (True/Made-Up) | Did AI Remember? | How |
|-------|-----|-------|------|
| Group 1 | True | Yes | Said it back exactly |
| Group 2 | Made-Up | Yes | Accepted it as true |
| Group 3 | True | Partly | Remembered the topic but gave different details |
| etc. | | | |

As each group reports, fill in the table. This is the visual summary of your class data.

**Part 2: Look for Patterns (10 minutes)**

Ask guiding questions:

> "Look at the 'True' facts. Did the AI remember them? All of them? Most of them?"

> "Look at the 'Made-Up' facts. What happened with those? Did the AI catch that they were wrong?"

> "Did the AI remember EXACTLY what we taught it, or close to it?"

> "Which facts did the AI remember best? Why do you think?"

Jot down student observations:

- "The AI remembered the true facts better."
- "It caught one wrong fact but not the other."
- "It gave back the idea but with different words."
- "It seemed unsure about one and added extra information."

**Part 3: The Big Question — Humility (10 minutes)**

Introduce the humility component gently:

**Teacher Script:**

> "Here's something really interesting. The AI remembered some made-up facts without catching that they were wrong. That means the AI doesn't always KNOW when it's wrong. It can't fact-check itself perfectly. And you know what? Neither can humans. We believe things that aren't true sometimes. That's why science is so important—we test our ideas instead of just trusting our gut."

Ask: "What does this teach us about being wrong?"

Listen for and affirm:

- "Being wrong is not bad—it's how we learn."
- "Even smart things (like AIs) can be wrong."
- "We need to check our facts."
- "It's okay to not know everything."

**Optional: Use Are-Self as Sense-Maker**

If you have time and access, ask the AI a question about the data:

**Teacher or student to Are-Self:**

> "We taught you some facts and some made-up facts. Some you remembered, some you didn't. What patterns do you notice in your results? Why do you think you remember some things better than others?"

The AI might offer insights like: "I remember well-established facts better than novel or unusual claims" or "I tend to accept information that's presented to me as fact."

Then ask: "Does that mean you're not perfect at remembering?"

Validate that the AI is willing to admit limitations: "Notice how the AI just admitted it's not perfect at remembering. That takes humility."

**Reflection (5 minutes)**

Ask the class: "What would a scientist do next? If we wanted to figure out WHY the AI remembered some facts better, what would we test?"

Students might suggest: "Try facts about the same topic," "Use harder words," "Ask the question in a really different way," "Wait longer," "Teach it the same fact twice."

Affirm: "Great ideas. That's what real scientists do—one experiment leads to another question."

**Assessment**

- Can students identify a pattern in the data? (Not every student will find the same pattern, and that's okay.)
- Do they understand that "the AI didn't remember" or "the AI got it wrong" is valid data?
- Are students connecting the results to the bigger idea about knowing and not-knowing?

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Focus on one simple comparison: "True or made-up—which did the AI remember better?" Count hands or tally.
- **Advanced:** Ask students to write a short hypothesis about WHY the AI remembered some things better: "I think the AI remembers facts about animals better because…"

## Day 5: Science Fair Mini-Poster

**Objective:** Students communicate their experiment and results in a written format. They create a one-page poster (digital or physical) showing the full scientific method in action.

**Materials:**  
- Poster paper or digital document template (Google Docs, Word, etc.)  
- Markers, colored pencils, or digital tools  
- Are-Self instance (optional, for research)  
- Gallery walk space  

**Warm-Up (5 minutes)**

Show the class a real science fair poster or a simple template that shows the five sections:

1. **Question**  
2. **Hypothesis**  
3. **Procedure**  
4. **Data / Results**  
5. **Conclusion**

**Teacher Script:**

> "Scientists don't just run experiments and keep the results to themselves. They share their work. Today, you're going to create a poster that shows your whole experiment—the question you asked, what you thought would happen, what you actually did, what you found out, and what it means. This is how real scientists tell the world about their discoveries."

**Main Activity (25–30 minutes)**

Each group creates a one-page poster. They can use:

- **Poster paper** with sections and handwriting/markers.
- **Google Docs or Word** with a template they fill in.
- **Canva** or a similar design tool if your school has access.

**Poster Sections:**

**SECTION 1: QUESTION**
"What question did you ask?"

*Example:* "If we teach Are-Self a wrong fact, will it remember it as true?"

**SECTION 2: HYPOTHESIS**
"What did you think would happen?"

*Example:* "We thought the AI would remember it as true because it doesn't have a way to check facts."

**SECTION 3: PROCEDURE**
"What did you do? Write the steps in order."

*Example:*
1. We taught Are-Self: "Penguins live in the North Pole."
2. We waited 3 minutes.
3. We asked: "Where do penguins live?"
4. We wrote down what it said.

**SECTION 4: DATA / RESULTS**
"What happened? What did you find out?"

*Example:* "The AI answered: 'Penguins live in the Southern Hemisphere, primarily in the Antarctic region.' It corrected our wrong fact."

*Students can include the AI's exact response, a quote, or a summary.*

**SECTION 5: CONCLUSION**
"Was your hypothesis right? What does this teach us?"

*Example:* "Our hypothesis was wrong. The AI actually CAUGHT the wrong fact and corrected it. This teaches us that the AI is pretty good at checking facts about animals."

**Optional: BONUS SECTION**
"What would you test next? What's another question you have?"

**Teacher Script (as students work):**

> "Your poster doesn't have to be fancy. It just has to be clear. If I read it, I should understand exactly what you asked, what you did, and what you found out."

**Are-Self as Research Assistant (Optional)**

If a group finishes early or wants to add background information, they can ask Are-Self:

> "We taught you that [fact]. Does this fact actually matter? Why would a scientist care about whether you remember it?"

Or: "What is known about [topic]? We want to understand our results better."

**Reflection & Gallery Walk (10 minutes, can extend to Day 5 afternoon/next day)**

**Gallery Walk:**

- Post all posters on the walls or project them on screen.
- Students walk around silently, reading each poster.
- They leave sticky notes with one positive comment: "I like how you…" or "I was surprised that…"

**Whole-Group Share (5 minutes):**

Bring students back together. Ask:

> "Which experiment surprised you most?"

> "Which group's hypothesis was totally wrong? How did that change how they thought about the AI?"

> "What was the coolest thing you learned about how Are-Self learns?"

Affirm the full spectrum of results: "Some of you found out the AI is really good at remembering. Others found out it can be fooled. Both are important discoveries."

**Assessment**

- **Poster Completeness:** Does the poster have all five sections?
- **Clarity:** Can someone who didn't do the experiment understand what was tested?
- **Accuracy:** Are the results written honestly (not prettied up)?
- **Reflection:** Does the conclusion show thinking, not just reporting results?

**Rubric (Simple 1–4 scale):**

| Criterion | 1 (Needs Work) | 2 (Developing) | 3 (Proficient) | 4 (Advanced) |
|-----------|---|---|---|---|
| **Question** | Missing or unclear | Present but vague | Clear and testable | Clear, specific, and interesting |
| **Hypothesis** | Missing | Guess, no reasoning | Prediction with reasoning | Prediction with scientific reasoning |
| **Procedure** | Vague or incomplete | Most steps present | Clear, step-by-step | Clear with details about variables |
| **Data** | Missing or very incomplete | Some data recorded | All data recorded clearly | Data organized and complete |
| **Conclusion** | Missing | Restates data | Explains what data means | Explains meaning and reflects on why |

**Differentiation**

- **Struggling:** Provide a poster template with blanks and sentence starters: "Our question was…" "We predicted that…"
- **Advanced:** Ask students to include one sentence about the *limitations* of their experiment: "One thing we could have done better is…" or "If we did this again, we would…"

## Week 4 Reflection & Connection

**For the AI Journal (end of week):**

Students write or draw (3–5 sentences/sketches):

> "What surprised me most about what the AI could remember?"
> 
> "Did the AI's results change how I think about it? How?"
> 
> "What's one more question I have about how the AI learns?"

**Connection to Inquiry + Humility:**

Wrap the week by connecting back to Mira and the are-self habit:

> "This week, you were all like Mira. You asked 'one more question'—actually, lots of questions. You designed an experiment to find out if your guesses were right. And you learned something important: sometimes you're wrong, and that's how science works. Being humble means admitting you don't know everything. Being curious means asking questions anyway. Together, those habits make a good scientist."

## Teacher Notes

- **Tech Flexibility:** This week works whether you have one shared computer or multiple devices. If shared, each group's turn becomes a mini-demo for the class.
- **Are-Self as Partner, Not Oracle:** The key is positioning the AI as a lab partner who has limitations. It's not a source of Truth from on High—it's another experimental subject and a thinking partner.
- **Embrace Wrong Results:** If the AI does something totally unexpected, celebrate it. Wrong results teach us something. That's the whole point.
- **Humility Is Gentle:** Don't hammer the "being wrong is good" message. Let the experiment itself teach it. Students will see it when the data shows the AI was fooled or when their hypothesis didn't come true.
- **Assessment Is Low-Stakes:** These worksheets and posters are formative. The goal is thinking and testing, not perfection. Mistakes are data.
