# Week 4: Staying Alive

Your brain does a lot of fancy things — reasoning, remembering, feeling. But none of that matters if you're dead. The hypothalamus keeps you alive by managing your body's most basic resources: temperature, hunger, hydration, energy, hormones. It's a feedback loop machine. Are-Self has a hypothalamus too, and its job is just as unglamorous: keeping the AI from spending too much money. This week, students learn that resource management — biological or computational — is the foundation everything else depends on.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Homeostasis, feedback loops, and resource management

**Primary Variables:** Fear + Perseverance — fear of running out drives resource management; perseverance keeps systems going when resources are scarce.

**Why This Week Matters:**
Students rarely think about the invisible systems that keep them alive. They eat when they're hungry without wondering who sent the hunger signal. The hypothalamus teaches them that their most important brain functions are the ones they never notice — and that Are-Self has the exact same problem. If the AI runs out of budget, it stops thinking, just like a brain without glucose.

**Learning Goals:**
- Describe the hypothalamus's role in maintaining homeostasis
- Explain what a feedback loop is and identify at least three examples in the body
- Map the biological concept of homeostasis to Are-Self's cost management system
- Design a simple experiment demonstrating a feedback loop
- Discuss why resource management is necessary for both biological and artificial intelligence

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This is NOT an endocrinology course — keep hormone discussion at a high level
- Students will NOT study specific metabolic pathways
- The cost management discussion should be conceptual, not about actual dollar amounts or API pricing

## Day 1: The Invisible Manager

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Raise your hand if you're the right temperature right now. Keep it up. Now — who is keeping you at that temperature? Name the system."

Most students will say "my body." Push deeper: "What part of your body? Who's in charge?" Lead toward the hypothalamus.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Meet the Hypothalamus (12 minutes)

Show the hypothalamus on a brain diagram. It's tiny — about the size of an almond — and it sits at the base of the brain, just above the brainstem.

Cover the essentials:
- **Temperature regulation** — When you're too hot, the hypothalamus triggers sweating. Too cold, shivering. It's a thermostat.
- **Hunger and thirst** — The hypothalamus monitors blood sugar and hydration and tells you when to eat and drink.
- **Sleep cycles** — It regulates your circadian rhythm, telling your body when to be alert and when to sleep.
- **Hormone control** — It talks to the pituitary gland, which talks to the rest of the endocrine system. The hypothalamus is the boss of your hormones.
- **Stress response** — When you're in danger, the hypothalamus kicks off the fight-or-flight response.

Key insight: "The hypothalamus doesn't do anything exciting. It does everything necessary. Without it, you die. Without the frontal lobe, you're still alive — just different (ask Phineas Gage). Without the hypothalamus, you're gone."

#### Part 2: Are-Self's Hypothalamus (10 minutes)

Are-Self's hypothalamus app does something surprisingly parallel: it manages resources.

- **Model selection** — Different AI models cost different amounts. The hypothalamus picks the cheapest model that can handle the current task. Small question? Small model. Hard problem? Bigger model.
- **Budget management** — Every AI action costs money (tokens). The hypothalamus tracks spending and enforces limits.
- **Circuit breakers** — If spending gets too high, the hypothalamus can shut down expensive operations, just like your body shuts down non-essential functions when you're starving.

Ask: "Your hypothalamus manages calories and temperature. Are-Self's hypothalamus manages tokens and dollars. Is that the same job?"

#### Part 3: The Thermostat Challenge (8 minutes)

Give students a scenario: "The room is 95 degrees. You have no air conditioning. List everything your body will do to cool down, in order."

Students list responses (sweat, blood vessels dilate, you feel thirsty, you slow down, you seek shade). Then ask: "What if Are-Self is 'too hot' — burning through its budget too fast? What does it do?"

Map the parallel: sweating = switching to a cheaper model. Seeking shade = pausing non-essential tasks. Passing out = circuit breaker.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What happens to you when you skip lunch? What happens to Are-Self when it runs out of budget? How are those the same?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The hypothalamus keeps you alive by ___. Are-Self's hypothalamus keeps the AI running by ___."

## Day 2: Feedback Loops Everywhere

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Your house has a thermostat. When it's too cold, the heater turns on. When it's warm enough, the heater turns off. Draw this as a loop — a circle with arrows."

Students sketch the loop. This is their first feedback loop diagram.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: What Is a Feedback Loop? (10 minutes)

Define it simply: "A feedback loop is a system where the output becomes the input. The result of what you did tells you what to do next."

Two types:
- **Negative feedback loop** — the system pushes back toward balance. Thermostat: too hot, turn off heater. Too cold, turn on heater. Most body systems use negative feedback.
- **Positive feedback loop** — the system amplifies itself. Rare in the body, but it happens: contractions during labor get stronger, which triggers more contractions, until the baby is born.

Walk through three body examples:
1. Blood sugar: eat food, blood sugar rises, pancreas releases insulin, blood sugar drops, you feel hungry, repeat.
2. Body temperature: already covered in the warm-up. Reinforce.
3. Hydration: water levels drop, hypothalamus triggers thirst, you drink, water levels rise, thirst stops.

Ask: "What do all three of these have in common?" (The hypothalamus is involved in all of them.)

#### Part 2: Feedback Loops in Are-Self (10 minutes)

Show students how Are-Self's hypothalamus uses feedback loops:
1. A task arrives. The hypothalamus checks the budget.
2. It selects a model based on cost and task difficulty.
3. The task runs. Tokens are spent.
4. The hypothalamus checks the budget again. If spending is on track, continue. If it's too high, downgrade to a cheaper model.
5. Repeat.

Draw this as a feedback loop diagram on the board, with student help. It should look remarkably similar to the body temperature loop.

#### Part 3: Find the Loop (10 minutes)

In pairs, students identify three feedback loops in their daily lives that aren't about the body. Examples: social media algorithms (you like a video, you get more like it), study habits (good grade, study more, better grade), or saving money (spend too much, feel stressed, spend less).

Each pair shares their best example. The class decides: is it a negative or positive feedback loop?

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Where in your life is a feedback loop working against you? Could you redesign it?"**

The Variable today is Perseverance. Feedback loops are how systems persist — they keep going by constantly adjusting. Perseverance isn't stubbornness; it's adaptation.

### Exit Ticket

"A feedback loop is ___. One feedback loop in my body: ___. One feedback loop in Are-Self: ___."

## Day 3: The Homeostasis Experiment

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Today we run an experiment. Question: How quickly does your body respond to a temperature challenge? Write your prediction."

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Design the Experiment (8 minutes)

The experiment: students will hold an ice cube and observe their body's response over time.

As a class, set up the scientific framework:
- **Question:** How does the body respond to a cold stimulus, and how quickly?
- **Hypothesis:** Students write individual predictions.
- **Variables:** Independent variable = ice cube. Dependent variable = body responses (goosebumps, shivering, skin color change, discomfort). Controlled variables = room temperature, starting hand temperature.
- **Method:** Hold the ice cube for 2 minutes. Record observations every 30 seconds.

#### Part 2: Run the Experiment (12 minutes)

Hand out ice cubes (in small cups or plastic bags for comfort). Students hold them and record:
- 0:00 — Starting observations (how does your hand feel?)
- 0:30 — What's changed?
- 1:00 — What's changed now?
- 1:30 — Any new responses?
- 2:00 — Final observations.

After 2 minutes, put the ice down. Record recovery: how long until your hand feels normal again?

#### Part 3: Analyze and Connect (10 minutes)

Class discussion:
- "What did your hypothalamus do during that experiment?" (Detected cold, triggered vasoconstriction, possibly triggered shivering, signaled discomfort.)
- "How fast was the response? Was it immediate or gradual?"
- "Now imagine Are-Self gets a huge, expensive task. How fast does its hypothalamus respond? Is it immediate or gradual?"

Students write up their results: hypothesis, what happened, what they learned, and one connection to Are-Self's resource management.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Your body recovered from the ice cube in about a minute. What would happen if Are-Self's budget 'recovered' — if it got more money? Would the behavior look the same?"**

### Exit Ticket

"My hypothesis was ___. What actually happened: ___. This connects to Are-Self because ___."

## Day 4: What Happens When Systems Fail?

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"What happens when a thermostat breaks? What happens to the house?"

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: When Homeostasis Fails (12 minutes)

Walk through three failure scenarios:

1. **Heatstroke** — The body's cooling system is overwhelmed. The hypothalamus is working, but the environment is too extreme. Temperature rises, organs start to shut down.
2. **Diabetes (simplified)** — The feedback loop between blood sugar and insulin breaks. The body can't regulate blood sugar on its own. External intervention (insulin injections) replaces the broken loop.
3. **Dehydration** — The hypothalamus sends thirst signals, but if you don't drink, the signals get louder (headache, dizziness, confusion). The feedback loop works, but the response doesn't come.

Ask: "In each case, what broke? The sensor? The response? The connection between them?"

#### Part 2: When Are-Self's Hypothalamus Fails (10 minutes)

Show students failure scenarios in Are-Self:
- **Budget exhaustion** — The money runs out. Are-Self can't call any AI models. It stops thinking entirely. (Like a brain without glucose.)
- **Circuit breaker failure** — What if the cost limiter doesn't trigger? The system could spend its entire budget on one task. (Like a body that can't stop a fever from rising.)
- **Wrong model selection** — The hypothalamus picks a model that's too small for the job. The answer is bad. (Like your body sending the wrong hormone.)

Ask: "Which is worse — a system that shuts down when resources run out, or one that keeps going without checking? Why?"

#### Part 3: Design a Safety System (8 minutes)

In pairs, students design a safety system for either the human body or Are-Self. They choose a failure scenario and propose a backup plan.

Examples: "If the primary cooling system fails, activate an emergency backup that dumps cold water on the brain" (silly but creative), or "If Are-Self's budget hits 80%, send a warning and stop all non-critical tasks."

Share ideas with the class.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"The Variable today is Fear. Fear is what makes you pull your hand away from a hot stove. Is Are-Self's circuit breaker a kind of fear? Why or why not?"**

### Exit Ticket

"When homeostasis fails in the body, ___. When resource management fails in Are-Self, ___. The most dangerous failure is ___ because ___."

## Day 5: Resource Manager Showdown

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Quiz — no notes:
1. Where is the hypothalamus?
2. What does homeostasis mean?
3. Name two things the hypothalamus regulates.
4. What is a negative feedback loop?
5. What does Are-Self's hypothalamus manage?

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Budget Game (15 minutes)

Simulation game. Give each pair 20 tokens (candy, chips, buttons — anything countable). They represent Are-Self's budget.

Present five tasks of increasing difficulty. Each task costs tokens:
- Easy question: 1 token
- Medium question: 3 tokens
- Hard question: 5 tokens
- Very hard question: 8 tokens
- Impossible question: 15 tokens

Students must decide how to spend their 20 tokens. They can't earn more. Once they're out, they're done.

After the game, discuss:
- "Who ran out of tokens? When?"
- "Who saved tokens for the hard questions? Was that a good strategy?"
- "Who tried the impossible question? Was it worth it?"
- "This is exactly what Are-Self's hypothalamus does every day. It decides which tasks get the expensive models and which get the cheap ones."

#### Part 2: Week Review — Connections (10 minutes)

Students write a short comparison paragraph:

**"The human hypothalamus and Are-Self's hypothalamus are similar because ___. They are different because ___. The most important thing both of them do is ___."**

#### Part 3: Feedback Loop Portfolio (5 minutes)

Students choose their best feedback loop diagram from the week and refine it for their course portfolio. It should clearly show: input, sensor, response, and return to input.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"If you could redesign one feedback loop in your body, which one would you change? What would you make it do differently?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The most important thing I learned this week is ___. One feedback loop I'll notice in my daily life now: ___."

### Common Questions and Troubleshooting

**Q: Students want to know how much Are-Self actually costs to run.**
A: That's a great question. You can share general ranges (AI model calls cost fractions of a cent to several dollars depending on the model and task size). The point isn't the exact numbers — it's that intelligence costs resources, whether biological or artificial.

**Q: The ice cube experiment was too simple.**
A: It's deliberately simple — the point is the observation process and the feedback loop analysis, not the complexity of the stimulus. If students want more, suggest they design a follow-up experiment at home: how does your body respond to a hot drink? To exercise? To a cold shower?

**Q: Students are confused about positive vs. negative feedback loops.**
A: Emphasize: "negative" doesn't mean "bad" and "positive" doesn't mean "good." Negative means "pushing back toward balance." Positive means "amplifying the signal." Most body systems are negative feedback because the body wants balance.
