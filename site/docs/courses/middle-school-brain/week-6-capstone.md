# Week 6: Where Does It Break?

This is the payoff. For five weeks, students have been learning about brains and AI side by side — memory, reasoning, resource management, communication. Now they pick the one spot where the comparison falls apart, build an argument, and present it. This is a scaffolded version of the HS Bio capstone: more support, more structure, same intellectual demand.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Capstone — finding the gap, building the argument, presenting the case

**Primary Variables:** All twelve. This is where they come together.

**Why This Week Matters:**
Knowing facts is not the same as having a position. This week, students transition from learning about the brain-AI comparison to making a claim about it. They argue. They defend. They listen to each other's arguments and decide whether they're convinced. This is the highest-order thinking in the course, and it's built on everything that came before.

**Learning Goals:**
- Identify a specific gap in the brain-AI metaphor and articulate why it matters
- Gather evidence from the course (experiments, observations, discussions) to support a claim
- Build a structured argument with a claim, evidence, and reasoning
- Present the argument clearly to peers in under 3 minutes
- Evaluate classmates' arguments and provide constructive feedback
- Reflect on how their thinking changed across six weeks

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This is NOT a test — students are building and presenting original arguments, not recalling facts
- There are NO wrong topics — any genuine gap in the metaphor is valid
- Students do NOT need to solve the gap — identifying and explaining it is enough

## Day 1: Choose Your Gap

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Return the "First Impression" paragraphs from Week 1, Day 5. Students read their own writing silently.

"You wrote this five weeks ago. Has anything changed? Take 60 seconds and underline anything you'd say differently now."

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Gap Gallery (10 minutes)

Put these prompts on the board — one per poster or section of whiteboard. Students walk around and add sticky notes with their thoughts:

1. **Memory:** What can human memory do that Are-Self's memory can't? (False memories, emotional memory, forgetting on purpose)
2. **Thinking:** What can human reasoning do that Are-Self's reasoning can't? (Emotion in decisions, creativity, intuition)
3. **Survival:** How is biological homeostasis different from budget management? (The body can die. Can Are-Self die?)
4. **Communication:** How is neural communication different from software communication? (Speed, redundancy, error types)
5. **Identity:** What makes human identity different from AI identity? (Growth, experience, the relationship between memory and self)
6. **Something else:** Is there a gap the course didn't cover?

Give students 10 minutes to add notes and read what others have written.

#### Part 2: Pick Your Topic (10 minutes)

Each student chooses one gap to focus on for their capstone. They write:
- **My gap:** In one sentence, what is the gap?
- **Why it matters:** In one sentence, why should anyone care?
- **My early evidence:** What did we do in class that supports this?

Students share their topics with a neighbor. The neighbor's job: ask one clarifying question. "What do you mean by ___?" or "How would you prove that?"

#### Part 3: Research and Evidence Planning (10 minutes)

Students begin an evidence organizer:

| Evidence Source | What I Observed / Learned | How It Supports My Gap |
|----------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Week __ activity | | |
| Week __ experiment | | |
| Week __ discussion | | |

They fill in at least three rows. This becomes the backbone of their argument.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Is your gap something that might be fixed someday, or something that will always be true? How do you know?"**

### Exit Ticket

"My capstone gap: ___. I chose it because ___."

## Day 2: Build Your Argument

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"What makes a good argument? Not a fight — a logical argument. What does it need?"

Collect answers on the board. Guide toward: a clear claim, evidence that supports it, reasoning that connects the evidence to the claim.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Argument Framework (10 minutes)

Teach the CER framework (Claim, Evidence, Reasoning):

- **Claim:** One clear statement about your gap. "The brain-AI metaphor breaks down when it comes to ___ because ___."
- **Evidence:** Specific observations, experiment results, or facts from the course that support your claim.
- **Reasoning:** The explanation of WHY your evidence supports your claim. This is the hard part.

Model it with an example: "The brain-AI metaphor breaks down when it comes to fear. Evidence: Are-Self's hypothalamus manages costs, but it doesn't feel afraid of running out. In our ice cube experiment, we felt discomfort — a signal that drove us to act. Are-Self's circuit breaker is a rule, not a feeling. Reasoning: In the biological brain, fear isn't just information — it's motivation. Without the feeling, the hypothalamus is a calculator, not a survival system."

#### Part 2: Draft Your CER (15 minutes)

Students write their full argument using the CER framework. Circulate and help. Common issues:
- Claim is too vague ("AI can't think") — push for specificity ("AI can't make decisions that involve weighing emotional consequences")
- Evidence is from outside the course — redirect to class activities and experiments
- Reasoning is just restating the evidence — ask "Why does this evidence matter? What does it prove?"

#### Part 3: Peer Review (5 minutes)

Trade papers with a partner. Partners read and mark:
- Is the claim clear? (Yes / Needs work)
- Is there enough evidence? (Yes / Needs more)
- Does the reasoning connect? (Yes / I'm not convinced because ___)

Students get their papers back and note what to fix.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What's the difference between an argument and an opinion?"**

An opinion is "I think." An argument is "I think, and here's why, based on what I observed." That difference is the heart of Inquiry.

### Exit Ticket

"My claim: ___. My strongest piece of evidence: ___."

## Day 3: Strengthen and Practice

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Today you make your argument bulletproof. Here's the test: if someone in this class can destroy your argument with one question, it's not ready."

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Counter-Argument (10 minutes)

Every good argument anticipates the opposition. Students write one counter-argument to their own claim — the strongest thing someone could say to disagree.

Then they write a response to that counter-argument. "Someone might say ___, but I would respond ___ because ___."

This is hard for middle schoolers. Help them. "If you say AI can't feel fear, someone might say 'but the circuit breaker acts like fear — it avoids danger.' How would you respond?"

#### Part 2: Presentation Prep (12 minutes)

Students prepare a 2-3 minute presentation. They can use:
- A poster or visual aid
- Note cards (not a script — they should talk, not read)
- Their brain map from any week as a prop

Structure their presentation:
1. State the gap (15 seconds)
2. Explain why it matters (15 seconds)
3. Present evidence (60 seconds)
4. Explain the reasoning (45 seconds)
5. Address a counter-argument (30 seconds)
6. Closing statement (15 seconds)

#### Part 3: Practice Rounds (8 minutes)

In pairs, students present to each other. The partner's job: ask one hard question after the presentation. The presenter practices answering under pressure.

Switch roles and repeat.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What Variable did you practice most while building your argument? Was it Inquiry? Perseverance? Humility? Responsibility?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The part of my presentation I'm most confident about: ___. The part I'm most worried about: ___."

## Day 4: Presentations (Part 1)

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Today, half the class presents. Tomorrow, the other half. Ground rules:"

1. Listen actively. No side conversations.
2. After each presentation, one question from the audience.
3. Questions should be genuine — trying to understand, not trying to embarrass.
4. If you disagree, say "I see it differently because ___" not "you're wrong."
5. Every presenter gets applause. Presenting is brave.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Presentations: First Half of Class (30 minutes)

Each presenter gets 3 minutes for their argument plus 1 minute for one audience question.

With 3-minute presentations and 1-minute Q&A, you can fit 7-8 presentations in 30 minutes.

**Teacher role during presentations:**
- Keep time strictly. Hold up a "30 seconds left" card.
- Note strong arguments for the class discussion on Day 5.
- Note common themes across presentations.
- If a question is too aggressive, redirect: "Can you rephrase that as a genuine question?"

**Assessment notes:** Track each presenter on:
- Clarity of claim
- Quality of evidence (from course activities)
- Strength of reasoning
- Response to the audience question
- Confidence and communication

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What's one argument you heard today that made you think differently? What was convincing about it?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The most convincing argument I heard today was about ___. It convinced me because ___."

## Day 5: Presentations (Part 2) and Course Wrap-Up

### Warm-Up (3 minutes)

"Same rules as yesterday. Let's hear from the rest of the class."

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Presentations: Second Half of Class (18 minutes)

Remaining presenters. Same format: 3 minutes plus 1-minute Q&A.

#### Class Discussion: The Big Picture (12 minutes)

After all presentations, facilitate a closing discussion:

"You've now heard every gap your class found in the brain-AI metaphor. Let's talk about what that means."

Questions to drive the discussion:
- "Was there a gap that everyone seemed to agree on? What was it?"
- "Was there a gap that surprised you — something you hadn't thought of?"
- "Here's the hardest question: Are these gaps fixable? Could someone build an Are-Self that doesn't have these gaps? Or are some of them permanent?"
- "Last one: After six weeks of studying brains and AI, do you think the brain-AI comparison is helpful or misleading? Why?"

Let the conversation go where it goes. Don't force a conclusion. The point is the thinking, not the answer.

### Reflection (7 minutes)

#### The Time Capsule

Return the Week 1 "First Impressions" and the Week 1 "Quick Draw" brain sketches. Students read their old work.

Then they write a final reflection:

**"Six weeks ago, I thought ___. Now I think ___. The thing that changed my mind the most was ___. The question I still can't answer is ___."**

Collect these. They're the most valuable assessment in the course — not because they show what students know, but because they show how students think.

### Exit Ticket (Final)

"One thing from this course I'll remember a year from now: ___."

### Common Questions and Troubleshooting

**Q: Some students are nervous about presenting.**
A: Normalize it. "Everyone is nervous. The feeling you have right now? That's your hypothalamus triggering a stress response. Your amygdala thinks you're in danger. You're not. You're in a classroom with people who want to hear what you think." Give nervous students the option to present to you privately or to a small group instead of the whole class.

**Q: The arguments are shallow — just opinions without real evidence.**
A: Redirect during the drafting phase (Day 2). "Where in our class did we see this? What experiment showed this? What happened when we tested this?" If the evidence isn't there, help them find it or adjust their claim to something they can support.

**Q: Students all picked the same topic.**
A: That's okay. Different students will have different evidence and reasoning even with the same claim. If you want more variety, limit each topic to 3 students and use a sign-up sheet on Day 1.

**Q: How do I grade this?**
A: Focus on the process, not the conclusion. A student who makes a clear, well-supported argument that "AI can think" deserves the same grade as one who argues "AI cannot think." Grade the claim's clarity, the evidence's relevance, the reasoning's strength, and the presentation's communication quality.
