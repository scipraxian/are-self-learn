# Week 1: Meet Your Brain

What does a brain actually do? Students find out by meeting the real one and its software double in the same week. By Friday, they'll know the major brain regions, they'll have seen Are-Self in action, and they'll have their first opinions about whether software can think.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Orientation — what's inside your head and what's inside the machine

**Primary Variable:** Humility — saying "I don't know yet" is not weakness, it's where learning starts.

**Why This Week Matters:**
Everything in this course is built on a comparison: the brain in your head versus the brain someone built in software. Before students can evaluate that comparison, they need to know both sides. This week is about first contact with both.

**Learning Goals:**
- Name at least six major brain regions and describe what each one does
- Explain what Are-Self is in their own words
- Identify at least two things the brain and Are-Self have in common
- Identify at least one thing that seems different
- Practice saying "I'm not sure" without embarrassment

**What This Week Is Not:**
- Students are NOT expected to understand any brain region deeply — that comes in Weeks 2-5
- No experiments yet — this week is observation, mapping, and first impressions
- Students do NOT need to understand how the software works under the hood

## Day 1: What Does a Brain Do?

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Write on the board: **"Your brain is doing something right now. What is it doing? List everything you can think of in 90 seconds."**

Students write individually, then share. Collect answers on the board. You'll get the obvious (thinking, reading) and the less obvious (keeping my heart beating, balancing me in this chair, deciding whether this is boring). Keep the list visible all week.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Brain Speed Tour (15 minutes)

Project a labeled brain diagram. Walk through the major regions, spending about 90 seconds on each. Keep it conversational — this is a first look, not a textbook chapter.

For each region, give the name and one sentence about what it does:

- **Hippocampus** — Makes new memories and decides what's worth remembering
- **Frontal Lobe** — Where you think things through and make decisions
- **Prefrontal Cortex** — The planner in your brain — it thinks ahead and stops you from doing dumb things
- **Hypothalamus** — Tiny but powerful — keeps your temperature, hunger, and energy balanced
- **Temporal Lobe** — Helps you understand language and process sounds
- **Central Nervous System** — Your brain and spinal cord together — headquarters
- **Peripheral Nervous System** — Every nerve that isn't in headquarters — fingers, toes, skin, gut
- **Thalamus** — The mailroom — takes in sensory information and sends it to the right department
- **Synaptic Cleft** — The tiny gap between neurons where chemical messages jump across

After each one, ask: "Did anyone already know that? Did anything surprise you?"

Students sketch a rough brain map in their notebooks and label at least six regions.

#### Part 2: What's Missing from Our List? (10 minutes)

Go back to the brainstorm list from the warm-up. For each item, ask: "Which brain region handles that?" Some items will map cleanly. Others won't. That's the point.

Ask: "Is there anything on our list that NO single region handles? What does that tell us?"

Lead toward the idea that the brain works as a system — regions cooperate, not in isolation.

#### Part 3: Quick Draw (5 minutes)

Each student draws their favorite brain region from memory (no peeking) and writes one sentence about what it does. Collect these — you'll use them in Week 6 to show how much they've learned.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What's the one brain region you want to know more about? Why?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The brain region I found most interesting today is ___ because ___. One thing I'm not sure about: ___."

## Day 2: What Is AI, Anyway?

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Write on the board: **"What is artificial intelligence? Write your honest definition — there are no wrong answers today."**

Collect definitions. Don't correct anyone yet. Just gather the range — you'll get everything from "robots" to "Siri" to "computers that think." Leave them visible.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: AI Myths vs. Reality (10 minutes)

Read some of the student definitions aloud. For each one, ask the class: "Is this true? Sort of true? Not really?" Guide the conversation toward these ideas:

- AI is software that finds patterns in data. It is not a robot. It is not alive.
- AI does not "want" anything. It processes input and produces output.
- Some AI is very good at specific things (playing chess, generating text). No AI is good at everything.
- AI can seem like it understands you. Whether it actually does is one of the biggest open questions in science.

Don't lecture. Let the students argue. Your job is to keep the conversation honest and evidence-based.

#### Part 2: Meet Are-Self (15 minutes)

Project the Are-Self interface. Explain the basic idea: "A group of people built software that is organized like a brain. It has a hippocampus for memory, a frontal lobe for reasoning, and a hypothalamus for managing resources — just like yours."

Show the brain-software map from the course index. Let students look at it and react.

Then do a live interaction: type a question into Are-Self and let the class watch what happens. Narrate what you see: "It's thinking — the frontal lobe app is running. Now it's checking its memory — that's the hippocampus. Now it's choosing which AI model to use — that's the hypothalamus managing costs."

Ask: "What do you notice? What questions do you have?"

#### Part 3: Same or Different? (5 minutes)

On the board, create two columns: "Same" and "Different." Students contribute observations about what the brain and Are-Self have in common and what seems different. No wrong answers. Keep the list for future reference.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Did watching Are-Self change your definition of AI from the beginning of class? How?"**

### Exit Ticket

"Before today I thought AI was ___. Now I think AI is ___."

## Day 3: The Mapping Challenge

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Put the nine brain regions on the board in random order. Challenge: "Match as many brain regions to their Are-Self software counterparts as you can. You have two minutes. Work from memory."

See how the class does. Celebrate correct matches and talk through the tricky ones.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Build the Map (20 minutes)

In pairs, students create a visual map on large paper. Left side: the human brain with regions labeled and colored. Right side: the Are-Self architecture with the matching software apps. Between them: connecting lines with a one-sentence explanation of how they're related.

Circulate and ask questions:
- "Why do you think the software needs a hypothalamus? What would happen without it?"
- "The thalamus routes signals in your brain. What does the software thalamus route?"
- "Your peripheral nervous system goes all the way to your toes. What's the software equivalent of toes?"

#### Part 2: Gallery Walk (10 minutes)

Post the maps around the room. Each student gets three sticky notes. Walk around and leave notes on other pairs' maps:
- One thing you like about this map
- One question this map made you think of
- One thing you'd add

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Which connection between the brain and Are-Self makes the most sense to you? Which one feels like a stretch?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The strongest parallel between the brain and Are-Self is ___. The one that feels the weakest is ___."

## Day 4: Brain Region Bingo

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Quick review: "Someone give me one brain region and tell me what it does. Go." Rapid-fire around the room.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Brain Region Bingo (20 minutes)

Each student gets a 3x3 bingo card with brain regions in the squares. The teacher reads descriptions — not names, but descriptions of what the region does. Students figure out which region matches and mark it.

Round 1: Biology descriptions. "This region makes new memories and decides what to keep." (Hippocampus)

Round 2: Software descriptions. "This app manages how much money the AI spends on each task." (Hypothalamus)

Round 3: Mix of both. Students have to figure out whether the description is biology or software, then name the region.

Play multiple rounds. Let students who get bingo explain their answers to the class.

#### Part 2: Write Your Own (10 minutes)

Each student writes three bingo clues — one biology, one software, one that could be either. Collect these for future review games.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Which region was hardest to identify from a description? Why?"**

The Variable in play today is Inquiry — the game forces students to ask "what is this really describing?" over and over.

### Exit Ticket

"The region I can explain best to someone else is ___. The region I still mix up with another one is ___."

## Day 5: First Impressions

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Return the Quick Draws from Day 1. Ask: "Would you draw it differently now? What would you change?"

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Ask Are-Self Anything (15 minutes)

This is the fun part. Students generate questions they want to ask Are-Self. Vote on the top five. Then, as a class, ask each question and watch Are-Self respond.

After each response, discuss:
- "Which brain regions do you think activated in the software to produce that answer?"
- "How would YOUR brain have answered differently?"
- "What did the AI get right? What did it miss?"

#### Part 2: My First Impression (15 minutes)

Students write a short paragraph (5-7 sentences) answering this prompt:

**"After one week of learning about brains and AI, here is what I think: Are-Self is like a brain because ___. Are-Self is NOT like a brain because ___. The question I most want answered is ___."**

Collect these. They'll revisit them in Week 6 to see how their thinking changed.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What's one thing you're genuinely unsure about after this week? Remember — Humility means being comfortable not knowing yet."**

### Exit Ticket

"My biggest question going into next week: ___."

### Common Questions and Troubleshooting

**Q: Students are fixated on whether AI is "alive."**
A: Don't shut this down — it's a great question. Ask them: "What would you need to see to be convinced something is alive? Make a list." That list will get more sophisticated over six weeks.

**Q: Some students already know a lot about AI and are dominating the conversation.**
A: Give them a role. "You're the class AI consultant — when we have a technical question, you get first crack at it." Channel the expertise; don't suppress it.

**Q: I can't get Are-Self running.**
A: Week 1 only needs a projector demo on Days 2 and 5. If the software isn't ready, use screenshots or recordings. The course still works — you just lose the live interaction for now.
