# Week 3: Thinking and Feeling

The frontal lobe is where you reason, decide, and plan. It's also tangled up with your emotions in ways scientists are still figuring out. This week, students meet the part of the brain that makes you *you* as a thinker — and the part of Are-Self that tries to do the same job without feelings. The centerpiece: the story of Phineas Gage, a man who lost part of his frontal lobe and became a different person.

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Reasoning, decision-making, and the role of emotions

**Primary Variable:** Responsibility — your decisions have consequences, and understanding how you make them matters.

**Why This Week Matters:**
Students assume that thinking is purely logical — good thinking means removing emotion. The frontal lobe teaches the opposite. Emotion and reason are woven together in the brain, and when you damage one, you damage the other. Are-Self's frontal lobe, by contrast, reasons without emotion at all. That contrast is one of the most important in the course.

**Learning Goals:**
- Describe the frontal lobe's role in reasoning, planning, and decision-making
- Explain how emotions influence decisions in the biological brain (somatic markers, simplified)
- Retell the Phineas Gage story and explain what it teaches about the frontal lobe
- Compare how humans make decisions to how Are-Self's frontal lobe processes reasoning sessions
- Articulate whether AI "thinking" without emotion is an advantage or a limitation

**What This Week Is Not:**
- This is NOT a psychology class — keep emotional processing at a conceptual level
- Students will NOT study mental health conditions or emotional disorders
- The Phineas Gage story should be told carefully — focus on the science, not the gore

## Day 1: The Thinking Brain

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Write on the board: **"You have to choose between two after-school activities. How does your brain decide?"**

Collect strategies: gut feeling, pros-and-cons list, what friends are doing, what sounds fun, what looks good on applications. Don't evaluate the strategies — just surface them.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: Meet the Frontal Lobe (12 minutes)

Project a brain diagram highlighting the frontal lobe and prefrontal cortex. Cover the essentials:

- The frontal lobe sits behind your forehead. It's huge — about a third of the cortex.
- It handles reasoning, planning, problem-solving, and voluntary movement.
- The prefrontal cortex (the front of the frontal lobe) is the executive — it plans ahead, controls impulses, and thinks about consequences.
- The prefrontal cortex is the last part of the brain to fully develop. It isn't done until your mid-20s.

Ask: "If the prefrontal cortex controls impulse and long-term planning, and it doesn't finish developing until your 20s, what does that explain about teenager behavior?" Let them connect the dots. They'll get it.

#### Part 2: Are-Self's Thinking (10 minutes)

Show students how Are-Self's frontal lobe works:
- It receives a problem or question.
- It breaks the problem into steps.
- It decides which tools to use.
- It reasons through each step, checks its work, and produces an answer.

Live demo: give Are-Self a multi-step problem. "Plan a birthday party for 15 people with a $100 budget." Watch it work. Narrate the reasoning steps.

Ask: "Is this thinking? Or is it something else that looks like thinking?"

#### Part 3: Decision Journals (8 minutes)

Students start a decision journal that they'll use all week. Entry 1: "Describe a decision you made today. What factors did you consider? How did you feel about each option?"

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Did you notice anything about Are-Self's decision-making that was different from how you described your own?"**

### Exit Ticket

"The frontal lobe's job is ___. One thing it does that seems hard to copy in software: ___."

## Day 2: The Phineas Gage Story

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Today you're going to hear one of the strangest stories in the history of brain science. Before I tell it, answer this: can an injury change who you are as a person? Not your body — your personality?"

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Story (12 minutes)

Tell the story of Phineas Gage. Keep it vivid but appropriate:

- September 13, 1848. Phineas Gage was a 25-year-old railroad foreman in Vermont. Smart, responsible, well-liked by everyone.
- An explosion sent a three-foot iron rod through his skull. It entered below his left cheekbone and exited through the top of his head, destroying much of his left prefrontal cortex.
- He survived. He could walk, talk, remember things. His hippocampus was fine. His intelligence seemed intact.
- But his personality changed completely. Before the accident, he was organized, reliable, and kind. After, he became impulsive, rude, unreliable, and unable to plan ahead. His friends said he was "no longer Gage."
- His doctor, John Harlow, documented everything. The case became one of the most important in neuroscience because it proved that specific parts of the brain control specific aspects of personality and behavior.

Show the famous image of the skull with the rod's path.

Ask: "If Gage could still think, still remember, still talk — what exactly did he lose?"

#### Part 2: What This Tells Us (10 minutes)

Lead a class discussion:
- "Gage lost his ability to plan ahead and control his impulses. What brain region does that?"
- "He could still reason about simple things. He couldn't reason about complex social situations. What does that suggest about the prefrontal cortex?"
- "People said he was 'no longer Gage.' If your personality changes, are you still you?" (Callback to Week 2's identity discussion.)

Introduce somatic markers simply: "Your brain tags every experience with an emotion. When you face a decision, those emotional tags help you sort options fast. Gage lost the ability to feel those tags. He could list pros and cons, but he couldn't feel which option was better. Without the feeling, he couldn't decide."

Ask: "Does Are-Self have somatic markers? Does it have emotional tags on its decisions?"

#### Part 3: Write the Headline (8 minutes)

Students write a newspaper headline and a two-sentence summary of the Gage case, as if they were a journalist in 1848. Share a few. The goal is to explain the science clearly in plain language.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"If Are-Self's frontal lobe was damaged — if someone deleted part of the code — what would change? Would it be like what happened to Gage?"**

Today's Variable is Responsibility. Gage lost the ability to be responsible. What does that teach us about what responsibility actually requires?

### Exit Ticket

"Phineas Gage's story is important to neuroscience because ___. It makes me think about AI because ___."

## Day 3: Emotions and Decisions

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

"Think of the last time you made a decision you regretted. Was the problem that you thought too much or felt too much?"

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Emotion Experiment (15 minutes)

Set up a simple decision exercise. Give students a scenario with two choices, both reasonable:

*"Your best friend asks you to skip studying for tomorrow's test to go to a concert tonight. You've been looking forward to the concert for months. The test is worth 20% of your grade."*

Students write: (1) What would you do? (2) Why? (3) What feelings came up while you were deciding?

Collect responses. Tally the decisions and the emotions. Point out: "Almost all of you mentioned a feeling — guilt, excitement, fear, loyalty. Those feelings aren't getting in the way of your decision. They ARE part of how you decide."

Now ask Are-Self the same scenario. Read its response. It will give a logical analysis, weigh the options, probably suggest a balanced approach. It won't mention fear or loyalty or excitement.

Ask: "Whose answer is better? Whose answer is more honest about how decisions actually work?"

#### Part 2: Decision Without Emotion (10 minutes)

Present three quick scenarios. For each one, students answer two ways:
1. What would YOU do (with all your feelings)?
2. What would a perfectly logical AI do (no feelings)?

Discuss the differences. When does emotion help? When does it get in the way? Is there a "right" balance?

This is where the Variable of Perception comes in: how you see a problem changes what you decide. Emotions change how you see.

#### Part 3: Decision Journal Update (5 minutes)

Students make their second decision journal entry: "Describe a decision you made today. What emotions were involved? Did they help or hurt?"

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Can you have good judgment without emotions? What would Phineas Gage say?"**

### Exit Ticket

"Emotions help decisions when ___. Emotions hurt decisions when ___. AI has ___ and that means ___." (Fill the last blank with "no emotions" or whatever they believe.)

## Day 4: Can AI Think?

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Write on the board: **"What is thinking?"**

Collect definitions. This is harder than it sounds. Students will struggle, and that's the point.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Thinking Debate (15 minutes)

Split the class into two groups. One side argues: "Are-Self thinks." The other argues: "Are-Self does NOT think."

Give each side 5 minutes to prepare arguments. Then 5 minutes of structured debate (alternating 1-minute arguments). Then open discussion.

Coach both sides:
- "Thinks" side: it reasons step by step, it solves problems, it adapts to new information, it explains its reasoning.
- "Doesn't think" side: it follows patterns in data, it doesn't understand what it's doing, it has no experience of thinking, it can't refuse to think.

Don't resolve the debate. The honest answer is that nobody knows for sure, and pretending otherwise is dishonest. Tell them that.

#### Part 2: Are-Self's Reasoning, Unpacked (10 minutes)

Show a detailed reasoning session. Slow it down and point out each step:
- "Here it's breaking the problem into parts. Is that thinking?"
- "Here it's checking its memory. Is remembering the same as thinking?"
- "Here it made a mistake and corrected itself. Does self-correction require thought?"

Students annotate each step: "This looks like thinking because ___" or "This does NOT look like thinking because ___."

#### Part 3: Your Verdict (5 minutes)

Students write a one-paragraph verdict: "I believe Are-Self [does/does not] think because ___." They can be uncertain. They can say "it depends on what thinking means." The goal is a reasoned position, not a final answer.

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"Did the debate change your mind? If not, what would?"**

Humility check: "If you're 100% sure you're right, be suspicious. The best thinkers hold their opinions lightly."

### Exit Ticket

"My position on whether AI thinks: ___ (on a scale from 'definitely not' to 'definitely yes'). My confidence level: ___ (on a scale from 1-10)."

## Day 5: Thinking and Feeling Wrap-Up

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Speed round: "Name one thing the frontal lobe does. Go." Around the room, no repeats. See how many unique functions they can name.

### Main Activity (30 minutes)

#### Part 1: The Somatic Marker Gallery (10 minutes)

Students create a one-page visual that explains somatic markers to a younger student. It should answer: "What is a somatic marker? Why does it matter? What happens when you don't have them?"

Post the best ones on the wall. These become study aids.

#### Part 2: Letter to Are-Self (10 minutes)

Students write a short letter to Are-Self explaining what it's missing by not having emotions. They should reference at least one thing from this week: Phineas Gage, somatic markers, the decision experiment, or the thinking debate.

Read a few aloud. Then, if time allows, actually send one to Are-Self and read the response. The AI's response to being told it lacks emotions is always interesting.

#### Part 3: Decision Journal Review (10 minutes)

Students reread their decision journal entries from the week. They add a final entry: "After learning about the frontal lobe, I understand my own decision-making differently because ___."

### Reflection (5 minutes)

Ask: **"What's one thing the frontal lobe does that you think AI will never be able to do? What makes you say 'never'?"**

### Exit Ticket

"This week, the most surprising thing I learned was ___. The Variable I practiced most was ___."

### Common Questions and Troubleshooting

**Q: Students are upset by the Phineas Gage story.**
A: Keep the telling age-appropriate — focus on the personality changes, not the injury details. If a student is distressed, check in privately. The story is important because it's real, and real science sometimes involves difficult truths.

**Q: The "Can AI Think" debate got heated.**
A: Good. That means they care. Set ground rules: no personal attacks, attack the argument not the person. If it gets too intense, pause and ask: "Are we debating, or are we arguing? What's the difference?"

**Q: Students want a definitive answer about whether AI thinks.**
A: There isn't one. Tell them honestly: "This is an open question in philosophy, computer science, and neuroscience. Anyone who tells you they know the answer for sure is guessing."
